The clinical presentation varies from a chronic, productive, daily cough and recurrent infections to haemoptysis, dyspnoea, and respiratory failure<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d32679e163>Bronchiectasis has diverse aetiologies, including idiopathic (up to 50% of cases), post-respiratory tract infection, rare immunodeficiency disorders, genetic abnormalities, autoimmune conditions, chronic inflammation, and mechanical obstruction<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. It is also increasingly being recognised as part of a disease entity coinciding with and complicating other pulmonary conditions such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).</p><p class="" id=d32679e173>Prevalence rates fluctuate globally, between age groups, and between the sexes and are still largely unknown. However, a recent publication suggests that there is increasing overall incidence and prevalence<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. In the UK, overall incidence increased from 21 to 35 in women and 18 to 26 in men per 100,000 patient years between 2004 and 2013. The prevalence rates in women increased from 350 to 566 per 100,000 patient years and in men increased from 301 to 485 per 100,000 patient years in the same time<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. This may be, in part, due to increased diagnosis of the condition and improved computed tomography techniques. The study also illustrated the increased mortality rates in this population group and the need for hospitalisation, further strengthening the case for the increasing impact it has on our healthcare systems<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d32679e188>The mainstay of management is improving symptoms and reducing exacerbations. This article will cover idiopathic and post-infective bronchiectasis in adults and will not include allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA), cystic fibrosis (CF), immunodeficiency, and non-tuberculous mycobacterium (NTM) disease. It will focus on the therapeutic strategies in bronchiectasis management, particularly highlighting new evidence from between 2013 and 2016.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d32679e194>The patient-led approach</h2><p class="" id=d32679e197>The first approach to management is to treat the underlying cause if one is identifiable<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a>,<a href="#ref-5">5</a>,<a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. British Thoracic Society (BTS) guidelines have a systematic method for screening patients for ABPA, immunodeficiency, CF, and environmental tuberculosis (TB) infection<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d32679e214>The importance of risk stratification has been highlighted in recent years<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a>,<a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>. Two main scoring systems have been developed to illustrate severity and mortality – the bronchiectasis severity index (BSI) and FACED<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a>,<a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. Both include FEV<sub>1</sub> (forced expiratory volume in 1 second), dyspnoea, <i>Pseudomonas</i> colonisation, and radiological features. They have their limitations, mainly sample size and disparity between mild and moderate phenotypes, but are deemed suitable to predict mortality<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a>,<a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. Additionally, the BSI also includes predictors for hospitalisation and annual mortality risk<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>. These may be useful as adjuncts to decide on clinical management, but further studies are needed to see if such screening systems can be utilised in this way.</p><p class="" id=d32679e249>The vicious cycle hypothesis was first introduced by Cole and moulds our management interjections<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>. Failure of mucus clearance, persistent infection, and inflammation leading to structural damage are all key aspects. Breaking this cycle by optimising interventions via airway clearance and antibiotic therapy for acute infections has been a backbone of treatment<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>. Broadly categorising therapies into non-pharmacological, pharmacological, and standard and long-term agents, we will explore these aspects and highlight new evidence from the last 3 years.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d32679e263>Standard therapeutic strategies</h2><div class=section><a name=d32679e266 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Vaccination</h3><p class="" id=d32679e271>There is an established role for the influenza and pneumococcal vaccinations in the management of chronic respiratory and medical conditions. This is based on limited and low-quality evidence<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>. There have been no recent studies specific to bronchiectasis.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d32679e279 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Airway clearance techniques and pulmonary rehabilitation</h3><p class="" id=d32679e284>Chest physiotherapy forms a core of patient-led management in bronchiectasis irrespective of severity and symptoms. These techniques are patient-centred and variable and aim to aid the removal of secretions from the lung through non-pharmacological methods<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. Whether these techniques have a clear impact on quality of life or reducing exacerbations has not been adequately proven, and most studies are limited to the CF patient population<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. Additionally, the use of multidisciplinary exercise interventions (pulmonary rehabilitation [PR]) is seen as an integral part of multimodal management for several chronic diseases<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. However, much of the data are based on CF or other chronic respiratory conditions. Further randomised controlled trials (RCTs) are required for PR specific to NCFB. <a href="#T1">Table 1</a> summarises the most recent studies and their outcomes for both RCTs.</p><p class="" id=d32679e302>The recent Cochrane review in 2015 included a total of seven studies in children and adults and a total of 105 patients. Overall, they showed within this limited field that airway clearance techniques (ACTs) are safe in adults and improve certain quality of life measures, lung function, and symptoms<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>. Further studies are required to assess the short- and long-term impact on exacerbations and disease progression.</p><div class="table-wrap panel clearfix"><a name=T1 class=n-a></a><div class=caption><h3>Table 1. rehabilitation.</p></div></div></div></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d32679e523>Short-term therapy</h2><div class=section><a name=d32679e526 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Antimicrobial therapy</h3><p class="" id=d32679e531>Fourteen days of antibiotic therapy is recommended for an acute exacerbation of bronchiectasis. The BTS guidelines identify an exacerbation as deterioration in local symptoms (cough, increased sputum volume or change of viscosity, increased sputum purulence with or without increasing wheeze, dyspnoea, and haemoptysis) and or systemic upset<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d32679e538>Intravenous (IV) antibiotics are required when there has been a failure of oral therapy or there is need for hospital admission or <i>in vitro</i> resistant pathogens that necessitate IV treatment. There were no studies addressing this. More evidence-based practice for choice and duration of antimicrobial therapy is required. The optimal management of exacerbations remains a vast area of untapped research for robust RCTs and future focus.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d32679e545 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Eradication therapy</h3><p class="" id=d32679e550>Patients with known <i>Pseudomonas aeruginosa</i> (PA) have a reported severe phenotype with increased rates of exacerbation and an independent 3-fold mortality risk<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. This may be a result of the pathogenicity of the organism and its ability to form biofilms, rendering standard antimicrobial therapies less effective. Therefore, attempted eradication for first isolation is considered reasonable<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. Further studies are needed to assess 1) if eradication is needed and 2) the optimal management regime.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d32679e568>Long-term therapies</h2><div class=section><a name=d32679e571 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Muco-active therapies</h3><p class="" id=d32679e576>Muco-active therapies can be used for both exacerbations and chronic management. Available as oral, inhaled, or nebulised agents, they reduce sputum viscosity and aid expectoration, thereby theoretically shortening exacerbation length or frequency and improving symptoms<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d32679e583>Although CF and previous studies have shown the potential benefit of hypertonic over 0.9% saline, more recently the evidence indicates equal efficacy in NCFB<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. Further studies are required to show whether saline (0.9% or hypertonic) is recommended in practice. Bilton <i>et al</i>. studied the effects of inhaled mannitol in a 12-month double-blinded RCT<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. There was no statistically significant reduction in exacerbation rates; however, there was an improvement in time to first exacerbation and quality of life indicators<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. The results are summarised in <a href="#T2">Table 2</a>. Oral agents such as carbocisteine are commonly prescribed in the UK as part of bronchiectasis therapy; however, there are no RCTs to date. Dornase alfa therapy shows an increase in exacerbation rate in NCFB and is not recommended<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d32679e610 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Anti-inflammatory agents</h3><p class="" id=d32679e615>This broad heading covers many drugs, including corticosteroids, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), leukotriene receptor antagonists, and others. All have different mechanisms of action and vary as either long- or short-term therapy.</p><p class="" id=d32679e618> <b> <i>NSAIDs.</i> </b> There is some evidence to suggest the use of ibuprofen or other NSAIDs in patients with mild CF<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>. There are no studies that support their routine use in non-CF bronchiectasis.</p><p class="" id=d32679e631> <b> <i>Leukotriene receptor antagonists.</i> </b> There are no studies to date for this subset of therapy specific to bronchiectasis.</p><p class="" id=d32679e640> <b> <i>Inhaled corticosteroids +/- long acting beta 2 agonists.</i> </b> The Cochrane review of the long-acting beta<sub>2</sub> agonists (LABA) and inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) combination demonstrated a lack of quality evidence<sup><a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>. Only one RCT (2012) was highlighted in the 2014 review evaluating a bronchiectasis adult population without asthma who received inhaled budesonide and formoterol (640µg and 18µg) or high-dose budesonide (1,600µg). The authors found that there was an improvement in dyspnoea symptoms between the combination group and the ICS group<sup><a href="#ref-23">23</a></sup>. Study size was small and it lacked statistically significant differences in outcomes<sup><a href="#ref-23">23</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d32679e665>Predominant complications with long-term inhaled therapy are possible pneumonia risk, adrenal suppression, thin skin, and haemoptysis. This risk-benefit profile needs further investigation before management is accepted as routine in the bronchiectasis patient.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d32679e669 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Bronchodilator therapy</h3><p class="" id=d32679e674> <b> <i>Beta 2 adrenoreceptor agonists (short- and long-acting beta agonists).</i> </b> These agents have been illustrated in clinical trials for asthma and COPD; however, there have been none to date in bronchiectasis. Furthermore, there are no RCTs evaluating the use of inhaled anticholinergics. The role of bronchodilator therapy in bronchiectasis is unproven but often used in clinical practice with the breathless patient. If there is subjective improvement, it is sensible to continue such treatment.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d32679e684 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Other long-term therapies</h3><p class="" id=d32679e689>Mandal <i>et al.</i> reported the use of atorvastatin versus placebo with the primary endpoint of reducing cough<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>. A total of 16 patients were randomised to placebo and 22 to treatment for 28 days<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. There was no statistical significance in sputum neutrophil count or weight. Secondary endpoint of quality of life questionnaire showed a clinical difference suggestive of benefit, but the results did not meet significance<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. Further studies on similar novel agents are encouraged in order to broaden our scope for long-term anti-inflammatory treatments.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d32679e951 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Macrolides</h3><p class="" id=d32679e956>The use of chronic macrolide therapy has been described for many respiratory conditions. The mechanism of action has been the point of research topics and includes anti-inflammatory, immunomodulatory, and antimicrobial actions. Their chronic use has been noted within the diffuse panbronchiolitis and CF populations<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>. This has spurred clinical trials in the bronchiectasis group.</p><p class="" id=d32679e963>Three major studies in 2012 and 2013 from Australia, New Zealand, and the Netherlands compared azithromycin daily, azithromycin three times weekly, and low-dose erythromycin and have led the way in establishing macrolide use in long-term bronchiectasis therapy<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a>–<a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. Despite the publication date, all three studies (BLESS, BAT, and EMBRACE) have been included in this report because of their significance in current bronchiectasis management. The three trials reported their primary outcome as exacerbation frequency, and they have illustrated significant reduction in exacerbation rates<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a>–<a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. However, we must balance this with the possible emergence of antibiotic resistance and possible cardiovascular, audiologic, and gastrointestinal adverse events (AEs). A practical but prudent issue is the necessity to screen these patients for NTM infection prior to embarking upon long-term macrolide therapy, as this would have potential consequence on NTM management<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d32679e1884>The prospect of non-antimicrobial therapies and novel anti-inflammatories exposes an exciting area for future phase II and III clinical trials.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d32679e1888 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Inhaled or aerosolised antibiotic or other therapies</h3><p class="" id=d32679e1893>With the burden of antimicrobial resistance increasingly real and a global political agenda for stewardship, the need for enhancing techniques and the delivery of antimicrobials responsibly with reduced adverse effects is paramount. Aerosolised and inhaled delivery within the lung has been of interest, as high concentrations of drug can be administered within the airways with reduced systemic side effects. The basis for inhaled antibiotics in bronchiectasis has not yet been established in clinical trials; however, recent work is exposing an exciting field. Currently, there are no approved inhaled antibiotics licensed for use by UK, European, or USA drug agencies for bronchiectasis.</p><p class="" id=d32679e1896>Barker <i>et al.</i> studied the effects of inhaled aztreonam in bronchiectasis patients in two double-blinded, randomised, placebo-controlled trials<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup> (<a href="#T3">Table 3</a>). Their primary endpoint of quality of life did not reach statistical significance, and they also reported more treatment-related AEs<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d32679e1913> <a href="#T3">Table 3</a> illustrates several RCTs analysing the safety and effect of inhaled or nebulised agents in long-term therapy. Haworth <i>et al.</i> studied the effectiveness of inhaled colistin for up to 6 months within 21 days of an exacerbation<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>. Although they did show a difference in the median time to first exacerbation, it did not reach statistical significance. However, improvements in secondary endpoints of bacterial density and quality of life did. This study also illustrated the importance of adherence, and, when taking more than 80% of therapy, it did improve the time to first exacerbation significantly<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d32679e1930>There are two phase III trials of inhaled ciprofloxacin, RESPIRE-1 and -2, that have now been completed with the results awaited. They have two regimens comparing 28 days on and off for 1 year versus 14 days on and off for 1 year. A competitor study has utilised liposomes to allow slow release of ciprofloxacin and molecular stability in nebulisation and to improve delivery to macrophages<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>. Successful phase II studies of liposomal ciprofloxacin (pulmaquin) have shown a significant reduction in PA colony-forming units (CFUs) in sputum compared with placebo<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. Subsequent to this, two identical international studies have completed enrolment to a phase III study (ORBIT-3 and -4)<sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a>,<a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>. Overall, the initial results are encouraging, prove tolerability, and promise a potential inhaled therapy for bronchiectasis (<a href="#T3">Table 3</a>).
