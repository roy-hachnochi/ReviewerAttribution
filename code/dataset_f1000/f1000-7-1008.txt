Over the last five decades, heart transplantation is considered a standard therapy for select patients with end-stage heart failure. In the present era, one-year survival after heart transplantation is almost 90% and has a conditional half-life of 13 years<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>, which is superior to that of end-stage heart failure.</p><p class="" id=d93078e148>The number of patients with heart failure requiring advanced therapies is growing, while the number of donor organs remains a constant and limiting factor<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. Heart transplant (HTx) candidates of the current era are also more complex. Increasing numbers are aged 65 years or more<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>, have mechanical circulatory support<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>, and have higher levels of antibodies to human leukocyte antigens (HLA), i.e. “sensitization”<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. Due to all these issues, these HTx candidates of the modern era are at increased risk for poor outcomes, including primary graft dysfunction and antibody-mediated rejection<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a>,<a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. The latest developments might be able to counter existing problems: 1) attempts to expand the pool of potential organ donors; 2) changes in the heart transplant donor allocation policy to allow for more equitable organ distribution; and 3) management of sensitized HTx candidates. Such advances could produce an increase in and a fairer allocation of donor organs and improved quality of life and survival for HTx recipients.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d93078e180>Expanding the donor pool</h2><div class=section><a name=d93078e183 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Extended criteria donors</h3><p class="" id=d93078e188>Currently, fewer than 50% of potential donors in the United States become actual organ donors<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. Initiatives have been taken to increase the utilization rate<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. Using less-than pristine donor hearts, so-called extended criteria donors, is one option to expand the donor pool. These hearts may be used for higher-risk recipients, such as those who are older, over age 65, with diabetes, renal dysfunction, or peripheral vascular disease. Considerable evidence shows that extended criteria donor hearts that may result in favorable post-HTx survival continue to be underutilized. In a retrospective review in California from 2001 to 2008 looking at 1872 possible organ donors found predictors of non-use to be age &gt;50 years, female gender, fatal cerebrovascular accident, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, an elevated troponin, left ventricular dysfunction (ejection fraction &lt;50%), left ventricular hypertrophy, and regional wall motion abnormalities. However, when such so-called extended criteria donor hearts are used for transplantation, outcomes are generally comparable<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a>–<a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d93078e207 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Limiting cold ischemic time</h3><p class="" id=d93078e212>The donor pool could be expanded by limiting cold ischemic time. An <i>ex vivo</i> perfusion platform would allow the donor heart to be kept in a warm, beating state while being transported until the time of implantation. Small registry studies have demonstrated its safety<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>. In a randomized trial examining the safety of an <i>ex vivo</i> platform, donor hearts managed with either the Organ Care System or standard cold storage were randomized to 130 patients. There was no difference in 30-day patient survival rates, 30-day graft survival rates, or serious adverse events<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d93078e229>The <i>ex vivo</i> perfusion platform may be particularly beneficial when used in conjunction with extended criteria donor hearts. Such donors, including those of older age, with left ventricular hypertrophy or moderately reduced ejection fraction, are more susceptible to injury with long ischemic time. Further study is needed to demonstrate the specific benefit of an <i>ex-vivo</i> perfusion platform in this population.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d93078e242>Heart transplant allocation policy</h2><div class=section><a name=d93078e245 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>The impetus for a new approach</h3><p class="" id=d93078e250>The allocation of donor hearts in the United States appears to be fair, as those patients who are sickest and who have been waiting the longest are the first to be considered in the event of a donor heart becoming available (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>). The gene expression profile test (AlloMap®, CareDx Inc, San Francisco, CA), an 11-gene expression signature from peripheral blood mononuclear cells, has a high negative predictive value for cellular rejection<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup> and is noninvasive. In randomized trials, gene expression profile was non-inferior to an endomyocardial biopsy in the diagnosis of cellular rejection<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup> and also useful early post-transplant<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. One role of the gene expression profile is to screen patients at low risk for cellular rejection at pre-determined intervals with biopsies performed only in cases in which the gene expression profile score is abnormal. However, patients with risk factors for antibody-mediated rejection cannot be tested with gene expression profile screening as the technique can only be used to assess cellular rejection.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d93078e767 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Cell-free DNA</h3><p class="" id=d93078e772>One more novel technique that can be used for noninvasive diagnosis of rejection uses cell-free DNA. Cell-free donor-derived DNA can be detected in the blood and urine of organ transplant recipients<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a>,<a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup> This cell-free DNA may be a potential marker for noninvasive diagnosis of graft injury, as cellular and antibody-mediated rejection events are associated with increased levels of cell-free donor-derived DNA<sup><a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d93078e787 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Biopsy-derived endomyocardial gene expression</h3><p class="" id=d93078e792>As mentioned above, the Allomap relies on peripheral blood gene expression profiling to refine diagnostic accuracy and does not detect antibody-mediated rejection (AMR). A recent study, however, studied mRNA extracted from endomyocardial biopsy samples and hybridized to a microarray system. Differences in AMR-selective gene expression classified AMR were linked with disease activity and ISHLT AMR grade<sup><a href="#ref-23">23</a></sup>. This advance has the potential to refine diagnostic accuracy and may in the future provide insight into the management of AMR<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d93078e807>Sensitization</h2><div class=section><a name=d93078e810 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Identification and quantification of anti-HLA antibodies</h3><p class="" id=d93078e815>The detection and measurement of anti-HLA antibodies is accomplished using solid phase assays (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>). Quantification is clinically relevant, as antibodies of greater intensity <i>in vitro</i> are potentially more cytotoxic <i>in vivo</i>. The presence of high-level anti-HLA antibodies (usually median fluorescent intensity [MFI] above 3,000–5,000) are considered potentially cytotoxic<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>.</p><a name=f2 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/16034/5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure2.gif"><img alt="5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure2.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/16034/5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure2.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 2. The detection of anti-HLA antibodies utilizing solid-phase assays.</h3><p id=d93078e841>Antibodies bind fluorescent-tagged antigens. A flow cytometer identifies anti-HLA antibodies and provides information on antibody strength and potential cytotoxicity.</p></div></div><p class="" id=d93078e848>However, not all high-intensity antibodies are detrimental to graft function and the fact that donor-specific antibodies have the ability to fix complement might be a superior way of signifying cytotoxicity<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a>,<a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>. The classical complement pathway is activated first by C1q binding to antibodies, as C1q is the first component of the pathway. After C1q activation, the complement cascade results in formation of the membrane attack complex and ultimately leads to cell lysis and death. In fact, antibodies with the ability to bind C1q are more likely to be cytotoxic<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a>,<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. The C1q assay is not currently available in all centers, so considering only antibodies that are strong binding by MFI after a 1:8 or 1:16 dilution may offer comparable information<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d93078e869><a href="#f3">Figure 3</a> illustrates one approach to the identification and quantification of antibodies against HLA and how management decisions are made in sensitized heart transplant candidates.</p><a name=f3 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/16034/5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure3.gif"><img alt="5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure3.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/16034/5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure3.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 3. Management of the sensitized patient.</h3><p id=d93078e885>Sensitized patients are those with a positive panel reactive antibody (PRA) screen; we consider a PRA &gt; 10% to be positive. The next step is to determine the identity and intensity of the anti-HLA antibodies. The results are used to determine the calculated PRA and the need for a virtual crossmatch. If the calculated PRA is above 50–70%, desensitization therapy may be used. This figure was reprinted with permission from <a href="#ref-28">28</a>.</p></div></div></div><div class=section><a name=d93078e896 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Approach to the crossmatch</h3><p class="" id=d93078e901>Prior to transplantation, the detection of anti-HLA antibodies is important to avoid hyperacute rejection: one would avoid donors with HLA correlating with high-level anti-HLA antibodies in the prospective recipient. One way to prevent hyperacute rejection is a prospective crossmatch, in which the potential recipient’s serum and donor cells are mixed to evaluate for complement-dependent cytotoxicity. However, this restricts the donor pool based on the location of the candidate’s serum, and so decreasing the number of possible donors.</p><p class="" id=d93078e904>Thus, the virtual crossmatch has essentially replaced the prospective crossmatch. HLA correlating with the candidate’s high-level anti-HLA antibodies are recorded as “avoids” in the United Network of Organ Sharing (UNOS) database. In this manner, potential donors with such HLA are not considered. This method has proven feasible in HTx<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d93078e912 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>The calculated PRA</h3><p class="" id=d93078e917>The identity and the intensity score (given by Luminex single-antigen bead assay) of anti-HLA antibodies is also valuable information for deciding which potential heart transplant recipients require desensitization, as expressed as the calculated PRA (cPRA)<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a>,<a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. The cPRA is the frequency of HLA defined as unacceptable in the donor population. For example, a HTx candidate with multiple anti-HLA antibodies might have a cPRA of 90%. This would mean that of all potential donors only 10% would be a match. cPRA highlights the fact that some high-level anti-HLA antibodies, the more common ones, will impact the ability to identify a suitable donor heart more than less common anti-HLA antibodies<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. If the cPRA is above 50–70%, therapies to reduce antibody levels may be administered.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d93078e932 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Approach to desensitization</h3><p class="" id=d93078e937>Management of the sensitized patient includes protocols that target antibodies by inactivation (intravenous immune globulin [IV Ig]<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>), removal (plasmapheresis), and reduced production (rituximab<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>) and bortezomib<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>). At our center, the desensitization process usually starts with a modified protocol built around one established for desensitization of kidney transplant recipients (<a href="#f4">Figure 4</a>)<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>.</p><a name=f4 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/16034/5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure4.gif"><img alt="5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure4.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/16034/5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure4.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 4. Desensitization protocol.</h3><p id=d93078e969>The treatment of circulating antibodies depends on the cPRA. Treatment is considered for those patients with cPRA &gt;50–70%. This figure was modified from <a href="#ref-32">32</a>.</p></div></div><p class="" id=d93078e979>If the combination of intravenous immune globulin and rituximab is ineffective in decreasing the cPRA below 50%, or if swift desensitization is required (i.e. a patient is listed as status 1A), then one may use bortezomib, a proteasome inhibitor targeting plasma cells<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>. To increase effectiveness, bortezomib can be combined with plasmapheresis (<a href="#f5">Figure 5</a>).</p><a name=f5 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/16034/5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure5.gif"><img alt="5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure5.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/16034/5f43aef8-356f-4707-8396-81d9fd9562fd_figure5.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 5. Desensitization of Status 1A patients or those with refractory antibodies.</h3><p id=d93078e999>Bortezomib is used for Status 1A patients or those with antibodies that do not respond to IV Ig and rituximab. This regimen will lower antibodies more effectively. This figure was reprinted with permission from Kittleson MM, Kobashigawa JA. Management of the Highly Sensitized Patient Awaiting Heart Transplant. January 8, 2015. Available at: <a target=xrefwindow href="http://www.acc.org/latest-in-cardiology/articles/2014/12/22/17/07/management-of-the-highly-sensitized-patient-awaiting-heart-transplant-expert-analysis" id=d93078e1001>http://www.acc.org/latest-in-cardiology/articles/2014/12/22/17/07/management-of-the-highly-sensitized-patient-awaiting-heart-transplant-expert-analysis</a> (accessed April 9, 2018).</p></div></div><p class="" id=d93078e1010><b><i>Eculizumab.</i></b> Pre-transplant interventions with IV Ig, rituximab, and bortezomib can reduce antibody levels so that it is possible to find an acceptable donor. However, for some patients such measures are not effective. Hyperacute rejection can still occur at the time of transplantation due to cytotoxic anti-HLA antibodies. This can happen if donor-specific antibodies were mistakenly classified as not cytotoxic by virtual crossmatch or if such antibodies were not present at the time banked blood was stored for a prospective crossmatch. Plasmapheresis may be used in the operating room at the time of transplantation in this setting. We have also found that eculizumab offers further insurance and protection against hyperacute rejection.</p><p class="" id=d93078e1016>The monoclonal antibody eculizumab blocks the last component of the complement cascade. This cascade is triggered by antigen-antibody complexes and results in formation of the membrane attack complex and ultimately cell death. Eculizumab specifically binds to the terminal complement component 5 (C5), and thus eventually blocks the production of the terminal complement complex C5b-9. Eculizumab is FDA-approved for use in two complement-mediated conditions, paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobunuria and atypical hemolytic-uremic syndrome. However, it also has benefit in sensitized kidney transplant recipients<sup><a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d93078e1028>Conclusion</h2><p class="" id=d93078e1031>The boundaries of HTx continue to expand to higher-risk candidates—older, on mechanical circulatory support, and with HLA antibody sensitization—and our goal remains to maintain favorable outcomes utilizing this scarce resource. The advances outlined here, from efforts to expand the donor pool, revision of the HTx allocation policy, newer ways to diagnose rejection, to developments in the detection and care of sensitized HTx candidates, will bring about improvements in survival and quality of life of end-stage patients who have HTx.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title
