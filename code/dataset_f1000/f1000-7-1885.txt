Trigger factors also lead to the release of various mediators such as keratinocytes (for example, cathelicidin, vascular endothelial growth factor, and endothelin-1), endothelial cells (nitric oxide), mast cells (cathelicidin and matrix metalloproteinases), macrophages (interferon-gamma, tumor necrosis factor, matrix metalloproteinases, and interleukin-26), and T helper type 1 (T<sub>H</sub>1) and T<sub>H</sub>17 cells. Additionally, trigger factors can directly communicate to the cutaneous nervous system and, by neurovascular and neuro-immune active neuropeptides, lead to the manifestation of rosacea lesions. Here, we aim to summarize the recent advances that preceded the new rosacea classification and address a symptom-based approach in the management of patients with rosacea. </div> <div The classification was the first to systemize rosacea diagnosis in the daily medical routine and gave resilient criteria at hand to efficiently evaluate therapeutic success in each subtype. The implementation of this new tool in the medical daily routine prompted a marked improvement in the medical care of rosacea patients worldwide. In general, the classification suggests a progression from one subtype to another over time and thereby supports a tendency to disregard deviating rosacea manifestations and overlaps between the subtypes. The progress in scientific insight that was accumulated since the introduction of the original rosacea classification in combination with our detailed clinical experience favored a modernized view of rosaceas pathophysiology as a product of multivariate disease processes that underlie the patient-specific clinical rosacea presentations<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. An updated rosacea classification was published in 2016, emphasizing a more patient-centric phenotype approach<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. This review aims to summarize the recent developments in our understanding of rosaceas pathophysiology that preceded the release of the modified rosacea classification and to illustrate the therapeutic management of individual rosacea patients on the basis of their symptoms.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e223>Epidemiology and diagnosis of rosacea</h2><p class="" id=d25769e226>In the US alone, more than 16 million patients are affected by rosacea, and worldwide incidences peak as high as 18%, particularly in populations with a predominant Celtic heritage, such as is observed in Ireland<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. Worldwide, the prevalence is estimated to reach over 5%. Females and males are affected equally<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. However, the prevalence of rosacea in many countries, including large countries like China and Australia, is still poorly explored, and the prevalence, especially of erythematous rosacea, demands careful differentiation from that of other erythematous diseases and origins of flushing, such as neuro-endocrine tumors.</p><p class="" id=d25769e237>Rosacea typically arises symmetrically in the central face with gender- and age-specific preferences with regard to lesion qualities. For instance, rhinophyma nearly exclusively presents in the male gender, flushing and erythema often are the first disease signs in younger ages, and telangiectasias make up first rosacea lesions in older ages. The overall rosacea manifestations are flushing, transient or persistent erythema, telangiectasia, papules, pustules, phymata, and (micro)edema (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>)<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a>,<a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>. Additionally, patients often report stinging or burning pain and very rarely pruritic sensations. Despite the typical centro-facial localization of rosacea, a causative association with the unique centro-facial skin composition that is characterized by a dense presence of sebaceous glands, dense nerval and vascular networks, and <i>Demodex</i> mites cannot be drawn conclusively as of yet. However, <i>Demodex</i> infestation is increased in some patients with rosacea, and eradication seems to alleviate rosacea symptoms probably by preventing the formation of pro-inflammatory cytokines<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a>,<a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>.</p><a name=T1 class=n-a></a><div class="table-wrap panel clearfix"><div class=caption><h3>Table 1. Briefly, phymatous changes and persistent centro-facial erythema are considered the only diagnostic features (signs) of rosacea, whereas flushing, telangiectasia, and inflammatory papules/pustules are considered major symptoms and only in combination can suggest the diagnosis of rosacea. Stinging or burning pain, edema, and dry sensation are defined as secondary features of rosacea (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>).</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e549>Novelties in the pathophysiology of rosacea</h2><p class="" id=d25769e552>Rosacea skin is characterized by dysregulated inflammatory (perivascular or pilosebaceous infiltrate), vascular (dilation), lymphatic (dilation), glandular (hyperplasia), and fibrotic processes, a composition that reflects the multivariate process of the skin disease. Simultaneously, this heterogeneous histological picture hints at rosaceas unclear pathophysiologic event of onset. Does rosacea originate from an initial dysregulation in inflammatory processes? Is rosacea a disease of the vasculature or rather of the lymphatic system? Does it result from glandular tissue? Or does rosacea represent a skin disease that ultimately arises from combined dysfunctional systems that could involve the gut?</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e558>Immunity</h2><p class="" id=d25769e561>The adaptive immune system along with the innate immune system might take a central part in rosaceas pathophysiology. Both the early stage perivascular and later-stage pilosebaceous infiltrates are strongly composed of T helper type 1 (T<sub>H</sub>1) and T<sub>H</sub>17 cells and show marked expression of innate immune cells such as additional macrophages and mast cells in papules and erythema and additional neutrophils in pustules and plasma cells in phymata<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. CD4<sup>+</sup> T<sub>H</sub> cells dominate the immune cell infiltrate, but overall rosacea, like its differential diagnosis acne vulgaris, displays a T<sub>H</sub>1/T<sub>H</sub>17 polarization pattern<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. These immuno-histochemical findings have been confirmed by transcriptome analysis, where markedly elevated expressions of T<sub>H</sub>1 signature genesinterferon-gamma and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-)and upregulated expression of T<sub>H</sub>17-associated genes coding for IL17A, IL22, IL6, IL20, and CCL20 were found. Neither T<sub>H</sub>2-associated genes nor signature markers for regulatory T cells (FOXP3, IL10, CCR4, and CCR8) were found to be altered in the expression in rosacea when compared with healthy skin<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. In acne vulgaris, <i>Propionibacterium acnes</i> capably drives the polarization of the immune cell infiltrate to a T<sub>H</sub>1/T<sub>H</sub>17 type<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a>,<a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>. This mechanism too could be of importance in rosacea, since some studies could associate patient colonization with <i>Demodex</i> spp., <i>Bacillus oleronius</i>, <i>Staphylococcus epidermidis</i>, <i>Helicobacter pylori</i>, and <i>Bartonella quintana</i> with the development of rosacea<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a>,<a href="#ref-23">23</a><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. Rosaceas association with facial <i>Demodex</i> spp. colonization was described quite some time ago, but its consequence for rosacea pathophysiology is still not understood and is even disputed by some. These concerns were substantiated by multiple clinical trials that achieved a reduction or eradication of <i>Demodex</i> colonization but often did not observe a marked amelioration of the clinical presentation in patients<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>. However, in case microbes initiate the shift of the infiltrate observed in rosacea toward a stable T<sub>H</sub>1/T<sub>H</sub>17 polarization, eradication of an early rosacea signal might have a smaller therapeutic effect than anticipated in later disease stages. The causative role of especially <i>Demodex</i> spp. and <i>Demodex</i>-dependent and -independent microbes for the T<sub>H</sub>1/T<sub>H</sub>17 polarization observed in rosacea will need to be investigated, and the importance of the T-cell pattern for the initiation and perpetuation of rosacea is in need of detailed clarification.</p><p class="" id=d25769e680>Another pathogen that has been suggested to be involved in the pathophysiology of rosacea is <i>H. pylori</i><sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a>,<a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. However, a recent meta-analysis found only a weak association between <i>H. pylori</i> infection and rosacea and between successful eradication of <i>H. pylori</i> and improvement of rosacea manifestations<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e705>Microbes and the cathelicidin axis in rosacea</h2><p class="" id=d25769e708>Products of microbes can be recognized by cells of the innate immune system and activate, for example, Toll-like receptors (TLRs) and the G-protein-coupled receptor proteinase-activated receptor 2 (PAR<sub>2</sub>) that are expressed by keratinocytes and can nurture inflammatory processes<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. Notably, TLR-2 and probably PAR<sub>2</sub> are upregulated in patients with rosacea, and <i>in vitro</i> activation of both receptors promotes the activation of cathelicidin, an anti-microbial peptide that is also overexpressed in patients with rosacea (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>)<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a>,<a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. TLR-2 signaling can further activate the NLRP3 inflammasome with subsequent IL-1- and TNF-mediated inflammation amplification and prostaglandin E<sub>2</sub> synthesis, which support pustule formation, pain sensation, and vascular responses<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>. TLR-2 activation additionally promotes the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines, chemokines, proteases, and pro-angiogenic factors, which are mediators associated with rosacea symptoms such as erythema, telangiectasia, or inflammation or a combination of these<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-33">33</a><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. A direct link among <i>Demodex</i> mites, microbes, and PAR<sub>2</sub> or TLR-2 has not yet been demonstrated in rosacea. Also, functional <i>in vivo</i> data for cathelicidin in humans are still lacking. In mice, TLR-2-induced cathelicidin activation needs functional kallikrein-5 (KLK-5) protease activity for the formation of rosacea-like erythema and angiogenesis<sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup>. However, KLK-5 is increased in patients with rosacea and KLK(s)-5 can activate PAR<sub>2</sub><sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>. PAR<sub>2</sub> is a known mediator of (neuro)inflammation, pruritus and pain sensation, T-cell and neutrophil recruitment to sites of inflammation, mast cell degranulation and vasodilation, and promotion of release of inflammatory mediators such as IL-1, IL-6, IL-8, TNF, chemokines, matrix metalloproteinases, and prostaglandins<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a></sup>. PAR<sub>2</sub> is expressed by various skin cell types, including keratinocytes, endothelial cells, and innate and adaptive immune cells, activated by microbe-derived proteases, and interacts with TLRs<sup><a href="#ref-39">39</a><a href="#ref-43">43</a></sup>. The upstream signal or signals of enhanced PAR<sub>2</sub>, TLR-2, and KLK-5 proteins in rosacea have not been identified yet. However, vitamin D can increase TLR-2 and KLK-5 expression in keratinocytes and is found in excess in some patients with rosacea and might be a candidate (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>)<sup><a href="#ref-44">44</a></sup>. Further activation of pro-inflammatory receptors (for example, PAR<sub>2</sub>) can lead to a secondary skin barrier deficit which may lead to more inflammation<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a>,<a href="#ref-46">46</a></sup>.</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/18074/9a397584-5092-4510-836d-1d493a797a68_figure1.gif"><img alt="9a397584-5092-4510-836d-1d493a797a68_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/18074/9a397584-5092-4510-836d-1d493a797a68_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. Current understanding of the pathomechanisms in rosacea.</h3><p id=d25769e821>Rosacea triggers lead to the activation of downstream effectors (white boxes) in various cell types (gray boxes) probably by the activation of a few specific receptors and channels (white boxes), which in cooperation nurture processes of (neurogenic) inflammation, including edema and vasodilation, fibrosis, pain, and angiogenesis (lilac boxes). For instance, epidermal and probably immune cell-expressed proteinase-activated receptor-2 (PAR<sub>2</sub>) and Toll-like receptor-2 (TLR-2) are activated by rosacea-associated bacterial and <i>Demodex</i>-derived proteases, leading to the induction of the inflammasome and subsequent release of pro-inflammatory agents such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-) and interleukin-1 (IL-1) as well as enhanced expression of the innate immune peptide LL-37. ATP, adenosine triphosphate; CGRP, calcitonin gene-related peptide; ET1, endothelin-1; ETAR, endothelin A receptor; KLK-5, kallikrein-5; LL-37, cathelicidin; MMP, matrix metalloproteinase; NALP3, NACHT, LRR, and PYD domain-containing protein 3; PACAP, pituitary adenylate cyclase-activating peptide; SP, substance P; TGF-, transforming growth factor-beta; TRP, transient receptor potential; TSLP, thymic stromal lymphopoietin; VEGF, vascular endothelial growth factor.</p></div></div><p class="" id=d25769e834>The importance of microbes and microbe-associated products and their respective target receptor(s) for rosacea pathophysiology is further supported by the recent observation that rosacea patients with SIBO (small intestinal bacterial overgrowth) benefit from treatment with the T-cell modulator rifaximin and present with improved rosacea symptoms<sup><a href="#ref-47">47</a><a href="#ref-50">50</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e848>Neurovascular processes and neurogenic inflammation in rosacea</h2><p class="" id=d25769e851>Patients with rosacea react to a vast panel of trigger factors such as temperature changes, heat, cold, exercise, UV radiation, and spicy food and alcoholic beverages with deterioration of rosacea lesions<sup><a href="#ref-51">51</a></sup>. The precise mediating receptors and messengers for each trigger factor are in most cases not identified, but recent transcriptomic analysis and immunohistochemical findings indicate that the transient receptor potential familyin particular, members of the ankyrin subfamily (TRPA1) and the vanilloid subfamily (TRPV1 and TRPV4)might convey cellular responses to several of the rosacea-specific trigger factors<sup><a href="#ref-52">52</a>,<a href="#ref-53">53</a></sup>. TRPV1 and TRPA1 are well-described targets for various pungent compounds such as capsaicin (TRPV1) and mustard oil (TRPA1 and TRPV1)<sup><a href="#ref-54">54</a></sup> and could render rosacea stimuli such as heat (TRPV1)<sup><a href="#ref-55">55</a></sup>, possibly cold temperatures (TRPA1)<sup><a href="#ref-56">56</a></sup>, UVB irradiation (TRPV4)<sup><a href="#ref-57">57</a></sup>, and toxins and cosmetics ingredients (for example, TRPA1)<sup><a href="#ref-58">58</a></sup> into clinical rosacea manifestations. In particular, neuronally expressed TRP channels could be responsible for the activation of the cutaneous vasculature leading to flushing, one hallmark feature of rosacea, and erythema by a neurovascular mechanism involving neurogenic inflammation mediators (see <a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>)<sup><a href="#ref-59">59</a><a href="#ref-61">61</a></sup>. Rosacea trigger factor activated TRP channels in fact lead to the release of vasoactive neuropeptides such as substance P (SP), pituitary adenylate cyclase-activating peptide (PACAP), and the migraine-associated calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP)<sup><a href="#ref-61">61</a><a href="#ref-63">63</a></sup>. Sensory nerves also express the neuro-inflammatory TLR-2 and PAR<sub>2</sub> which might perpetuate the neurovascular dysregulation observed in rosacea. Because TRP channels (particularly TRPA1 and TRPV1) and PAR<sub>2</sub> can crosstalk with neuropeptide receptors or at least trigger neuropeptide release, these interactions could help to sustain the neurovascular loop and neurogenic inflammation in rosacea<sup><a href="#ref-52">52</a>,<a href="#ref-53">53</a>,<a href="#ref-64">64</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d25769e920>Along sensory nerves, dysregulation of the autonomic nervous system (ANS) can produce facial flushing. However, a distinct role for this mechanism in rosacea-typical flushing is not confirmed at this stage but could be promoted by ANS-expressed PACAP or stress-induced increase of skin sympathetic nerve activity<sup><a href="#ref-65">65</a><a href="#ref-67">67</a></sup>. In summary, the neurovascular circuits that appear to be involved in rosaceas pathophysiology might explain the patient-specific trigger profiles and differences in the clinical presentation of rosacea. Altered downstream signaling and target structures might account for the phenotypic variability observed in rosacea.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e933>Genetics of rosacea</h2><p class="" id=d25769e936>The existence of genetic traits that underlie rosacea is expressed by a positive family history for rosacea. Only recently, a handful of genome-wide association studies have been conducted that propose genetic risk loci for rosacea. An American study included individuals of European descent (all customers of the genetic company 23andme, Mountain View, CA, USA) identified single-nucleotide polymorphisms in the butyrophilin-like 2 (BTNL2) and the human leukocyte antigenDRA genes. Both genes are associated with the major histocompatibility complex of the acquired immune system, indicating a central role for dysregulations of the immune system in rosaceas pathogenesis. Another study found a null mutation polymorphism in the glutathione S-transferase (GST) gene that encodes for an enzyme involved in cellular oxidative stress<sup><a href="#ref-68">68</a>,<a href="#ref-69">69</a></sup>. In a case study of a patient with granulomatous rosacea, a polymorphism in the NOD2/CARD15 gene was observed<sup><a href="#ref-70">70</a></sup>. NOD2/CARD15 protein functions as a caspase recruitment protein and is associated with the function of innate immune system receptors such as TLR-2 and subsequent inflammatory processes. Interestingly, a recent population-based case control study revealed that rosacea shares genetic risk loci with various autoimmune diseases such as multiple sclerosis, type 1 diabetes mellitus, celiac disease, and rheumatoid arthritis. This observation underlines the importance of a thorough risk assessment for the individual patient with rosacea at risk of developing an autoimmune disorder so that multidisciplinary medical care can be organized for affected patients<sup><a href="#ref-71">71</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e957>Therapeutic management of rosacea</h2><div class=section><a name=d25769e960 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>General skin care</h3><p class="" id=d25769e965>Consequent adherence to skin care advice and consequent application of adequate non-irritating skin care can significantly prevent events of rosacea aggravation and improve the patients quality of life. Skin care advice consists primarily of avoidance of trigger factors (including stress management), usage of sunscreen with 30+ sun protection factor, application of moisturizers for dry skin and drying applications for oily skin, and gentle cleansing of the whole face<sup><a href="#ref-72">72</a></sup>.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e976>Symptom-based treatment</h2><p class="" id=d25769e979>Because rosacea symptoms derive from distinct pathophysiologies, the therapeutic regimen will in most cases consist of combinations of topicals with systemics or physical therapy or both<sup><a href="#ref-73">73</a>,<a href="#ref-74">74</a></sup>.</p><div class=section><a name=d25769e989 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Flushing and erythema</h3><p class="" id=d25769e994>According to recent guidelines, two approved topicals can be used to treat persistent erythema in adults with rosacea: brimonidine<sup><a href="#ref-75">75</a><a href="#ref-77">77</a></sup> (a beta2-adrenergic agonist) and oxymetazoline hydrochloride 1% cream<sup><a href="#ref-78">78</a></sup> (an alpha1A-adrenoreceptor agonist that also activates alpha2-receptors at higher concentrations)<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a href="#ref-17">17</a>,<a href="#ref-72">72</a>,<a href="#ref-73">73</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d25769e1021>Certain laser therapies can be used, but they should be avoided in pain-sensitized patients<sup><a href="#ref-79">79</a><a href="#ref-81">81</a></sup>. Off-label usage of beta-blockers such as carvedilol or adrenergic receptor modulators (for example, brimonidine) may alleviate these symptoms<sup><a href="#ref-80">80</a></sup>. In case of pain association, an analgesic therapy with, for instance, lidocaine gel (4%) or polidocanol cream in mild cases and antiphlogistics (for example, ibuprofen), anti-depressants (for example, amitriptyline), or anticonvulsants (for example, gabapentin and pregabalin) in more severe cases may be helpful<sup><a href="#ref-82">82</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d25769e1040 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Telangiectasia</h3><p class="" id=d25769e1045>Only a few options exist for the treatment of telangiectasia, among which physical laser therapy and intravascular aethoxysklerol (0.5%1%) injections are most commonly used.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e1052>Papules and pustules</h2><p class="" id=d25769e1055>Patients with mild to moderate papules and pustules benefit from topical treatment with ivermectin (1%)<sup><a href="#ref-83">83</a></sup>, metronidazole (1%)<sup><a href="#ref-84">84</a><a href="#ref-86">86</a></sup>, azelaic acid (15%)<sup><a href="#ref-87">87</a></sup>, or sodium sulfacetamide sulfur<sup><a href="#ref-88">88</a></sup>. Off-label therapy with topical erythromycin (2%), isotretinoin, clindamycin, permethrin, doxycycline, minocycline, and oral erythromycin has been reported with good results. Combination therapy often helps to prolong symptom-free periods. In severe or therapy-refractory cases, systemic treatment with metronidazole, clarithromycin, and azithromycin can be conducted. In cases of <i>Demodex</i> infestation, permethrin (off-label) or ivermectin cream and oral ivermectin can improve the therapeutic result<sup><a href="#ref-72">72</a>,<a href="#ref-89">89</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e1091>Phymata</h2><p class="" id=d25769e1094>Patients affected by mild phymata benefit from tetracycline therapy. Standard therapies for progressed phymata are ablative (destructive) laser and dermato-surgery. Low-dose isotretinoin appears to reduce phymata by its anti-inflammatory capacity and by lessening the number of sebaceous glands and inhibiting their proliferation<sup><a href="#ref-90">90</a><a href="#ref-92">92</a></sup>. Systemic immunomodulatory therapy such as dapsone was used in some cases with mixed results.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e1108>Facial (lymph)edema</h2><p class="" id=d25769e1111>No US Food and Drug Administration-approved therapy exists. Immunomodulators such as isotretinoin and dapsone or combination therapies with doxycycline/prednisolone have been tried, but the therapeutic value is unclear<sup><a href="#ref-93">93</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e1121>Ocular rosacea</h2><p class="" id=d25769e1124>The appropriate treatment of ocular rosacea requires a multidisciplinary effort from ophthalmologists and dermatologists. Basic lid hygiene routines such as warm compresses and lubricating drops can be conducted by the patient. Artificial tear substitutes help ocular dryness and accompanying burning and stinging. Successful therapy with topical ivermectin was recently reported<sup><a href="#ref-94">94</a></sup>. In more severe cases, cyclosporine eye drops and systemic tetracycline can be prescribed<sup><a href="#ref-73">73</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e1138>Summary and future directions</h2><p class="" id=d25769e1141>Basic, translational, and clinical research has significantly increased our understanding of a common skin disease, rosacea, leading to novel anti-inflammatory and anti-erythematous treatments. Combination therapies, similar to those for acne and atopic dermatitis, are a key for the successful therapy of this poly-symptomatic disease<sup><a href="#ref-95">95</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d25769e1148>Several questions remain to be answered: which genes are involved in rosacea? What is the prevalence of rosacea globally? Which are the key mediators and receptors of rosacea in the various clinical symptoms and signs? Which comorbidities are associated with rosacea? How can we optimize the diagnosis and treatment of rosacea? Translational research is demanded to better understand and treat this commonly neglected skin disease.</p></div><div id=article1-back class=generated-article-footer><div class=back-section><a name=d25769e1 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d26056>Grant information</h2><p>The author(s) declared that no grants were involved in supporting this work.</p></div><div class=back-section><a name=d25769e1155 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d25769e1157>Acknowledgments</h2><p class="" id=d25769e1160>We thank Hasna Amal Boumenar for proofreading the manuscript and assisting in the preparation of <a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>.</p></div><div class=back-section><a name=d25769e1167 class=n-a></a><span class="research-layout prime-recommended-wrapper reference-heading"><span class="f1r-icon icon-79_faculty_recommended_badge red vmiddle default-cursor"></span><span class="prime-red big">F1000 recommended</span></span><h2 class=main-title id=d26365>References</h2><div class="section ref-list"><a name=d25769e1167 class=n-a></a><ul><li><a name=ref-1 class=n-a></a><span class=label>1. </span>&nbsp;<span class=citation><a name=d25769e1174 class=n-a></a>Wilkin J, Dahl M, Detmar M, <i> et al.</i>: Standard classification of rosacea: Report of the National Rosacea Society Expert Committee on the Classification and Staging of Rosacea. <i>J Am Acad Dermatol.</i> 2002;
