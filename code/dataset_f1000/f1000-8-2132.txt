development and open science in bioinformatics. Launched in 2000, BOSC has been held every year since. BOSC 2019, the 20th annual BOSC, took place as one of the Communities of Special Interest (COSIs) at the Intelligent Systems for Molecular Biology meeting (ISMB/ECCB 2019). The two-day meeting included a total of 46 talks and 55 posters, as well as eight Birds of a Feather interest groups. The two-day meeting (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>) included a total of <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.open-bio.org/events/bosc-2019/bosc-2019-schedule/" id=d13299e414>46 talks and 55 posters</a>, as well as eight self-organized <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.open-bio.org/events/bosc/bofs/" id=d13299e417>Birds of a Feather</a> interest groups (<a href="#f3">Figure 3</a>). During the meeting, attendees generated over 1500 <a target=xrefwindow href="https://twitter.com/search?q=%23BOSC2019&amp;src=typed_query&amp;f=live" id=d13299e424>tweets mentioning #BOSC2019</a> (you can find them in JSON format <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.dropbox.com/s/phznj50qkjet1lh/twitter_BOSC.json.gz?dl=0" id=d13299e427>here</a>).</p><a name=f2 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23767/754c4e0f-bd35-4135-92c2-1a6496c4f946_figure2.gif"><img alt="754c4e0f-bd35-4135-92c2-1a6496c4f946_figure2.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23767/754c4e0f-bd35-4135-92c2-1a6496c4f946_figure2.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 2. This figure is shared under the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/2.0/" id=d13299e474>CC BY-SA 2.0 license</a>.</p></div></div><p class="" id=d13299e493>The meeting opened with chair Nomi Harris noting that <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.open-bio.org/events/bosc/about/" id=d13299e495>over its 20 years</a> of history, BOSC has been held in 12 different countries, 6 US states and 2 Canadian provinces. BOSC co-chair Heather Wiencko followed these remarks with details about the Open Bioinformatics Foundation, BOSCs parent organization, and Kai Blin discussed the OBFs participation in <a target=xrefwindow href="https://obf.github.io/GSoC/" id=d13299e498>Google Summer of Code</a>.</p><p class="" id=d13299e503>The talks at BOSC were, as usual, organized into topic-based sessions, which included four recurring topic sessions:</p><div class=list><a name=d13299e506 class=n-a></a><ul><li><p id=d13299e510>Open data</p></li><li><p id=d13299e516>Open science</p></li><li><p id=d13299e522>Workflows</p></li><li><p id=d13299e528>Late-breaking Lightning Talks</p></li></ul></div><p class="" id=d13299e533>This year, BOSC added four new sessions to the program:</p><div class=list><a name=d13299e536 class=n-a></a><ul><li><p id=d13299e540>Data crunching</p></li><li><p id=d13299e546>Data modeling and formats</p></li><li><p id=d13299e552>Containers</p></li><li><p id=d13299e558>Building Open Source Communities</p></li></ul></div><p class="" id=d13299e563>Two of the new sessions, <i>Data crunching</i> and <i>Data modeling and formats</i>, discussed different aspects of working with data: representing it, storing it, analyzing it, and sharing it, all in the context of open source software and open data. The new <i>Containers</i> session discussed the use of containers such as Docker for enabling reproducible run-anywhere analyses. The popular <i>Workflows</i> session covered tools and metrics for CWL, the workflow language that was germinated during BOSC 2014, as well as other workflow systems. The <i>Building Open Source Communities</i> session (a new session that shares a familiar acronym with our conference) was devoted to efforts in open source and open science that go beyond software; for example, there were talks about training efforts, social networking in science, and ways to promote diversity and inclusivity in open source communities.</p><div class=section><a name=d13299e581 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Keynote</h3><p class="" id=d13299e586>This years <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.open-bio.org/events/bosc-2019/bosc-2019-keynote/" id=d13299e588>keynote speaker</a> was University of Cape Town professor Nicola Mulder, who spoke on Building infrastructure for responsible open science in Africa. In her keynote, she highlighted how sharing data in Africa involves technical, ethical and social challenges, observing that Its really hard to convince people to share their data and their tools when they have such a history of being exploited. Despite these obstacles, the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://h3abionet.org/" id=d13299e591>H3ABioNet Consortium</a> that Prof. Mulder leads is making progress in building a pan-African bioinformatics network offering a diverse set of services across the continent. Among other things, they (1) provide distributed training, with a recent iteration teaching 700 people across Africa simultaneously; (2) support an African Open Science Platform; and (3) run crowdsourcing challenges to design predictive models for Malaria.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d13299e597 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Birds of a Feather</h3><p class="" id=d13299e602>Birds of a Feather (BoFs) are informal, self-organized meetups focused on specific topics. They are a great way to meet other like-minded community members and have an in-depth discussion on a topic of interest. BoFs, which can be proposed and run by any attendee, are always a popular part of BOSC. BOSC 2019 had eight <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.open-bio.org/events/bosc/2019-bofs/" id=d13299e604>BoFs</a>:</p><div class=list><a name=d13299e608 class=n-a></a><ul><li><p id=d13299e612>Welcome to BOSC (<a href="#f3">Figure 3</a>): This now-traditional BoF is designed to welcome both new and returning members, answer their questions, and gather feedback to help the organizers make future meetings even better.</p></li><li><p id=d13299e621>Common Workflow Language: Participants learned about using CWL to create and run data analysis workflows in a portable and interoperable manner, and shared their experiences with writing and running CWL.</p></li><li><p id=d13299e627>Reimagining the paper: This BoF offered an opportunity to discuss and critique tools designed to drive reproducible research.</p><p class="" id=d13299e630>KNIME: Attendees learned about the open source KNIME Analytics Platform for reproducible interactive data analysis, and offered feedback on use cases and features.</p></li><li><p id=d13299e636>Variant Storage and Analysis: Participants in this BoF discussed challenges and solutions for ingesting and storing variant data.</p></li><li><p id=d13299e642>The Open Bioinformatics Foundation public board meeting: This is discussed in the next section.</p></li><li><p id=d13299e649>Cloud Capable Data and Tooling: This BoF focused on pain points and opportunities for working with large datasets in the cloud.</p></li><li><p id=d13299e655>Biopython and Biotite Collaboration: Participants in this BoF discussed how to bring together the best aspects of both of these Python-focused projects.</p></li></ul></div></div><div class=section><a name=d13299e661 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>OBF Board Meeting</h3><p class="" id=d13299e666>The <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.open-bio.org/" id=d13299e668>OBF</a>, an organization open to anyone who demonstrates interest in open source or open science in biology, holds <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.open-bio.org/board/" id=d13299e671>Board</a> meetings at least once a year that are open to the public. The <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.open-bio.org/2019/08/16/obf-public-board-meeting-july-2019/" id=d13299e674>July 2019 public Board meeting</a> (<a href="#f4">Figure 4</a>) was held at ISMB/ECCB 2019, and was open to any conference attendees who wanted to participate, as well as to OBF members participating remotely. Public OBF Board meetings give OBF members an opportunity to vote on new Board member candidates and officers. At this meeting, Malvika Sharan was elected as a new Board member. Further topics of discussion included plans for the creation of an OBF-wide code of conduct that will cover new member projects and in-person events, potential changes to the OBF membership policies, and the community-assisted crafting of a mission statement for the OBF.</p><a name=f4 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23767/754c4e0f-bd35-4135-92c2-1a6496c4f946_figure4.gif"><img alt="754c4e0f-bd35-4135-92c2-1a6496c4f946_figure4.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23767/754c4e0f-bd35-4135-92c2-1a6496c4f946_figure4.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 4. The OBF Board (including newly-elected member Malvika Sharan, far left) at the public Board meeting held during BOSC 2019.</h3><p id=d13299e691>Photo by Michael Crusoe. This figure is shared under the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/2.0/" id=d13299e693>CC BY-SA 2.0 license</a>.</p></div></div></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d13299e716>CollaborationFest</h2><p class="" id=d13299e719>The two days after BOSC, about 50 people participated in the OBF-run <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.open-bio.org/events/bosc/collaborationfest/" id=d13299e721>CollaborationFest</a> (CoFest for short), an event at which participants work together to contribute to open source bioinformatics software, documentation, training materials, and use cases. Organized by Alexander Peltzer, Michael Heuer, Peter Cock, and Bastian Rieck, CoFest 2019 was held at <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.dayone.swiss/dayone.html" id=d13299e724>DayOne, The Swiss Innovation Hub for Personalized Medicine</a> in Basel (<a href="#f5">Figure 5</a>). Participants worked together to fix bugs, add new features, write tests, and improve documentation in new and ongoing projects including <a target=xrefwindow href="https://biopython.org/" id=d13299e730>Biopython</a>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.commonwl.org/" id=d13299e733>Common Workflow Language (CWL)</a>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.nextflow.io/" id=d13299e737>Nextflow</a>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://github.com/bigdatagenomics/cannoli" id=d13299e740>Cannoli</a>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://github.com/codeisscience/software-usage-reporting" id=d13299e743>CodeCite</a>, the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://github.com/terra-workflows/synthetic-seq-data" id=d13299e746>Synthetic Sequence Data Project</a>, and the OBFs <a target=xrefwindow href="https://github.com/OBF/obf-docs/pull/44" id=d13299e749>affiliated project policy</a>.</p><a name=f5 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23767/754c4e0f-bd35-4135-92c2-1a6496c4f946_figure5.gif"><img alt="754c4e0f-bd35-4135-92c2-1a6496c4f946_figure5.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23767/754c4e0f-bd35-4135-92c2-1a6496c4f946_figure5.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 5. Some of the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://github.com/SoftDev4Research/codecite" id=d13299e762>CodeCite</a> team at CoFest 2019.</h3><p id=d13299e766>This group worked on software citation tools that can be used to generate CFF (citation file format) based on existing metadata on GitHub or Zenodo. Photo by Sonika Tyagi. This figure is shared under the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/2.0/" id=d13299e768>CC BY-SA 2.0 license</a>.</p></div></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d13299e791>Plans for BOSC 2020</h2><p class="" id=d13299e794>BOSC 2019 closed with an announcement: next years meeting will be held in collaboration with Galaxys Community Conference as the Bioinformatics Community Conference (<a target=xrefwindow href="https://bcc2020.github.io/" id=d13299e796>BCC2020</a>), which will take place in Toronto, Canada, July 1922, 2020. BCC2020 will include a training day, the main two-day conference, two CoFest days, and two additional optional CoFest Encore days.</p><div class=section><a name=d13299e800 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Consent for publication</h3><p class="" id=d13299e805>All photos in this report are shared under a <a target=xrefwindow href="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/2.0/" id=d13299e807>CC BY-SA 2.0 license</a>.
