Abstract </h2> <div class="article-abstract article-page-general-text-mobile research-layout"> <div class="abstract-text is-expanded"> <b>Background:</b> Breast cancer is a major health problem for women globally. Many attempts have been promoted to cure cancer by finding new anticancer medicines from natural resources. Despite the richness of biodiversity discovered, there are some natural resources that remain unexplored. Fruit peels of Duku (<i>Lansium domesticum</i> Corr.) are rich with compounds that may have the potential to be developed as anticancer drugs. This study aimed to isolate cytotoxic compounds from the fruit peels of <i>L. domesticum </i>and assess their cytotoxic nature against T47D cells.<br/> <b>Methods:</b> Powdered peels were macerated with ethyl acetate and the filtrate was evaporated to give EtOAc extract A. Dried extract A was triturated with n-hexane to give n-hexane soluble fraction B and insoluble fraction C. The cytotoxic nature of these three&nbsp; samples were assessed using MTT assay using T47D cells and doxorubicin as a control.<br/> <b>Results:</b> Fraction C that showed the smallest IC50 (25.56 &plusmn; 0.64g/mL) value compared to&nbsp; extract A and fraction B. Fraction C was further fractionated by vacuum liquid chromatography to give 6 subfractions. Subfraction 2 showed a single compound based on thin layer chromatography, and this compound was identified as Lamesticumin A on the basis of its spectroscopic data. Lamesticumin A demonstrated cytotoxic activity against T47D cell lines with an IC50 value of 15.68&nbsp;&plusmn; 0.30&micro;g/mL.<br/> <b>Conclusions:</b> Further research is needed to investigate the potential of the natural compound Lamesticumin A derived from<i> L. domesticum</i> fruit peel as an anticancer therapy. </div> <div class=abstract-for-mobile> <div class="margin-top-30 In Indonesia, it was reported that approximately 21% of cancer deaths among women were due to breast cancer<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>. Therefore, new medicines to eradicate this type of cancer is required. Duku (<i>Lansium domesticum</i> Correa) widely grows in Indonesia. Traditionally, <i>L. domesticum</i> bark and seeds have been used to treat dysentery and fever<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. Based on previous studies, chloroform and methanol extracts of <i>L. domesticum</i> displayed cytotoxic activity on murine melanoma (B<sub>16</sub>F<sub>10</sub>) and colon cancer (HT29) cells<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. In addition, it has been shown that ethanol and ethyl acetate fractions of the peel have a deterrent activity on DNA damage in lymphoblast cells induced by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> exposure<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. Onoceranoid-type of triterpenoids have been isolated from twigs and leaves of <i>L. domesticum</i>, and these compounds showed antibacterial and antimutagenic activities<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a>,<a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. In this study, the cytotoxic effects of compound extracted from the peels of <i>L. domesticum</i> are assayed against breast cancer T47D cells.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d12288e257>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d12288e260 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Plant material</h3><p class="" id=d12288e265>The fruits of <i>L. domesticum</i> were collected on March 2018 from Bantul, Yogyakarta (GPS : -7.871098, 110.394854) and identified at the Department of Pharmaceutical Biology, Faculty of Pharmacy, Universitas Gadjah Mada.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d12288e272 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Chemicals and equipment</h3><p class="" id=d12288e277>Organic solvents (methanol, ethyl acetate, chloroform, n-hexane) used were pro analytical grades obtained from Merck. Silica gel F<sub>254</sub>, Silica gel PF<sub>254</sub>, (Merck), RPMI 1640, Fetal Bovine Serum, Penicillin-Streptomycin, Fungizon, Sodium bicarbonate (Gibco), HEPES (Invitrogen), Phosphate Buffered Saline, MTT (Sigma Aldrich cat. M5655), Doxorubicin (Sigma Aldrich). Infrared (KBr) spectrum was obtained from spectrophotometer (Shimadzu) using a method previously described by Ashokkumar and Ramaswamy<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. Ultraviolet spectrum (CHCl<sub>3</sub>) was obtained from UV spectrophotometer (Hitachi UH 5300). Sample (1 mg) were diluted in 1 mL CHCl<sub>3</sub> and was run between 200400 nm. Spectra of <sup>1</sup>H- and <sup>13</sup>C- NMR in CDCl<sub>3</sub> solvent were measured using JEOL JNM-ECZ 500R/S1 at 500 MHz.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d12288e307 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Extraction and fractionation</h3><p class="" id=d12288e312>The peel was separated from the fruit, dried in oven 50C for 24 hours and powdered using a blender. Powdered <i>L. domesticum</i> fruit peel (200 gr) was macerated with ethyl acetate (EtOAc; 2 L) overnight. This solution was filtrated (0,45m) and the filtrate was evaporated to dryness with a rotary evaporator set at 50C, to give dried EtOAc extract (A, 50.13 g). In order to separate the extract into non-polar and polar fractions, 6 gr of extract A was diluted 5 times using n-hexane (20 mL) to give soluble n-hexane fraction B (supernatant; 3.03 g) and insoluble n-hexane fraction C (residue; 2.83 g).</p><p class="" id=d12288e318>Fraction C was the most active among other fractions (see <i>Results</i>), and was therefore further fractionated using vacuum liquid chromatography as described by Mae Sri Hartati <i>et al</i>.<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>. In brief, using silica gel preparation grade (15 gr) as stationary phase this was eluted with n-hexane and increasing amounts of ethyl acetate. Six subfractions were obtained and subfraction 2 contained a major compound which appeared as white crystals (referred to as compound 1). Compound 1 was obtained as a single compound from subfraction 2, while the other subfractions still contained various compounds. Compound 1 (142.6 mg), had a melting point at 140150C (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>). Compound 1 was identified using spectroscopy data such as ultraviolet (UV), infrared (IR), <sup>13</sup>C-NMR and <sup>1</sup>H-NMR (see section <i>Chemicals and equipment</i>).</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23191/00632097-3576-4817-9e42-5a34ea21ae0a_figure1.gif"><img alt="00632097-3576-4817-9e42-5a34ea21ae0a_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23191/00632097-3576-4817-9e42-5a34ea21ae0a_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. Isolation process of cytotoxic compound 1 (subfraction 2) from <i>Lansium domesticum</i> fruit peels.</h3></div></div></div><div class=section><a name=d12288e362 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Cytotoxic assay</h3><p class="" id=d12288e367>The bioassay followed the methodology described by Bahuguna <i>et al</i>.<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup> with modifications. In brief, 100 l T47D cells (in RPMI mediaFaculty of Medicine, Universitas Gadjah Mada) were placed in each well of a 96 well micr oplates, resulting in 1  10<sup>4</sup> cells/well. The cells were incubated for 24 hours at 37C in a CO<sub>2</sub> incubator.</p><p class="" id=d12288e383>Extract A, fractions B and C and compound 1 (5mg) were dissolved in DMSO (50 L). Serial concentrations of extract and fractions (50, 25, 12.5, 6.25, 3.125 g/mL), compound 1 (25, 12.5, 6.25, 3.125 g/mL) and doxorubicin (positive control; 0.5, 0.25, 0.125, 0.0625, 0.0312 g/mL) were obtained. Cells were treated with the dose dependent samples and incubated for 24 hours at 37C. The culture medium was removed by pipette, and MTT solution (100L) was added to each well and incubated for 4 hours at 37C. After incubation, stop solution (10% SDS, 100 L) was added to each well and let stand at room temperature for 24 hours.</p><p class="" id=d12288e386>Absorbance was measured by microplate reader (Bio Rad) at 595 nm. positive control The data generated were used to plot a dose-response curve and IC<sub>50</sub> of the samples was determined.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d12288e394 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Statistical analysis</h3><p class="" id=d12288e399>The IC<sub>50</sub> values were analyzed by one-way ANOVA with statistical significance P &lt; 0.05 using IBM SPSS ver.23.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d12288e409>Results</h2><div class=section><a name=d12288e412 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Compound 1 characterization</h3><p class="" id=d12288e417>Identified as Lamesticumin A.</p><p class="" id=d12288e420>White crystal. The T47D cell line is an epithelial breast cancer cell subtype luminal A cell line that express estrogen and progesterone receptors<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. Based on National Cancer Institute guidelines, a natural compound has potent anticancer activity if it has IC50 &lt;4 g/ml or 10 M<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d12288e1237>Many triterpenoid compounds have been previously isolated from <i>L. domesticum</i>. Most of these compounds are UV inactive or have no strong UV absorbance because triterpenoids lack of a conjugated functional group<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>. Lansiosida A and Dukunolida A has been isolated from n-hexane extract of <i>L. domesticum</i> fruit peel<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a>,<a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. Lamesticumin A is an onoceranoid-type triterpenoid, isolated previously from <i>L. domesticum</i> twigs, that has antibacterial activity against <i>Staphylococcus aureus, Staphylococcus epidermidis, Micrococcus luteus, Bacillus subtilis, Micrococcus pyogenes</i> and <i>Bacillus cereus</i> with minimum inhibitory concentration of &lt;15 g/ml<sup>5</sup>. Another onoceranoid-type triterpenoid Lansium acid I-IX were isolated from <i>L. domesticum</i> leaves, which was reported to have antimutagenic activity<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d12288e1277>Based on several <i>in vitro</i> tests, some terpenoid compounds had anticancer activity. Sesquiterpene lactone compounds are known to inhibit Nf-kB, thereby inducing apoptosis<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>. Celastrol has anticancer properties by regulating various transcription factors, angiogenesis processes, cell cycle arrest and induction of apoptosis<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. Betulinic acid can induce apoptosis in HT-29 colon cancer cells and acts as a chemosensitizer for chemotherapeutic agents in wildtype adenocarcinoma cancer cells (SNU-C5/WT)<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. Clematangoticosides D and F from <i>Clematis tangutica</i> are known to have cytotoxic activity against human gastric cancer cell line (SGC-7901) with IC<sub>50</sub> 24.22 and 21.35 M, respectively<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. Cycloartane-type and oleanane-type triterpenoids from <i>Ligularia przewalskii</i> show cytotoxicity in Hela, HEPG2, SGC7901, MDA231, HL-60, and Lewis cell lines with IC<sub>50</sub> 8.4024.39 M<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d12288e1316>It has been reported that natural compounds combined with low doses of antineoplastics can increase effectiveness and reduce toxic effects<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. Betulinic acid can induce apoptosis when combined with 5-fluorouracil, irinotecan and oxaliplatin<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. Ursolic acid (UA), a pentacyclic triterpenoid, is known to have anticancer activity through interfering with multiple signaling pathways. Furthermore, UA has been shown to act as a chemosensitizing agent to increase the effect of conventional anticancer drugs<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>, and to increase the effect of doxorubicin by increasing the cellular amount of the drug in the MCF cell line<sup><a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>. Further study is needed to investigate the possibility of Lamesticumin A to be combined with doxorubicin for its potential to have synergistic effect.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d12288e1338>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d12288e1341>Extract, fractions and Lamesticumin A derived from the peel of <i>L. domesticum</i> showed cytotoxic activity against the T47D breast cancer cell line. Further research is needed to investigate the potential of the natural compound Lamesticumin A derived from <i>L. domesticum</i> fruit peel as an anticancer therapy.</p></div><div id=article1-body
