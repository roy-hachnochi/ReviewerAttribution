            <meta name="dc.description" content="Back pain (BP) is a common condition of major social importance and poorly understood pathogenesis. Intervertebral lumbar disc degeneration in all its guises is one of the major biological risk factors for BP . Previously, we identified the locus at 8q24.21 associated with chronic BP, which has been found elsewhere associated with sciatica after surgery for lumbar disc herniation. In the current study we used co-localisation methods to identify the gene most likely to harbor the causal variant. We show that the same functional variant at the 8q24.21 locus is responsible for both lumbar disc degeneration and BP, and we also studied the effects of this locus on related phenotypes. Our results link the locus to intervertebral disc and bone mineral density, but not to anthropometric measurements, thus corroborating the epidemiological evidence. Moreover, the same functional variant at the locus is more likely to affect the expression of the nearby FAM49B gene, rather than the GSDMC gene, which was previously proposed as a causative one for BP.">

According to the Global Burden of Disease 2016 study, it leads the list of disabling conditions in many parts of the world<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. The greatest biological risk for episodes of severe BP is thought to be lumbar disc degeneration (LDD)<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. Rather than a discrete disease, LDD is now considered a continuous lifelong aging process that usually starts in the third decade of life but may also be seen in children<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. It is highly heritable<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>, as well as being influenced by smoking<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>, age<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup> and body mass index<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>. The overall burden of lumbar disc degeneration on magnetic resonance imaging is a strong predictor of BP episodes<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a>–<a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>. There is a clear genetic predisposition to both BP and LDD with estimates of heritability in the range of 30%–70%<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a>–<a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>. Also, these traits have been shown to share genetic risk factors<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. However, little is known about the genetic architecture of BP and only three associated genetic loci have been identified<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a>,<a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d27871e332>This work was focused on the locus at chromosome 8 (8q24.21), which was identified in our previous BP studies<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a>,<a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. The same locus has been reported associated with having lumbar disc herniation (LDH) in Icelanders undergoing surgery for sciatica<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. Bjornsdottir <i>et al.</i> additionally reported an association of this region to height, but concluded that the region does not influence LDH through height. They noted that the most highly associated SNP, rs6651255, was linked to expression of the nearby gasdermin (<i>GSDMC</i>) gene in GTEx data<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. Further, the immune-related function of gasdermins implied that the inflammatory and immunological consequences of disk herniation may be linked to sciatica symptom severity.</p><p class="" id=d27871e356>However, closely located SNPs associated with different traits do not always reflect pleiotropy<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>. Furthermore, in the case of eQTL studies, causal hypotheses generated through single-variant look up are highly likely to be false positives<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>. Consequently, a variety of co-localization methods have been introduced<sup><a href="#ref-22">22</a>–<a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup> to distinguish the two common scenarios: pleiotropy (the same functional genetic variant is associated with both traits) and linkage (two different functional genetic variants, each associated with a different trait, that are in linkage disequilibrium).</p><p class="" id=d27871e374>The aim of this study was to (1) verify whether the same functional variant at 8q24.21 is responsible for BP and LDH and (2) to study the effects of this variation on related phenotypes. As a result, we offer an alternative explanation to the assertion by Bjornsdottir <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup> regarding the role of immunity and inflammation in generating symptoms of sciatica and BP due to variation in expression at the locus in between genes <i>CCDC26</i> and <i>GSDMC</i>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d27871e392>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d27871e395 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>GWAS data</h3><p class="" id=d27871e400>We used the BP GWAS performed in all genetically confirmed white British UK Biobank participants (N= 453,862) as target data for future analysis<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. We used the summary level-data of LDH GWAS from the original paper of deCODE group<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. They do not provide full GWAS scan, only the statistics for the significant SNPs from the 8q24.21 region. We used information about 40 SNPs from this locus for which information about effect size, effective and reference alleles and p-value was available (Supplementary Table 1,3 of the work<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>).</p><p class="" id=d27871e415>We derived summary statistic of 2,419 complex traits provided by the <a target=xrefwindow href="http://www.nealelab.is/" id=d27871e417>Neale Lab</a> and 34 traits from the <a target=xrefwindow href="http://geneatlas.roslin.ed.ac.uk/" id=d27871e420>Gene ATLAS</a> database. Summary statistics for gene expression levels were obtained from Westra Blood eQTL<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup> (peripheral blood, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cnsgenomics.com/software/smr/#eQTLsummarydata, file: westra_eqtl_data_hg19.zip (hg19)" id=d27871e427>http://cnsgenomics.com/software/smr/#eQTLsummarydata, file: westra_eqtl_data_hg19.zip (hg19)</a>) and the GTEx database<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup> (14 tissues<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.gtexportal.org/home/datasets" id=d27871e439>https://www.gtexportal.org/home/datasets</a>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://storage.googleapis.com/gtex_analysis_v6/single_tissue_eqtl_data/GTEx_Analysis_V6_all-snp-gene-associations.tar" id=d27871e442>file: GTEx_Analysis_V6_all-snp-gene-associations.tar</a>).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d27871e447 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>SMR-HEIDI analysis</h3><p class="" id=d27871e452>Summary-level mendelian randomization and heterogeneity in dependent instruments (SMR-HEIDI)<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup> approach was used to test for potential pleiotropic effects of the 8q24.21 region on back pain (BP) and LDH and other complex traits including gene expression levels in certain tissues. SMR analysis provides evidence for pleiotropy, but is unable to define whether both traits are affected by the same underlying causal polymorphism. The latter is specified by a HEIDI test that distinguishes pleiotropy from linkage. Zhang F. and colleagues developed the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cnsgenomics.com/software/smr/#Overview" id=d27871e458>software SMR tool</a> implementing the SMR-HEIDI methods<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d27871e466>SMR tool is suitable to analyze pleiotropic association using summary-level data from GWAS and eQTL studies. We conducted two groups of analysis: target trait vs eQTL and target trait vs other complex traits. Summary-level data from BP GWAS<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup> was used as target (<tt>--gwas-summary</tt> option) in both cases. For eQTL analysis we used Westra Blood eQTL data (converted to suitable BSED format by developers) and GTEx v7, converted using <tt>--make-besd</tt> option (<tt>smr --eqtl-flist Gtex_tissue_1.txt --make-besd --out Gtex_tissue_1</tt>, where <tt>Gtex_tissue_1.txt file</tt> correspond to summary-level data of selected tissue). In order to apply SMR tool for complex traits we selected the region +-250kb around the target SNP from each complex trait GWAS. After that, we add new columns Probe_ID with trait_name to corresponded SNP subset. Finally, we merge these SNP subsets into one file and converted it in BESD format using <tt>--make-besd</tt> option.</p><p class="" id=d27871e489>In both cases we set the minor allele frequency threshold as 0.03 and included in the HEIDI test SNPs inside +-250kb window centered around the target SNP; other parameters used default settings. The LD matrix for the HEIDI test was estimated using <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.cog-genomics.org/plink2" id=d27871e491>PLINK</a> 1.9 software and individual data of white UK Biobank participants, the subset of individuals used to calculate BP GWAS (N=10,000).</p><p class="" id=d27871e495>Nominal P for SMR test was set at 6e-4 (0.05/89, where 89 is the total number of probes for the gene expression analysis) and 2e-5 (0.05/2,453, where 2,453 is the total number of complex traits used in analysis). For HEIDI analysis we used a conservative threshold of P = 0.05 (P &lt; 0.05 corresponds to the rejection of pleiotropy hypothesis).</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d27871e502>Results and discussion</h2><p class="" id=d27871e505>We had previously performed the largest-to-date GWAS for chronic BP using the UK Biobank and CHARGE consortium cohorts, with total of 119384 cases and 334478 controls<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. Cases were defined as those having BP of duration &gt;3-6 months; controls included those without back pain or with back pain of &lt;3-6 months. We found variants at 8q41.21 locus that showed a genome-wide significant association (OR=1.05, p = 4.4 × 10<sup>-13</sup>, after genomic control correction)<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. The location chr8:130,717,716 is tagged by intergenic variant rs10956487 which is in perfect LD with rs6651255 (r<sup>2</sup>=1 according to 1000 Genomes EUR samples), the lead SNP in the Icelandic study of LDH by Bjornsdottir <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. The strongest association signal for BP is situated near <i>GSDMC</i> gene; yet another protein-coding gene, <i>FAM49B</i>, is located within a 500 kbp window. The summary of the previously reported results and our findings for locus 8q24.21 are shown in <a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>.</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/25092/71b09290-ab5e-4985-b664-15c83ea7f815_figure1.gif"><img alt="71b09290-ab5e-4985-b664-15c83ea7f815_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/25092/71b09290-ab5e-4985-b664-15c83ea7f815_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. Pleiotropic effects of locus 8q24.21 on BP and related traits.</h3><p id=d27871e548>The BP-related traits and genes analyzed in our work are blue. The locus 8q24.21 is connected to associated traits (p-value &lt; 5*10<sup>-8</sup>) by green arrows. Possible relations between traits, as inferred from previous studies, are marked by grey arrows. Pairwise pleiotropic effects tested by SMR-HEIDI are denoted by a red cross if the test failed, and by a blue check if the test passed. A new detected pleiotropic effect on expression of <i>FAM49B</i> and BP based on SMR-HEIDI results is denoted by the blue arrow. LDH, lumbar disk herniation; SRDP, self-reported disk problems; HBMD, heel bone mineral density t-score.</p></div></div><p class="" id=d27871e561>We used the SMR-HEIDI approach<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup> to test for potential pleiotropic effects of the 8q24.21 region (rs6651255) on BP and other complex traits including gene expression levels in relevant tissues. SMR analysis provides evidence for pleiotropy, but is unable to determine whether both traits are influenced by the same underlying causal polymorphism. The latter question was addressed by the HEIDI test which distinguishes pleiotropy from linkage. For SMR-HEIDI testing we used as the target trait the BP GWAS previously performed in all genetically confirmed white British UK Biobank participants<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d27871e572>First of all, we checked relationships between BP and LDH. We found strong evidence that co-association of these traits to 8q24.21 is due to the pleiotropic action of the same functional variant (P<sub>SMR</sub>=2.8 × 10<sup>-7</sup> and P<sub>HEIDI</sub> = 0.87; extended data<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>). We further applied SMR-HEIDI to test for pleiotropic effects of the region in the collection of 2,453 traits available from the <a target=xrefwindow href="http://www.nealelab.is/blog/2017/7/19/rapid-gwas-of-thousands-of-phenotypes-for-337000-samples-in-the-uk-biobank" id=d27871e587>UK biobank study</a> and <a target=xrefwindow href="http://geneatlas.roslin.ed.ac.uk/" id=d27871e591>GeneAtlas</a>. We found that the BP risk allele of rs6651255 was negatively associated with heel bone mineral density t-score and positively associated with self-reported prolapsed or slipped disc (both FDR<sub>SMR</sub>&lt;0.01, underlying data<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup> and extended data<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>) and also strongly associated with standing height (underlying data<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup> and extended data<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>) and several measures of fat-free mass (all FDR<sub>SMR</sub>&lt;0.001). However, HEIDI tests showed strong evidence against pleiotropic effects of the same functional variant in this region for BP and these anthropometric traits (all P<sub>HEIDI</sub> &lt; 0.001), while associations with heel bone mineral density t-score and self-reported prolapsed or slipped disc were likely the result of pleiotropy, controlled by the same functional variant (all P<sub>HEIDI</sub> &gt; 0.05). This suggests that genetic variants influencing structural spine features – the vertebral body and intervertebral disc – provide the link to BP symptoms, supporting previous work in twins highlighting shared genetic influence between spine degeneration and BP<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a>,<a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d27871e632>The unanticipated results were obtained in the study of expression data. We have previously<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup> demonstrated that variants in LD with the lead SNP at 8q24.1 were located in potential regulatory regions in chondrocytes and osteoblasts (Roadmap Epigenomics Consortium<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>). In the current study, we tested expression of genes in the 8q24.21 region in all available tissues considered potentially relevant to development of pain (underlying data<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>), including musculoskeletal, brain and nerve tissue (from GTEx,<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>). Given the suggestion by Bjornsdottir <i>et al.</i> that the locus may act through regulation of immune function<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>, we also investigated expression in whole blood using the blood eQTL database<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup> and five immune cell subsets from CEDAR<sup><a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>. Similar to the Bjornsdottir <i>et al.</i> study<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>, we observed that rs6651255 is co-associated with expression of <i>GSDMC</i> in skeletal muscle (FDR<sub>SMR</sub>=0.01, P<sub>SMR</sub>=0.0001, underlying data<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>), with the BP-associated C allele of rs6651255 being associated with increased expression of the gene. However, HEIDI results show this overlap to be unlikely due to pleiotropic action of the same functional variant (P<sub>HEIDI</sub> = 3×10<sup>-7</sup>, underlying data<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup> and extended data<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>). Our findings suggest that <i>GSDMC</i> is not the gene through which the functional variant acts to influence BP. The next strongest co-association was with increased expression of <i>FAM49B</i> in brain anterior cingulate cortex (P<sub>SMR</sub>=0.004, underlying data<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup> and extended data<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>) and decreased expression in cytotoxic T (CD8+) cells (P<sub>SMR</sub>=0.02, underlying data<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup> and extended data<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>), although these associations were only nominally significant (p&lt;0.05), yet not statistically significant after multiple testing correction (FDR<sub>SMR</sub>=0.18 and 0.28, respectively). All corresponding P<sub>HEIDI</sub> were &gt; 0.05, indicating these co-associations are likely due to pleiotropy. The product of <i>FAM49B</i> was found to be expressed in multiple tissues including bone, lymph node and spleen; according to Proteomics DB<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>, it has highest expression in lymphocytes, and was implicated as a key negative regulator of actin dynamics and T-cell activation<sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup>. The precise mechanism of which gene is involved in the development of BP is difficult to infer due to lack of eQTL data available for relevant tissues, such as intervertebral disc.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d27871e753>Conclusion</h2><p class="" id=d27871e756>We hypothesize that the locus 8q24.21 exhibits its influence on BP phenotypes via disk and bone remodeling, and not through influence on anthropometrics or pain processing. Our findings suggest that <i>FAM49B</i> and not <i>GSDMC</i> is the most likely mechanism through which the functional variant leads to BP.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d27871e768>Data availability</h2><div class=section><a name=d27871e771 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Underlying data</h3><p class="" id=d27871e776> Zenodo: Table S1.
