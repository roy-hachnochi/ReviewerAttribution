Here we present the first demonstration of the use of eggshell membrane for research on endangered Maleo (<i>Macrocephalon maleo</i>).&nbsp;<br/> <b>Methods:</b> We used 24 post-hatched eggshell membranes collected from two different sites, Tambun and Tanjung Binerean, in North Sulawesi, 12 samples in each. Two different DNA extraction methods: alkaline lysis method and gSYNC<sup>TM</sup> DNA Extraction Kit<i> </i>were applied. &nbsp;To determine the sex of Maleo, we utilized PCR-based DNA sexing using CHD genes, with the primer set 2550F/2718R.&nbsp;<br/> <b>Results:</b> We successfully extracted all samples; the mean sample concentration was 267.5 ng/&micro;l (range 47510.5 ng/&micro;l) and samples were of high purity (A260/280 ratio 1.85&plusmn;0.03). All samples were used to successfully identified sexes, 9 females and 15 males.&nbsp;<br/> <b>Conclusions:</b> Our research clearly illustrates that eggshell membranes can be used for DNA sexing and open the possibility to build noninvasive DNA collections over large spatial scales for population study of endangered birds. </div> <div class=abstract-for-mobile> <div class="margin-top-30 Oftentimes a necessary prerequisite for answering evolutionary or ecological questions is access to a good DNA sample. Birds blood contains nucleated red blood cells with abundant DNA, making it a preferred source of DNA<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. However, obtaining blood requires the capture of a bird, which can provoke an increased level of stress and might results in unusual behavior or nest desertion. For example, blood sampling has been reported to reduce annual survival in Cliff Swallows (<i>Petrochelidon pyrrhonota</i>)<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>, although the effects of blood collection in free-living adult and immature birds is not thought to have major negative effects on adult survival, reproductive success, body condition, or behavior<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d26737e193>As an alternative to invasive sampling, researchers have adopted noninvasive sampling methods such as DNA capture from molted feathers<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a>,<a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>, feces<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>, and egg shell membrane<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a>,<a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. In addition the application of moderately invasive sampling such as buccal swabs<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a>,<a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup> has also increased.</p><p class="" id=d26737e224>Megapodes (family Megapodidae) are a galliform clade, centered in Australasia<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>, that are known for their unique super-precocial behavior<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. Their ground-living habits, large body size and large egg size make them particularly vulnerable to human persecution, habitat destruction and habitat loss: 11 out of 21 species are now considered endangered or threatened in some form<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. Given this precarious conservation situation, the application of noninvasive DNA sampling techniques is crucial for megapode birds. Yet previous genetic studies on this family have used either blood<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup> or fresh tissue<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d26737e247>Previously, all megapodes were assumed to be monogamous. The mating system is considered to correlate with sexual selection, with sexually dimorphic birds are non-monogamous and monomorphic birds are monogamous. The evolution of non-monogamous systems in birds was believed to be an adaptive solution to an unbalanced sex ratio<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>. The sex-ratio in Maleo (<i>Macrocephalon maleo</i>) is unknown, but based on previous assumptions, it is expected that the Maleo has an evenly balanced sex ratio. Even though Maleo are slightly sexual dimorphic, the available population data only report total population size and never mention sex ratio. A study on the correlation of incubation temperature and sex ratio of chicks has been carried out in the Australian brush-turkey (<i>Alectura lathami</i>), which revealed that at average temperature the hatched chicks in the proportion of 1:1 of male and female chicks<sup><a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d26737e265>The purpose of our study was to determine whether the eggshell membrane of the endangered Maleo, a monotypic genus within the megapodes, could be successfully extracted and amplified for DNA sexing. Adult male and female Maleo are morphologically slightly different, but the chicks are not. To determine the sex in Maleo chicks, vent sexing has been conducted. Base on cloaca size and shape, a one-day-old male Maleo chick cloaca is bigger (3.96  0.11 cm) and rounded, than the female cloaca (3.20  0.10 cm), which is more oval in shape. The concentration of estrogen in female birds was also higher<sup><a href="#ref-23">23</a></sup>. Until recently, no molecular technique has been applied for sex determination of Maleo.</p><p class="" id=d26737e272>Maleo are endemic to Sulawesi, Indonesia<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a>,<a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. The bird is a burrow-nesting megapode that incubates its eggs in communal nesting sites on beaches (coastal nesting grounds) and in soil heated by volcanic activity mostly at inland localities. Due to its small, severely fragmented population and continued rapid decline, the International Union for Conservation of Nature has classified Maleo as an endangered species<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>. Among the major threats are the over-exploitation of eggs and loss of connectivity between forest and nesting grounds<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. To minimize these threats at some nesting grounds, conservation programs are currently removing eggs and hatching them in safer, semi-natural hatcheries, built close to the nesting grounds. These facilities provide an opportunity to collect noninvasive DNA samples from the eggshell membrane left in the soil or brought to the soil surface by the hatched Maleo.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26737e293>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d26737e296 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Study sites and genetic sampling</h3><p class="" id=d26737e301>Post-hatched egg-shell membranes were collected from semi-natural hatcheries of Maleo at two different nesting grounds: an inland geothermal heated nesting ground at Tambun (Bogani Nani Wartabone National Parks and a sun-heated sand beach nesting ground at Tanjung Binerean, North Sulawesi, Indonesia (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>). All samples were collected from 4<sup>th</sup> April until 1<sup>st</sup> May 2018. To prevent post-sampling contamination, each sample was placed separately in a zip-lock plastic bag and stored in silica gel for delivery to laboratory. The samples were stored at -40C until DNA extraction were conducted.</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/26162/2daaa849-36c0-4e7c-b8b4-8f5295f4360a_figure1.gif"><img alt="2daaa849-36c0-4e7c-b8b4-8f5295f4360a_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/26162/2daaa849-36c0-4e7c-b8b4-8f5295f4360a_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. Sampling sites of eggshell membrane of Maleo (<i>Macrochepalon maleo</i>) in North Sulawesi, Indonesia.</h3></div></div></div><div class=section><a name=d26737e331 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>DNA extraction</h3><p class="" id=d26737e336>We used two different DNA extraction methods: the alkaline lysis method and gSYNC DNA Extraction Kit (Genaid). For the alkaline lysis method, we followed the recommended procedure for rapid preparation of mouse tails or nail lysates suitable for amplification using DNA polymerase from hyperthermophilic archaeon <i>Pyrococcus kodakaraensis</i> (KOD FX Neo 1103; TOYOBO Co. Ltd.). The eggshell membrane (2025 mg; mostly with dry allantois blood vessel) was grinded using a micro-pestle in a 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tube; next, 180 L NaOH (50 mM) was added, the suspension mixed thoroughly by vortexing and then incubated at 90C in water-bath for 1029 min. Following this, 20 L Tris-HCl (1 M, pH 8.0) was added and the tube was vortexed thoroughly, then centrifuged at 12,000 RPM for 5 min. Supernatant was removed to new 1.5 mL microtube and store at freezer until used for PCR.</p><p class="" id=d26737e342>Meanwhile the protocol for gSYNC DNA Extraction Kit (Genaid) followed the provided user manual with little modifications. The eggshell membrane (25 mg; mostly with dry allantois blood vessel) was grinded using a micro-pestle in a 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tube; 300 l GST Buffer (Tris, SDS) and 30 l Proteinase K (10 mg/ml) was added to the sample mixture, mixed thoroughly by vortexing and incubated at 60C in water-bath overnight or until the tissue was lysed completely. Next, 200 l GSB Buffer was added to the sample mixture, mixed thoroughly by pulse-vortexing and incubated at 70C for 10 min. After this, 200 l ethanol (100%) was added to the sample mixture, which was mixed thoroughly by pulse-vortexing and brief spinning of the tube to remove drops from the inside of the lid. Next, a GS Column was placed in a Collection Tube and the mixture (including any precipitate) was carefully transferred to the GS Column, which was centrifuged at 14,000 RPM for 1 min then the GS Column was placed in a new Collection Tube. Following this, 400 l W1 Buffer was added to the GS Column and centrifuged at 14,000 RPM for 1 min then flow-through was discarded. Next, 750 l Wash Buffer was added to the GS Column, centrifuged at full speed for 1 min, then the flow-through was discarded, the tube centrifuged at 14,000 RPM for an additional 3 min to dry the column, 50 l of preheated Elution Buffer (pH 7.59.0) added to the membrane of the GS Column. The GS Column was then left to stand for 3 min, following a final centrifugation at full speed for 2 min to elute the DNA.</p><p class="" id=d26737e345>The eluded DNA (1 l) was quantified using NanoVue Plus (Biochrom, Harvard Bioscience, Inc), at A260 nm. The 260/280 nm absorbance ratio was also measured to give an indication of purity of the DNA. Pure DNA has expected ratios of 1.71.9.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d26737e349 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>DNA sexing</h3><p class="" id=d26737e354>To determine the sex of Maleo, we applied PCR based DNA sexing by using CHD genes, with the primer set 2550F/2718R<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. PCR used a 10 l total volume containing template DNA (genomic DNA or lysate), 1.2 l sterile dH<sub>2</sub>O, 5 l 2x PCR buffer KOD FX Neo, 2 l dNTPs (2 mM), (TOYOBO Co. Ltd.), 0.3 l Primer 2550F (10 M; 5'-GTT ACT GAT TCG TCT ACG AGA-3'), and 0.3 l Primer 2718R (10 M; 5'-ATT GAA ATG ATC CAG TGC TTG-3',<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>), and 0.2 U KOD polymerase enzyme. PCR was carried out in a Veriti 96-well thermal cycler (Applied Biosystems). For genomic DNA templates, the following profile was used: 1 cycle at 94C for 2 min followed by 35 cycles of 98C for 10 sec, 53C for 30 sec and 68C for 45 sec;, and a final extension at 68C for 7 minutes. For lysate as DNA template, the PCR profiles was the same for DNA genome, except that it was run for more cycles (40x). We employed eggshell membrane from female domestic chicken as positive control, and tube without sample as negative one.</p><p class="" id=d26737e368>Amplification of CHD Genes were resolved on a 2% agarose gel. Electrophoresis was conducted using TAE (0.5) buffer, stained by ethidium bromide (1%), at 100 V for 30 minutes; and 5 l PCR product was mixed with 1 l loading dye<i>.</i> After finish, the gel was visualized and analyzed on Gel Logic 200 Imaging System and Kodak Molecular Imaging Software. To confirm that the amplified fragments were the CHD genes, the PCR products of one male and one female sample, respectively, were sequenced. The gels were cut on upper and lower bands for female samples and the single band for male sample, then purified for sequencing. The sequence reactions were carried for both direction in sequencing services laboratory provided by 1<sup>st</sup> BASE Laboratories (Apical Scientific Sdn Bhd, Malaysia). The sequences were check and edited manually on Bioedit version 7.0.5.3 (Hall, 1999) and Chromas versi 2.6.5 (Technelysium Pty Ltd). Sequence similarity was probed using <a target=xrefwindow href="https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Blast.cgi" id=d26737e376>NCBI BLAST</a> (Zhang <i>et al.</i>, 2000).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d26737e384 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Statistical analysis</h3><p class="" id=d26737e389>Mann-Whitney U-test (at a significance level of 95%) was performed to assess the DNA concentration differences which were collected on Tanjung Binerean and Tambun. Analysis was performed using SPSS v17.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26737e396>Results</h2><p class="" id=d26737e399>All eggshell membranes were successfully extracted, with mean DNA concentrations around 267.5 ng/l (range 47510.5 ng/l). The average DNA concentration extracted from eggshell membrane collected from coastal nesting grounds (Tanjung Binerean: 213179 ng/l,) was significantly lesser than of that of inland nesting grounds (Tambun: 322153 ng/l, p=0.004; Data Supp.1). These results demonstrate that all samples were adequate for further PCR based analysis.</p><p class="" id=d26737e402>The 260/280 nm absorbance ratio of all samples ranged from 1.81 to 1.89, with an average of 1.85 (0.03). Meanwhile the average for Tambun and Tanjung Binerean samples were, respectively, 1.85 (0.03) and 1.84 (0.01); <a href="#T1">Table 1</a>). This result suggested good purity of DNA extracted from eggshell samples. class="" id=d26737e1418>This study has demonstrated the first successful DNA isolation from eggshell membranes of a megapode bird. Our success rate (100%) compares favorably to that of previous avian eggshell membrane studies of Black-tailed Godwits (<i>Limosa limosa</i>), which also successfully extracted DNA from all 47 eggshell membranes<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. <b></b>The freshness of the samples might be one of the determinant factors of DNA extraction success. Our samples were relatively fresh, extracted 515 days after collection and kept in the freezers until extraction, with no concomitant extraction failure and high purity (1.85 0.03) and concentration (267.5 ng/l) of DNA.</p><p class="" id=d26737e1430>PCR amplification of CHD genes succeeded in 96% of the eggshell membrane samples, with only one eggshell membrane isolate out of 24 failing to amplify. However, using lysate as DNA template for PCR resulted in 100% amplifications across 24 samples. This result show that eggshell membrane isolates yielded DNA with little amplification problems. Compared to blood DNA isolates, eggshell membrane DNA isolates of Black-tailed Godwits (<i>Limosa limosa</i>) also yielded fewer amplification problems<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. One eggshell membrane DNA isolate out of 21 and 3 samples of Black-tailed Godwit (<i>Limosa limosa</i>) did not amplify for 2 and 5 of the 11 microsatellite loci. The amplification success rates was 99.1%<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. Meanwhile the success rate of eggshell membrane of Sage Grouse (<i>Centrocercus uropihasianus</i>) for DNA sexing was only 55.6%<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d26737e1455>This study demonstrates that hatched eggshell membrane provides useful noninvasive DNA material as an alternative to invasive sampling in sex determination studies of Maleo. Information of the sex of the hatched eggs are important to understand demographic issues, such as the demographic consequences of offspring sex ratio bias or whether there is any sex-specific mortality or dispersal. Furthermore, this information is very important for translocation programs of endangered species, including Maleo.</p><p class="" id=d26737e1458>This study provides additional evidence that noninvasive DNA samples yield reliable results and eliminating the need for capture and invasive sampling. Collection of post-hatched eggshell membrane of Maleo, and other megapodes does not require specific skills. This noninvasive DNA sampling also open the possibility to build participation of local community or local conservation area staff on DNA collections over large spatial scales. Furthermore, the collected samples provide sufficient samples required for population and other ecological and evolutionary study of endangered bird species.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26737e1464>Data availability</h2><div class=section><a name=d26737e1467 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Underlying data</h3><p class="" id=d26737e1472>NCBI GenBank: Macrocephalon maleo isolate MT10-FTB_UAJY_01 chromo-helicase DNA binding protein (CHDZ) gene, partial cds.
