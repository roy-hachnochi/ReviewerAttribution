            <meta name="dc.description" content=" Background: Green micro-alga, Chlamydomonas reinhardtii (a Chlorophyte), can be cultured in the laboratory heterotrophically or photo-heterotrophically in  T ris- P hosphate- A cetate (TAP) medium, which contains acetate as the carbon source. Chlamydomonas can convert acetate in the TAP medium to glucose via the glyoxylate cycle, a pathway present in many microbes and higher plants. A novel bacterial strain, CC4533, was isolated from a contaminated TAP agar medium culture plate of a Chlamydomonas wild type strain. In this article, we present our research on the isolation, and biochemical and molecular characterizations of CC4533. 
 Methods: We conducted several microbiological tests and spectrophotometric analyses to biochemically characterize CC4533. The 16S rRNA gene of CC4533 was partially sequenced for taxonomic identification. We monitored the growth of CC4533 on Tris-Phosphate (TP) agar medium (lacks a carbon source) containing different sugars, aromatic compounds and saturated hydrocarbons, to see if CC4533 can use these chemicals as the sole source of carbon. 
 Results: CC4533 is a Gram-negative, non-enteric yellow pigmented, aerobic, mesophilic bacillus. It is alpha-hemolytic and oxidase-positive. CC4533 can ferment glucose, sucrose and lactose, is starch hydrolysis-negative, resistant to penicillin, polymyxin B and chloramphenicol. CC4533 is sensitive to neomycin. Preliminary spectrophotometric analyses indicate that CC4533 produces b-carotenes. NCBI-BLAST analyses of the partial 16S rRNA gene sequence of CC4533 show 99.55% DNA sequence identity to that of Sphingobium yanoikuyae strain PR86 and S. yanoikuyae strain NRB095. CC4533 can use cyclo-chloroalkanes, saturated hydrocarbons present in car motor oil, polyhydroxyalkanoate, and mono- and poly-cyclic aromatic compounds, as sole carbon sources for growth. 
 Conclusions: Taxonomically, CC4533 is very closely related to the alpha-proteobacterium S. yanoikuyae , whose genome has been sequenced. Future research is needed to probe the potential of CC4533 for environmental bioremediation. Whole genome sequencing of CC4533 will confirm if it is a novel strain of S. yanoikuyae or a new Sphingobium species.">

id=anchor-abstract> <span class="f1r-article-mobile-inline abstract-heading-border"></span> Abstract </h2> <div class="article-abstract article-page-general-text-mobile research-layout"> <div class="abstract-text is-expanded"> <b>Background:</b> Green micro-alga, <i>Chlamydomonas reinhardtii</i> (a Chlorophyte), can be cultured in the laboratory heterotrophically or photo-heterotrophically in <b><u>T</u></b>ris-<b><u>P</u></b>hosphate-<b><u>A</u></b>cetate (TAP) medium, which contains acetate as the carbon source. <i>Chlamydomonas</i> can convert acetate in the TAP medium to glucose via the glyoxylate cycle, a pathway present in many microbes and higher plants. A novel bacterial strain, CC4533, was isolated from a contaminated TAP agar medium culture plate of a<i> Chlamydomonas </i>wild type strain. In this article, we present our research on the isolation, and biochemical and molecular characterizations of CC4533.<br/> <b>Methods:</b> We conducted several microbiological tests and spectrophotometric analyses to biochemically characterize CC4533. The 16S rRNA gene of CC4533 was partially sequenced for taxonomic identification. We monitored the growth of CC4533 on Tris-Phosphate (TP) agar medium (lacks a carbon source) containing different sugars, aromatic compounds and saturated hydrocarbons, to see if CC4533 can use these chemicals as the sole source of carbon.<br/> <b>Results:</b> CC4533 is a Gram-negative, non-enteric yellow pigmented, aerobic, mesophilic bacillus. It is alpha-hemolytic and oxidase-positive. CC4533 can ferment glucose, sucrose and lactose, is starch hydrolysis-negative, resistant to penicillin, polymyxin B and chloramphenicol. CC4533 is sensitive to neomycin. Preliminary spectrophotometric analyses indicate that CC4533 produces b-carotenes. NCBI-BLAST analyses of the partial 16S rRNA gene sequence of CC4533 show 99.55% DNA sequence identity to that of <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae </i>strain PR86 and <i>S. yanoikuyae </i>strain NRB095. CC4533 can use cyclo-chloroalkanes, saturated hydrocarbons present in car motor oil, polyhydroxyalkanoate, and mono- and poly-cyclic aromatic compounds, as sole carbon sources for growth.<br/> <b>Conclusions:</b> Taxonomically, CC4533 is very closely related to the alpha-proteobacterium <i>S. yanoikuyae</i>, whose genome has been sequenced. Future research is needed to probe the potential of CC4533 for environmental bioremediation. Whole genome sequencing of CC4533 will confirm if it is a novel strain of <i>S. yanoikuyae </i>or a new<i> Sphingobium</i> species. </div> <div class=abstract-for-mobile> <div class="margin-top-30 The genus <i>Sphingomonas</i> was first described by Yabuuchi <i>et al.</i> (1990) as a strictly aerobic, chemoheterotrophic, yellow-pigmented, Gram-negative, rod-shaped bacterium containing glycosphingolipids in cell-envelope<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>. Takeuchi <i>et al.</i> (1994) described four phylogenetic clusters in the genus based on 16S rRNA gene sequence data, and later, combined phylogenetic, chemotaxonomic and physiological analyses to split the genus into four genera: <i>Sphingomonas</i>, <i>Sphingobium</i>, <i>Novosphingobium</i> and <i>Sphingopyxis</i><sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. Recently, the genus <i>Sphingosinicella</i> has been added to the family Sphingomonadaceae<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. The type species of the ever-expanding genus <i>Sphingobium</i> is <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> and species with valid published names are <i>S. amiense</i>, <i>S. aromaticiconvertens</i>, <i>S. chlorophenolicum</i>, <i>S. chungbukense</i>, <i>S. cloacae</i>, <i>S. francense</i>, <i>S. fuliginis, S. herbicidovorans</i>, <i>S. indicum</i>, <i>S. japonicum</i>, <i>S. olei</i>, <i>S. xenophagum</i>, <i>S. yanoikuyae</i>, <i>S. ummariense</i> and <i>S. rhizovicinum</i><sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a>–<a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d22566e404>In our research laboratory, we employ the green micro-alga <i>Chlamydomonas reinhardtii,</i> a member of the family Chlorophyceae, as a model system to study oxygenic photosynthesis. <i>C. reinhardtii</i> is grown at the laboratory in the presence (photo-heterotrophically) or absence of light (heterotrophically) in the acetate-containing <b><span style="text-decoration: underline">T</span></b>ris-<b><span style="text-decoration: underline">P</span></b>hosphate-<b><span style="text-decoration: underline">A</span></b>cetate (TAP) medium<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. TAP contains 0.1% acetate as the sole carbon source<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. The acetate in TAP medium is used by <i>Chlamydomonas</i> for net biosynthesis of glucose via the glyoxylate/C2 cycle<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. This allows <i>Chlamydomonas</i> to make sugar heterotrophically in the dark or photo-heterotrophically in the light, without being completely dependent on photosynthesis for glucose biosynthesis<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. Many bacteria possess glyoxylate cycle and can utilize acetate as a carbon source, like <i>Chlamydomonas</i><sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. Hence, we often encounter bacterial contamination on <i>Chlamydomonas</i> TAP-agar medium culture plates. We observed a yellow-pigmented bacterial contamination on the TAP-agar medium culture plate of the <i>Chlamydomonas</i> wild type strain, CC4533. This bacterium was able to grow on the TAP-agar medium because it was able to use acetate as the carbon source. We named this bacterium as CC4533 because it contaminated the <i>Chlamydomonas</i> wild type strain CC4533. A team of four undergraduate research students from the University of West Georgia and, one high school student from the Carrollton High School in Georgia (USA) were assigned a research project to test four antibiotics that were available in our laboratory to determine which one of these four antibiotics would be suitable for use to minimize <i>Chlamydomonas</i> contamination by CC4533. After the antibiotic-sensitivity of CC4533 was determined, we performed additional microbiological and molecular experiments to better characterize the bacterial strain, CC4533. </p><p class="" id=d22566e470>We employed a modified version of the Kirby-Bauer (KB) disc diffusion antibiotic susceptibility test to determine antibiotic sensitivity of CC4533. Two different doses of four antibiotics (penicillin, neomycin, chloramphenicol and polymyxin B) were tested in the KB experiment. The results from the KB test show that CC4533 is resistant to penicillin and chloramphenicol but is sensitive to neomycin. Although CC4533 is more sensitive to polymyxin B at higher dose, polymyxin B cannot be used for controlling <i>Chlamydomonas</i> contamination, as at higher dose Chlamydomonas growth is affected. We found that 50 µg/mL of neomycin in TAP medium was potent for eradicating CC4533 contamination on <i>Chlamydomonas</i> media plates in our research laboratory, without hindering <i>Chlamydomonas</i> growth.</p><p class="" id=d22566e482>We performed standard microbiological tests to biochemically characterize CC4533. These tests show that CC4533 is an aerobic, non-enteric, Gram-negative long rod shaped bacterium (bacillus). CC4533 does not grow on Mannitol Salt Agar and MacConkey agar. CC4533 is starch hydrolysis-negative, alpha-hemolytic and, is cytochrome c oxidase-positive. CC4533 has a bright yellow pigmentation on <b><span style="text-decoration: underline">L</span></b>ysogeny <b><span style="text-decoration: underline">B</span></b>roth (LB) agar and a pale yellow pigmentation on TAP agar. CC4533 grows best at temperatures ranging from 22°C-30°C. CC4533 cannot grow at 37°C on LB agar or on TAP agar medium. Preliminary spectrophotometric analyses of extracted yellow pigment of CC4533 strongly indicate that CC4533 produces β-carotenes. Preliminary results indicate growth medium type (LB Vs. TAP) and, temperature affects β-carotene production in CC4533.</p><p class="" id=d22566e494>0.1% acetate in the TAP medium can be substituted with alternative carbon sources (e.g. different sugars) to test if these alternative carbon sources can be used by bacteria for energy production. We monitored the ability of CC4533 to grow on <b><span style="text-decoration: underline">T</span></b>ris-<b><span style="text-decoration: underline">P</span></b>hosphate (TP) agar medium (lacks acetate) containing monosaccharide (glucose) and two disaccharides (sucrose and lactose). CC4533 was able to use these three sugars as the sole carbon source and was able to ferment these sugars to produce acid.</p><p class="" id=d22566e505>To determine the taxonomic identity of CC4533, we amplified the 16S rRNA gene partially by DNA polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR). We have submitted this partial 16S rRNA gene sequence to the NCBI GenBank in November 2019, with the definition: <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain PR86 variant; 16S ribosomal RNA gene, partial sequence (Accession number: MN633285.1). Our definition was based on the nearest relative identified by the NCBI-nucleotide BLAST analyses of the partial 16S rRNA gene sequence of CC4533 in 2019, which was <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain PR86 (Accession number: MN232173.1). In April 2020, we found another close relative of CC4533 in the NCBI database based on the partial 16S rRNA gene sequence, which was not detected in our earlier BLAST analyses in 2019. This new relative is <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain NRB095 (Accession number: MK543001.1). 16S rRNA partial gene sequences of these two <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strains have identical: score, percentage of sequence identity, E-values and nucleotide changes in the C4 region at identical locations in the 16S rRNA gene, relative to that of CC4533.</p><p class="" id=d22566e520><i>Sphingomonas sp.</i> and <i>Sphingobium sp.</i> are of particular interest due to their abilities to degrade cycloalkanes and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) and polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA) and their roles in environmental bioremediation<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a>–<a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup>. Hence, we monitored the growth of CC4533 on TP-agar medium plates containing common biohazardous chemicals that are found in our natural environment. We tested the following chemicals: saturated hydrocarbons, cyclohexyl chloride, aromatic acid like benzoate, aromatic ester like phenyl acetate, polyesters (polyhydroxybutyrate, a biodegradable plastic) and poly-cyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. Growth analyses revealed that CC4533 is capable of utilizing these toxic organic compounds as the sole carbon and energy source. In future, we plan to sequence the whole genome of CC4533. This will provide important insights into the metabolic diversity of CC4533 and, will reveal the genes that are recruited by this bacterium to generate biochemical pathways for degradation of xenobiotics. In this article, we present our student-driven research on the isolation of CC4533 (<i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain PR86 variant) and its characterizations at the biochemical and molecular level.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d22566e541>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d22566e544 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Growth media and cultures</h3><p class="" id=d22566e549><i>Chlamydomonas</i> wild type strain 4A+ (CC- 4051 4A+ mt+) was maintained in the lab on TAP agar medium in dim light intensities (15-20 µmol m<sup>-2</sup>s<sup>-1</sup>) at 22°C (room temperature). Standard TAP medium recipe can be found at the website of <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.chlamycollection.org/methods/media-recipes/tap-and-tris-minimal/" id=d22566e559>Chlamydomonas Resource Center</a>. Hutner’s trace element solution is an ingredient in the TAP medium. Hutner’s trace element recipe can also be found on the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.chlamycollection.org/methods/media-recipes/hutners-trace-elements/" id=d22566e562>Chlamydomonas Resource Center</a> website. Our lab’s TAP medium recipe is slightly different from the standard one and can be found at <a target=xrefwindow href="https://doi.org/10.17504/protocols.io.bgzujx6w" id=d22566e565>https://doi.org/10.17504/protocols.io.bgzujx6w</a><sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>. Liquid 4A+ cultures were grown in liquid TAP medium under low light (50-60 µmol m<sup>-2</sup>s<sup>-1</sup>) for 3 days on a New Brunswick Scientific Excella E5 platform shaker (Enfield, CT) at 150 rpm for aeration. CC4533 bacterial strain stock was maintained in the lab under dim light (15-20 µmol m<sup>-2</sup>s<sup>-1</sup>) at 22°C on either TAP or LB agar medium. Liquid cultures of CC4533 were grown in culture tubes in 3 mL of TAP medium at 22°C on a MaxQ420HP incubator shaker (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA) at 200 rpm for aeration. Light intensities were measured using a LI-250A light meter (LI-COR, Inc., Lincoln, NE). </p></div><div class=section><a name=d22566e586 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Imaging</h3><p class="" id=d22566e591>Images of all media plates used in the experiments were imaged with a Samsung Galaxy S5 cell phone camera. Image cropping and adjustments were made using Photos app in Windows 10. DNA gels were visualized and imaged with a Bio-Rad Molecular Imager Gel Doc XR+ (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d22566e595 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Antibiotic susceptibility test using the modified disc diffusion method</h3><p class="" id=d22566e600>Antibiotics that were tested are: penicillin; neomycin, chloramphenicol and polymyxin B. Two doses (50 µg and 100 µg) of each of these four antibiotics were tested in the modified <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.asm.org/Protocols/Kirby-Bauer-Disk-Diffusion-Susceptibility-Test-Pro" id=d22566e602>Kirby-Bauer (KB) disc diffusion antibiotic susceptibility tests</a>. KB tests were performed on TAP-agar plates as described in Mitra <i>et al.</i> 2020<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a>–<a href="#ref-43">43</a></sup>. Antibiotic plates were incubated at 22°C. CC4533 plates were imaged after 3 days of incubation and <i>Chlamydomonas</i> plates were imaged after 4 days of incubation. Diameters of zone of inhibitions were measured using a ruler. Statistical analyses<sup><a href="#ref-44">44</a></sup> and images of all antibiotic plates<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup> are available as <i>Underlying data</i>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d22566e632 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Data analyses</h3><p class="" id=d22566e637>Standard deviations and means of the zones of inhibitions from the KB test were calculated using Microsoft Excel. Statistical analyses of the data from the KB disc diffusion test were performed using Microsoft Excels’ t-Test: Paired Two Sample for Means tool in the analysis ToolPak.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d22566e642 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Standard microbiological tests</h3><p class="" id=d22566e647>1) <i>Growth at different temperatures</i>: CC4533 was streaked on fresh LB and TAP agar medium. Streaked CC4533 media plates were incubated at different temperatures: a) 22°C, b) 30°C, and c) 37°C, for 5 days and then imaged.</p><p class="" id=d22566e653>2) <i>Growth assays on MacConkey agar (MAC) and Mannitol Salt Agar (MSA)</i>: CC4533 was streaked on MAC and MSA plates purchased from Carolina Biological (Burlington, NC). It can ferment glucose, sucrose and lactose (<a href="#f5">Figure 5</a>). It is a non-enteric, salt-sensitive, alpha hemolytic, starch hydrolysis-negative, oxidase-positive bacterium (<a href="#f6">Figure 6</a>–<a href="#f10">Figure 10</a>). NCBI-nucleotide BLAST analyses of the partial 16S rRNA gene sequence of CC4533 revealed that the best match is to that of <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain PR86 (GenBank Accession #: MN232173.1) and to that of <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain NRB095 (Accession number: MK543001.1) with a sequence identity of 99.55% and zero E-value, indicating strongly that CC4533 is a new strain of <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae.</i> Whole genome sequencing can confirm if CC4533 is a new species of <i>Sphingobium</i> or a new strain of <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> (see discussion end).</p><p class="" id=d22566e2419>The family Sphingomonadaceae currently includes the genera <i>Sphingomonas</i>, <i>Sandaracinobacter</i>, <i>Blastomonas</i>, <i>Novosphingobium</i>, <i>Sphingobium</i>, <i>Sphingopyxis</i>, <i>Sandarakinorhabdus</i>, <i>Sphingosinicella</i>, <i>Stakelama</i>, <i>Sphingomicrobium</i>, <i>Sphingorhabdus</i>, <i>Parasphingopyxis</i>, and <i>Zymomonas</i><sup><a href="#ref-60">60</a></sup>. The type genus <i>Sphingomonas</i> was dissected into four genera based on clustering in the 16S rRNA gene sequence phylogeny and differences in the major polyamine and the 2-hydroxy fatty acid patterns: the genus <i>Sphingomonas</i> sensu stricto and the three new genera <i>Novosphingobium</i>, <i>Sphingobium</i>, and <i>Sphingopyxis</i><sup><a href="#ref-60">60</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2485><i>Sphingobium sp</i>. are chemoorganotrophs and have been isolated from soils, freshwater and marine habitats, activated sludge, phyllosphere and rhizosphere. The isolation source for <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain PR86 (GenBank Accession #: MN232173.1) is sunflower endo-phyllosphere and that for <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain NRB095 (Accession number: MK543001.1) is surface disinfested root of the tropical Koronivia grass <i>Brachiaria humidicola</i>. There are various examples of plant growth-promoting organisms within the Sphingomonadaceae family but the genus <i>Sphingobium</i> is largely limited to members that degrade xenobiotic compounds<sup><a href="#ref-61">61</a>–<a href="#ref-65">65</a></sup>. Exception is the <i>Sphingobium sp.</i> strain AEW4, isolated from the rhizosphere of the beach grass, <i>Ammophila breviligulata</i><sup><a href="#ref-61">61</a></sup>. This strain has plant growth promoting properties via production of siderophores and indole-3-acetic acid and induces root growth<sup><a href="#ref-61">61</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium paulinellae sp.</i> nov. and <i>Sphingobium algicola sp.</i> nov are isolated from <i>Paulinella chromatophora</i>, a freshwater filose amoeba with photosynthetic endosymbionts (chromatophores) of cyanobacterial origin<sup><a href="#ref-66">66</a></sup>. In nature, <i>C. reinhardtii</i> is predominantly found in temperate, nutrient-rich, cultivated field soils in Northern America and Japan<sup><a href="#ref-67">67</a>,<a href="#ref-68">68</a></sup>. We isolated CC4533 (<i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain PR86 variant) in our laboratory from TAP agar medium plates of the micro-green-alga <i>C. reinhardtii</i>.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2553>CC4533 is resistant to the β-lactam group of drugs like penicillin, and to the broad-spectrum antibiotic, chloramphenicol (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>). It is resistant to both 50 µg and 100 µg doses of the cationic antimicrobial polypeptide, polymyxin B, which is one of the most effective drugs against Gram-negative bacteria<sup><a href="#ref-69">69</a></sup> (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>; <a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>, Underlying data<sup><a href="#ref-44">44</a>,<a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup>). CC4533 is sensitive to neomycin (<i>Underlying data</i><sup><a href="#ref-44">44</a>,<a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup>; <a href="#T1">Table 1</a>; <a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>). The diversity and antibiotic resistance patterns of Sphingomonadaceae isolates from drinking water show that the highest antibiotic resistance prevalence values were in members of the genera <i>Sphingomonas</i> and <i>Sphingobium</i>, especially in tap water and in water from dental chairs<sup><a href="#ref-70">70</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium</i> isolates in drinking water are 89.3-100% penicillin-resistant, 96.4% polymyxin B-resistant, 25% sulfonamide-resistant and 100% neomycin-sensitive<sup><a href="#ref-70">70</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2611>Many <i>Sphingobium</i> strains capable of degrading PAHs and other aromatic compounds have been isolated<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a>–<a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain B1, previously known as <i>Sphingomonas yanoikuyae</i> strain B1 and <i>Beijerinckia sp.</i> strain B1, has caught attention due to its versatile capabilities to degrade various environmental pollutants, such as biphenyl, naphthalene, phenanthrene, toluene, m-, p-xylene, anthracene<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a>–<a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium</i> sp. 22B isolated from soil contaminated with PAHs from Argentina has great capacity of degrading PAHs as unique sources of carbon in mineral medium and in phenanthrene microcosms assays<sup><a href="#ref-23">23</a>,<a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>. Biphenyls and polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) are difficult to be completely mineralized by environmental microbes because of the accumulation of dead-end intermediates like benzoate and chlorobenzoates during biphenyl and PCB biodegradation. <i>Sphingobium fuliginis</i> HC3 strain isolated from PCBs-contaminated soil can degrade biphenyl and PCBs without dead-end intermediates accumulation<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2658><i>Sphingobium aromaticiconvertens</i> sp. nov. isolated from polluted river sediment can mineralize monochlorinated dibenzofurans<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium xenophagum</i> Bayram can utilize branched 4-nonylphenol as a sole carbon source<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain FM-2 isolated from river water is capable of degrading Bisphenol F<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. Three strains of <i>Sphingobium sp.</i> strain YBL1, strain YBL2 and strain YBL3 isolated from China were found to mineralize commonly used N, N-dimethyl-substituted phenylurea herbicides<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a>,<a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium wenxiniae</i> JZ-1 has a gene cluster that codes for phenoxybenzoic acid 1′,2′-dioxygenase, which allows this species to metabolize 3-phenoxybenzoic acid<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium scionense sp</i>. nov is an aromatic hydrocarbon-degrading bacterium isolated from a PAH-contaminated soil in New Zealand<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> XLDN2-5 isolated from petroleum-contaminated soils can degrade carbazole efficiently<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a>–<a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. This strain could also co-metabolically catabolize dibenzofuran, dibenzothiophene, and benzothiophene<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a href="#ref-34">34</a>,<a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> SHJ is a strain isolated from shallow aquifer sediment in China that can degrade diethyl phthalate, which is used as a plasticizer<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2734>Synthesis and accumulation of PHB is widespread in bacteria and depends on either the type of strain or the carbon source used in the metabolism<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a>,<a href="#ref-39">39</a></sup>. PHB is important for the physiology of the PAH-degrading strain <i>Sphingobium sp</i>. 22B, isolated from semi-arid region of Patagonia (South America) as it accumulates significant amounts of this polyhydroxyalkanoate<sup><a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup>. PHB has been shown to play diverse roles under different degrees of water stress: it can serve as an endogenous carbon source under low water stress and can protect cells under high water stress<sup><a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2755>We have shown in this work that CC4533 (<i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain PR86 variant) can utilize PAH, chlorinated alkanes, petroleum derivatives in 10W30 car motor oil, PHB and benzoic acids and phenyl acetate as sole carbon source in TP medium (<a href="#f14">Figure 14</a>–<a href="#f17">Figure 17</a>). Recently we isolated and characterized a novel bacterial strain, LMJ/Bacterium strain clone LIB091_C05_1243 variant 16S ribosomal RNA gene, partial sequence (Accession number: MN633292.1)<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>. The nearest relative of LMJ with a genus name is <i>Acidovorax sp.</i> strain A16OP12 (Accession #: MN519578.1)<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>. LMJ can grow on TP-medium containing saturated hydrocarbons, PHB and PAH like CC4533<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>. LMJ hardly grows on TP medium containing phenyl acetate and benzoate unlike CC4533<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>. CC4533 strain grows more robustly than LMJ on every tested toxic aromatic compound and hydrocarbon containing plates at all doses<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>. Hence, CC4533 displays a higher potential for environmental bioremediation than the bacterial strain LMJ strain<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2795>In future we plan to optimize the process of uniformly coating the TP medium surface with chemicals as we have noticed that the tested hydrophobic chemicals were unevenly deposited after coating, when the solvent chloroform evaporates. We think that this uneven coating can affect proper utilization of these aromatic chemicals by CC4533. Our TP medium is a nutritionally stringent minimal medium compared to the traditional <a target=xrefwindow href="http://www.thelabrat.com/protocols/m9minimal.shtml" id=d22566e2797>M9 minimal medium</a> used for bacterial growth<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>. The final concentrations of phosphate, nitrogen, magnesium and carbon in the M9 medium is approximately, 70-fold, 2.5-fold, 4-fold and 5-fold higher than that present in our lab’s TAP medium, respectively<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>. We would like to test if CC4533 can grow on the M9 medium without an added carbon source like a sugar or acetic acid. If CC4533 fails to grow on the M9 medium plates without a carbon source, we will compare the growth of CC4533 in M9 medium containing different PAHs or other aromatic compounds as alternative carbon sources against that in TP medium. Additionally, we need to collaborate with a research lab that can test the concentration of these toxic organic chemicals in TP medium before and after the CC4533 growth, to get additional evidence that CC4533 is degrading PAH, PHB and other hydrocarbons in the TP medium.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2809>Bacterial heterocyclic aromatic compound degradation pathways mainly involve the oxidation reactions such as angular dioxygenation of carbazole and dibenzofuran, lateral dioxygenation of dibenzothiophene in Kodama pathway, and S-oxidation of dibenzothiophene in 4S pathway<sup><a href="#ref-34">34</a>,<a href="#ref-71">71</a>,<a href="#ref-72">72</a></sup>. A consequence of the oxidation reaction is the formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which can damage DNA, proteins, and membranes<sup><a href="#ref-73">73</a>,<a href="#ref-74">74</a></sup>. Carotenoids present in a wide variety of bacteria, algae, fungi, and plants are the most prominent membrane-integrated antioxidants<sup><a href="#ref-75">75</a></sup>. <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> XLDN2-5, a yellow pigmented PAH-degrading strain, synthesizes zeaxanthin from β-carotene through β-cryptoxanthin via the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway<sup><a href="#ref-49">49</a></sup>. In <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> XLDN2-5, there is a direct correlation between the increase in the amount of zeaxanthin and the enhancement of hydrogen peroxide production during the biodegradation of heterocyclic aromatic compounds<sup><a href="#ref-49">49</a></sup>. High levels of carotenoids in this <i>Sphingobium</i> strain were consistent with the enhanced transcription of the gene encoding phytoene desaturase, one of the key enzymes for carotenoid biosynthesis<sup><a href="#ref-49">49</a></sup>. </p><p class="" id=d22566e2856>Pigment analyses of CC4533 strongly indicate the presence of β-carotene in the CC4533 pigment extract (<a href="#f11">Figure 11</a>; <a href="#f12">Figure 12</a>). We plan to confirm our results by conducting HPLC analyses of the CC4533 pigment extract and a commercial pure sample of β-carotene. Carotenoid production can be measured in CC4533 grown on LB medium that contain the ROS species like hydrogen peroxide or contains photo-sensitizers like Rose Bengal that generates the ROS, singlet oxygen, in the presence of light and oxygen<sup><a href="#ref-76">76</a></sup>. Bleaching herbicides, such as norflurazon, interferes with the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway<sup><a href="#ref-77">77</a></sup>. Norflurazon blocks the enzyme Phytoene desaturase (PDS), which converts phytoene (colorless carotene) to red-colored lycopene<sup><a href="#ref-78">78</a></sup>. ROS sensitivity of CC4533 can be tested in the presence and absence of the inhibitor of PDS. Carotenoids are used as regulators for membrane fluidity by <i>Staphylococcus xylosus</i><sup><a href="#ref-79">79</a></sup>. For two <i>Staphylococcus xylosus</i> strains there was an increase in staphyloxanthin and other carotenoids when grown at 10°C but no carotenoids could be detected when grown at 30°C<sup><a href="#ref-79">79</a></sup>. CC4533 cannot grow at 37°C and appears lot less yellow-pigmented at 30°C compared to when it is grown at 22°C (<a href="#f4">Figure 4</a>). Pigment reduction is more pronounced in CC4533 on TAP medium than on LB (<a href="#f4">Figure 4</a>). Quantitative carotenoid assays at different temperatures can be performed to study the temperature effect on pigment production.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2897>Partial 16S rRNA gene sequence of CC4533 shows two transitional nucleotide changes when compared to that of the <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain PR86 (GenBank Accession #: MN232173.1) and to that of <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain NRB095 (Accession number: MK543001.1). These two nucleotide substitutions are in the 11 bp invariable sub-region (788 bp -798 bp) within C4 region (<a href="#f13">Figure 13C</a>). These results show that conserved regions of the 16S rRNA are not truly “conserved”. Conserved regions of the 16S rRNA gene exhibit considerable variations that need to be considered when using this gene as a biomarker<sup><a href="#ref-80">80</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d22566e2913>Sphingomonads have attracted the attention of microbiologists and biotechnologists due to their biodegradative and biosynthetic capabilities, and have been utilized for a wide range of biotechnological applications from bioremediation of contaminants to production of extracellular polymers<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. We have shown in this study that CC4533 is a <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> strain and has traits that can be exploited for bioremediation upon further research. We found in the NCBI database, 20 genome assemblies of <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> (Representative genome: <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> ATCC 51230; ID: 3110) and 70 genome assemblies of uncharacterized environmental isolates. Because of funding limitations, we could not sequence the whole genome of CC4533 at the time of this manuscript submission. But we will have funds in fall 2020 to sequence the whole genome of CC4533 using the Pacific Biosciences technology. Whole genome sequencing will allow us to: <b>1</b>) confirm if CC4533 is a novel species of <i>Sphingobium</i> or a new strain of <i>Sphingobium yanoikuyae</i> and, <b>2</b>) will reveal genes in CC4533 that are recruited for degradation of xenobiotics and contribute to its metabolic diversity, relevant to environmental bioremediation and industrial biotechnology.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d22566e2945>Data availability</h2><div class=section><a name=d22566e2948 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Underlying data</h3><p class="" id=d22566e2953>Sphingobium yanoikuyae strain PR86 16S ribosomal RNA gene, partial sequence.
