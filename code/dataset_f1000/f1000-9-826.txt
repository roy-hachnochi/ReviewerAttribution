Once participants are sensorially isolated from external visual and auditory stimulation, they are in a favourable condition for producing inner mental contents about a randomly-selected target hidden amongst decoys. The mentation they produce can either be used by the participant to guide his/her target selection, or it can be used to assist in an independent judging process.</p><p class="" id=d43259e160>In the prototypical procedure, participants are tested in a room isolated from external sounds and visual information. After they made themselves comfortable in a reclining armchair, they receive the instructions related their task during the ganzfeld condition. Even if there are different verbatim versions, the instructions describe what they should do mentally in order to detect the information related to the target and how to filter out the mental contents not related to it. This information will be described aloud and recorded for playback before or during the target identification phase. After the relaxation phase, they are exposed to the ganzfeld condition for a period ranging from 15 to 30 minutes. During this phase, participants describe verbally all images, feelings, emotions, they deem related to the target usually a picture or a short videoclip of real objects or events.</p><p class="" id=d43259e163>Once the ganzfeld phase is completed, participants are presented with our choices (the target plus three decoys) of the same format, e.g. picture or videoclip, and they must choose which one is the target (binary decision). Alternatively, they may be asked to rate all four (e.g., from 0 to 100), to indicate the strength of relationship between the information detected during the ganzfeld phase and the images or video clips contents.</p><p class="" id=d43259e167>A variant of the judgment phase is to send the recording of the information retrieved during the ganzfeld phase to an external judge for independent ratings of the target. In order to prevent voluntary or involuntary leakage of information about the target by the experimenters, the research assistant who interact with the participants must be blind to the target identity until the participants’ rating task is over. The choice of the target and the decoys is usually made using automatic random procedures, and scores are automatically fed onto a scoring sheet.</p><div class=list><a name=d43259e170 class=n-a></a><ul style="list-style-type: none"><li><p id=d43259e176><span class=label></span> There are three different ganzfeld conditions:</p></li><li><p id=d43259e184><span class=label></span> -Type 1: the target is chosen after the judgment phase;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e192><span class=label></span> -Type 2: the target is chosen before the ganzfeld phase;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e200><span class=label></span> -Type 3: the target is chosen before the ganzfeld phase and presented to a partner of the participant isolated in a separate and distant room.</p></li></ul></div><p class="" id=d43259e205>These differences are related to some theoretical and perceptual concepts we will discuss later. It is important to note that type of task makes no difference to the participant who only engages in target identification <i>after</i> the ganzfeld phase.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d43259e214>Review of the Ganzfeld Meta-Analyses</h2><p class="" id=d43259e217>It is interesting to note that most of the cumulative findings (meta-analyses) of this line of investigation were periodically published in the mainstream journal <i>Psychological Bulletin</i>.</p><p class="" id=d43259e223><a href="#ref-11">Honorton (1985)</a> undertook one of the first meta-analyses of the many ganzfeld studies completed by the mid-1980s. In total, 28 studies yielded a collective hit rate of 38%, where mean chance expectation (MCE) was 25%. Various flaws in his approach were pointed out by <a href="#ref-9">Hyman (1985)</a>, but in their joint-communiqué they agree that “there is an overall significant effect in this database that cannot reasonably be explained by selective reporting or multiple analysis” (<a href="#ref-10">Hyman &amp; Honorton, 1986</a>, p. 351).</p><p class="" id=d43259e234>A second major meta-analysis on a set of ‘autoganzfeld’ studies was performed by <a href="#ref-2">Bem &amp; Honorton (1994)</a>. These studies followed the guidelines laid down by <a href="#ref-10">Hyman &amp; Honorton (1986)</a>. Moreover the autoganzfeld procedure avoids methodological flaws by using a computer-controlled target randomization, selection, and judging technique. They overall reported hit rate of 32.2% exceeded again the mean chance expectation.</p><p class="" id=d43259e243><a href="#ref-16">Milton &amp; Wiseman (1999)</a> meta-analysed further 30 studies collected for the period 1987 to 1997; reporting an overall nonsignificant standardized effect size of 0.013. However, Jessica Utts (personal communication, December 11, 2009) using the exact binomial test on trial counts only (<i>N</i> = 1198; Hits = 327), found a significant hit rate of 27% (<i>p</i> = 0.036).</p><p class="" id=d43259e255><a href="#ref-22">Storm &amp; Ertel (2001)</a> comparing <a href="#ref-16">Milton &amp; Wiseman’s (1999)</a> database with <a href="#ref-2">Bem &amp; Honorton’s (1994)</a> one, found the two did not differ significantly. Furthermore Storm and Ertel went on to compile a 79-study database, which had a statistically significant mean standardized effect size of 0.138.</p><p class="" id=d43259e266><a href="#ref-23">Storm <i>et al.</i> (2010)</a>, meta-analysed a database of 29 ganzfeld studies published during the period 1997 to 2008, yielding a standardized effect size of 0.14. <a href="#ref-19">Rouder <i>et al.</i> (2013)</a> reassessing <a href="#ref-23">Storm <i>et al.</i>’s (2010)</a> meta-analysis, with a Bayesian meta-analysis found evidence for the existence of an anomalous perception in the original dataset observing a Bayes Factor of 330 in support to the alternative hypothesis (p. 241). However they contended the effect could be due to “difficulties in randomization” (p. 241), arguing that ganzfeld studies with computerized randomization had smaller effects than those with manual randomization. The reanalysis by <a href="#ref-24">Storm <i>et al.</i>’s (2013)</a> showed that this conclusion was unconvincing as it was based on Rouder <i>et al.</i>’s faulty inclusion of different categories of study.</p><p class="" id=d43259e295>In the last meta-analysis by <a href="#ref-25">Storm &amp; Tressoldi (2020)</a>, related to the studies published from 2008 to 2018, the overall standardized effect size was 0.133; 95%CI: 0.06 - 0.18.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d43259e304>This study</h2><p class="" id=d43259e307>The main aim of this study is to meta-analyse all available ganzfeld studies dating from 1974 up to June 2020 in order to assess the overall effect size of the database and determine whether there are moderator variables that affect task performance; in particular, we hypothesize that participant type and type of task are two major moderators of effect size (see Methods section).</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d43259e313>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d43259e316 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Reporting guidelines</h3><p class="" id=d43259e321>This study will follow the guidelines of the APA Meta-Analysis Reporting Standard (<a href="#ref-1">Appelbaum <i>et al.</i>, 2018</a>) and the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis Protocols (PRISMA-P, <a href="#ref-17">Moher <i>et al.</i>, 2015</a>).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e337 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Studies retrieval</h3><p class="" id=d43259e342>Retrieval of studies related to anomalous perception in a Ganzfeld environment is simplified, firstly by the fact that most of these studies have already been retrieved for previous meta-analyses, as cited in the introduction. Secondly, this line of investigation is carried out by a small community of researchers. Thirdly, most of the studies of interest to us are published in specialized journals that adopted the editorial policy of accepting paper with results that are statistically non-significant (according to the frequentist approach). This last condition is particularly relevant because it reduces the publication bias due to the non-rejection of the statistical null hypothesis often consequent to reduced statistical power. However, in order to integrate the previous retrieval method, we will carry-out an online search with <a target=xrefwindow href="https://scholar.google.com/" id=d43259e344>Google Scholar</a>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/" id=d43259e347>PubMed</a> and <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.scopus.com/home.uri" id=d43259e350>Scopus</a> databases of all papers from 1974 to 2020 including in the title and/or the abstract the word “ganzfeld” (e.g. for PubMed: Search: ganzfeld[Title/Abstract] Filters: from 1974 – 2020).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e355 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Studies inclusion criteria</h3><p class="" id=d43259e360>The following inclusion criteria will be adopted:</p><div class=list><a name=d43259e363 class=n-a></a><ul style="list-style-type: none"><li><p id=d43259e370><span class=label>- </span> Studies related to anomalous perception in a ganzfeld environment;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e379><span class=label>- </span> Studies must use human participants only (not animals);</p></li><li><p id=d43259e388><span class=label>- </span> Number of participants must be in excess of two to avoid the inherent problems that are typical in case studies;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e397><span class=label>- </span> Target selection must be randomized by using a Random Number Generator (RNG) in a computer or similar electronic device, or a table of random numbers. Randomization procedures must not be manipulated by the experimenter or participant;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e406><span class=label>- </span> Studies must provide sufficient information (e.g., number of trials and outcomes) for the authors to calculate the direct hit-rates and effect size values, so that appropriate statistical tests can be conducted.</p></li><li><p id=d43259e416><span class=label>- </span> Peer reviewed studies even if published in proceedings;</p></li></ul></div></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e422 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Variables coding</h3><p class="" id=d43259e427>For each included study, one of the authors, expert in meta-analyses, will code the following variables:</p><div class=list><a name=d43259e430 class=n-a></a><ul style="list-style-type: none"><li><p id=d43259e437><span class=label>- </span> Authors;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e446><span class=label>- </span> Year of publication;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e455><span class=label>- </span> Number of trials;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e464><span class=label>- </span> Number of hits;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e473><span class=label>- </span> Number of choices of each trial;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e483><span class=label>- </span> Task type (Type 1,2 or 3);</p></li><li><p id=d43259e492><span class=label>- </span> Participants type (selected vs. unselected). The authors of the study will score as selected all participants that were screened for one or more particular characteristic deemed favourable for the performance in this type of task.</p></li></ul></div><p class="" id=d43259e497>The second author will randomly check 10% of all studies, and the data will be compared with those extracted by the other author. Discrepancies will be corrected by inspecting the original papers.</p><p class="" id=d43259e500>The complete database will be made available through open access posting within the dedicated project in the Open Science Framework (<a target=xrefwindow href="https://osf.io/t7sya/" id=d43259e502>https://osf.io/t7sya/</a>) platform.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e508 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Effect size measures</h3><p class="" id=d43259e513>As standardized measure of effect size, we will use the following formula: Binomial Z score/√number of trials. The Binomial Z score will be obtained applying the formula implemented online at <a target=xrefwindow href="http://vassarstats.net/binomialX.html" id=d43259e515>http://vassarstats.net/binomialX.html</a></p><p class="" id=d43259e518>In order to take in account the effect size overestimation bias in small samples, the effect size will be transformed in the Hedge’s <i>g</i> effect size, obtaining the corresponding variance by applying the formula presented in <a href="#ref-3">Borenstein <i>et al.</i> (2009, pp. 27–28)</a>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e531 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Overall effect size estimation</h3><p class="" id=d43259e536>The overall effect size estimation of the whole database will be calculated by applying both a frequentist and a Bayesian random model for testing its robustness.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e540 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Frequentist random model</h3><p class="" id=d43259e545>Following the recommendations of <a href="#ref-14">Langan <i>et al.</i> (2019)</a>, we will use the restricted maximum likelihood (REML) approach to estimate the heterogeneity variance with the Knapp and Hartung method for adjustment to the standard errors of the estimated coefficients (<a href="#ref-21">Rubio-Aparicio <i>et al.</i>, 2018</a>).</p><p class="" id=d43259e560>Furthermore, in order to control for possible influence of outliers, we will calculate the median and mode of the overall effect size applying the method suggested by <a href="#ref-6">Hartwig <i>et al.</i> (2020)</a>.</p><p class="" id=d43259e569>These calculations will be implemented in the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.r-project.org/" id=d43259e571>R</a> statistical environment with the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/metafor/index.html" id=d43259e574>metafor</a> package v. 2.4 (<a href="#ref-28">Viechtbauer, 2017</a>). See syntax provided as extended data (<a href="#ref-26">Tressoldi &amp; Storm, 2020</a>).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e585 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Bayesian random model</h3><p class="" id=d43259e590>As priors for the overall effect size we will use a normal distribution with Mean = 0.01; <i>SD</i> =0.03, constrained positive, lower bound = 0 (<a href="#ref-20">Rouder <i>et al.</i>, 2019</a>), given our expectation of a positive value. This Bayesian meta-analysis will be implemented with the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/metaBMA/index.html" id=d43259e601>MetaBMA</a> package v. 0.6.3 (<a href="#ref-7">Heck <i>et al.</i>, 2017</a>). See Syntax in the Supporting Information.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e612 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Publication bias tests</h3><p class="" id=d43259e617>Following the suggestions of <a href="#ref-4">Carter <i>et al.</i> (2019)</a>, we will apply three tests to assess publication bias:</p><div class=list><a name=d43259e626 class=n-a></a><ul style="list-style-type: none"><li><p id=d43259e633><span class=label>- </span> the 3-parameter selection model (3PSM), as implemented by <a href="#ref-5">Coburn &amp; Vevea (2019)</a> with the package ‘<a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/weightr/index.html" id=d43259e638>weightr</a>’ v.2.0.2;</p></li><li><p id=d43259e648><span class=label>- </span> the p-uniform* (star) v. 0.2.2 test as described by <a href="#ref-27">van Aert &amp; van Assen (2019)</a>, and</p></li><li><p id=d43259e660><span class=label>- </span> the sensitivity analysis using the <a href="#ref-15">Mathur &amp; VanderWeele (2020)</a> package <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/PublicationBias/index.html" id=d43259e665>PublicationBias</a> v.2.2.0.</p></li></ul></div><p class="" id=d43259e671>The three parameters model represent the average true underlying effect, <i>δ</i>; the heterogeneity of the random effect sizes, τ<sup>2</sup>; and the probability that there is a nonsignificant effect in the pool of effect sizes. The probability parameter is modeled by a step function with a single cut point at <i>p</i> = 0.025 (one-tailed), which corresponds to a two-tailed <i>p</i> value of 0.05. This cut point divides the range of possible <i>p</i> values into two bins: significant and nonsignificant. The three parameters are estimated using maximum likelihood (<a href="#ref-4">Carter <i>et al.</i>, 2019</a>, p. 124).</p><p class="" id=d43259e696>The <i>p</i>-uniform* test, is an extension and improvement of the <i>p</i>-uniform method. P-uniform* improves upon <i>p</i>-uniform giving a more efficient estimator avoiding the overestimation of effect size in case of between-study variance in true effect sizes, thus enabling estimation and testing for the presence of between-study variance in true effect sizes. </p><p class="" id=d43259e709>Sensitivity analysis as implemented by <a href="#ref-15">Mathur &amp; VanderWeele (2020)</a>, assumes a publication process such that “statistically significant” results are more likely to be published than negative or “nonsigniﬁcant” results by an unknown ratio, <i>η</i> (eta). Using inverse-probability weighting and robust estimation that accommodates non-normal true eﬀects, small meta-analyses, and clustering, it enables statements such as: “For publication bias to shift the observed point estimate to the null, ‘signiﬁcant’ results would need to be at least 30-fold more likely to be published than negative or ‘nonsigniﬁcant’ results” (p. 1). Comparable statements can be made regarding shifting to a chosen non-null value or shifting the conﬁdence interval. See Syntax in the Supporting Information</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e719 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Cumulative meta-analysis</h3><p class="" id=d43259e724>In order to study the overall trend of the cumulative evidence and in particular for testing the presence of a decline or incline effect, we will perform a cumulative effect size estimation (see Syntax in the Supporting Information)</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e729 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Moderators effects</h3><p class="" id=d43259e734>We will compare the influence of the following two moderators: (i) Type of participant, and (ii) Type of task.</p><p class="" id=d43259e737>As described in the Variable Coding paragraph, the variable Type of participant, will be coded in a binary way: selected vs unselected. Type of task will be coded as Type 1, Type 2, and Type 3, as described in the Introduction. See Syntax in the Supporting Information.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e741 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Statistical power</h3><p class="" id=d43259e746>Once the overall effect size and its precision are estimated, we will calculate the number of trials necessary to achieve a statistical power of at least .80 with an α = .05. With this estimation we can examine how many studies in the database reached this threshold.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e750 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Reporting</h3><p class="" id=d43259e755>The search and selection of the studies will be presented by using a PRISMA flowchart.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e759 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Descriptive statistics</h3><p class="" id=d43259e764>Descriptive statistics will be produced related to the variables, trials, hits, participant type, and task types.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e768 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Overall effect size</h3><p class="" id=d43259e773>We will present the estimated average effect size along with the corresponding 95% Confidence Intervals or Credible Intervals of both the Frequentist and Random Models as described in the Methods section. We will calculate the values of τ<sup>2</sup> and I<sup>2</sup> (<a href="#ref-8">Higgins &amp; Thompson, 2002</a>), and their confidence intervals, as measures of between-study variance.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e786 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Publication bias tests</h3><p class="" id=d43259e791>We will present the results of the three publication bias tests described in the Methods section.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e796 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Cumulative effect size</h3><p class="" id=d43259e801>The results of the cumulative meta-analysis will be represented with a cumulative forest plot.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e805 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Moderator effects</h3><p class="" id=d43259e810>We will present and compare the average effect size along with the corresponding 95% Confidence Intervals of the two types the participant, and of the three task types.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e814 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Dissemination of information</h3><p class="" id=d43259e819>Apart the Registered Report, all information related to this study will be made available open access at Open Science Framework.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e823 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Study status</h3><p class="" id=d43259e828>The study has not started yet.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d43259e835>Discussion</h2><p class="" id=d43259e838>We will discuss the robustness of the overall results in order to determine a degree of confidence in the evidence for anomalous perception. In case of an insufficient degree of confidence in the evidence, we will consider whether it is worthwhile pursuing such a line of investigation and offer solutions to improve the evidence.</p><p class="" id=d43259e841>However, even if the overall results show a sufficient degree of evidence, we will discuss how this line of investigation can instil greater confidence by using a preregistration registry as proposed by <a href="#ref-29">Watt &amp; Kennedy (2016)</a> in order to reduce so-called questionable research practices (<a href="#ref-12">John <i>et al.</i>, 2012</a>), and provide more transparent procedures during data collection and analysis (see for example, the Transparent Psi Project; <a href="#ref-13">Kekecs <i>et al.</i>, 2019</a>).</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d43259e863>Data availability</h2><div class=section><a name=d43259e866 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Underlying data</h3><p class="" id=d43259e871>No data are associated with this article</p></div><div class=section><a name=d43259e875 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Extended data</h3><p class="" id=d43259e880>Figshare: Anomalous perception in a gazfeld condition: a meta-analysis of more than 40 years of investigation. <a target=xrefwindow href="https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.12674618.v1" id=d43259e882>https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.12674618.v1</a> (<a href="#ref-26">Tressoldi &amp;
