            <meta name="dc.description" content=" Background: Gene silencing by CpG island hypermethylation often plays a role in colorectal cancer (CRC) progression. Certain regions of the genome, called high confidence differentially-methylated regions (DMRs), are consistently hypermethylated across numerous patient samples. 
 Methods: In this study, we used bioinformatics and bisulfite PCR sequencing of HCT-116 cells to investigate methylation levels at DMRs in the promoters of six genes: DKK3, EN1, MiR34b, SDC2, SPG20 , and TLX1 . We then investigated whether the anti-cancer drug decitabine, had a demethylating effect at these promoter regions. 
 Results: We found that hypermethylation correlated with lack of transcriptional enhancer binding in these six regions. Importantly, we observed that for all DMRs, decitabine significantly reduced CpG methylation. Decitabine also reduced clonogenic survival, suggesting that there is a correlation between lower CpG island methylation levels and reduced cancerous properties. 
 Conclusions: Our study provided single-nucleotide resolution and revealed hypermethylated CpG sites not shown by previous genome-wide methylation studies. In the future, we plan to perform experiments that demonstrate a causal link between promoter hypermethylation and carcinogenesis and that more accurately model treatments in CRC patients.">

Certain regions of the genome, called high confidence differentially-methylated regions (DMRs), are consistently hypermethylated across numerous patient samples.<br/> <b>Methods:</b> In this study, we used bioinformatics and bisulfite PCR sequencing of HCT-116 cells to investigate methylation levels at DMRs in the promoters of six genes: <i>DKK3, EN1, MiR34b, SDC2, SPG20</i>, and <i>TLX1</i>. Importantly, we observed that for all DMRs, decitabine significantly reduced CpG methylation. Decitabine also reduced clonogenic survival, suggesting that there is a correlation between lower CpG island methylation levels and reduced cancerous properties.<br/> <b>Conclusions:</b> Our study provided single-nucleotide resolution and revealed hypermethylated CpG sites not shown by previous genome-wide methylation studies. While CRC incidence has been decreasing for individuals above 50 years of age, the incidence rates increased by 22% between 2000–2013 in individuals below 50<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. Chemotherapy is not effective, and patients typically survive only 44 months after the completion of treatment<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. Despite this pressing reality, there is still debate about the molecular mechanisms underlying this disease. Genetic and epigenetic factors contribute to oncogenesis, but there is no consensus on a definitive molecular pathway<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. </p><p class="" id=d322e339>Cytosine methylation changes the expression of genes involved in cancer progression. DNA methylation is the transfer of a methyl group onto the C5 position of cytosine to form 5-methylcytosine<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. Promoter hypermethylation has been observed to drive CRC disease progression. For instance, promoter hypermethylation of the <i>hMLH1</i> gene leads to mismatch repair defects and a hypermutator phenotype in CRC<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. Genetic knockout of two DNA methyltransferase genes restores expression of the tumor suppressor gene <i>CDKN2A</i> and slows growth of the CRC cell line HCT116<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d322e360>Numerous genomic regions have been shown to be consistently hypermethylated in multiple samples of colon cancer in comparison to regular colon cells<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a>,<a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>. For instance, Simmer <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>, found 2867 genomic regions consistently differentially methylated regions in CRC (DMRs). However, the genome-wide methylation techniques used by these studies lack nucleotide-level resolution at these DMRs<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>, and do not address if these DMRs play a mechanistic role in CRC progression.</p><p class="" id=d322e380>We sought to investigate if the colorectal cancer cell line, HCT-116, can serve as a viable model for DMR hypermethylation. HCT-116 cells have been observed to be an orthotopic model for colon cancer in mice<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>. Furthermore, knockout of the DNA methyltransferases DNMT1 and DNMT3b has been observed to deplete &gt;98% of total methylation and slow HCT-116 growth<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>, indicating global DNA methylation plays a role in HCT-116 cell survival. If HCT-116 cells are to serve as a model for DMR hypermethylation in CRC, then we expect to observe hypermethylation at established high-confidence DMRs and a reversibility of methylation with the demethylating anticancer drug, decitabine<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d322e396>In the present study, we sought to investigate the effect of the decitabine on high confidence DMRs found in the promoter regions of genes previously connected to cancer progression from 2648 available DMRs. We selected DMRs proximal to <i>DKK3</i>, <i>EN1</i>, <i>mir34b</i>, <i>SDC2</i>, <i>SPG20</i>, and <i>TLX1</i>. DKK3 is a Wnt signaling pathway inhibitor found to be hypermethylated in colon cancer cells, possibly promoting oncogenic Wnt signaling<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. <i>EN1</i> is a canonical gene in development<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup> but has recently been linked to increased cell proliferation as a non-canonical prosurvival transcription factor in cancer cells<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. In addition, the <i>EN1</i> promoter is found to be hypermethylated in CRC with a CpG island methylator phenotype<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. <i>mir34b</i> is a micro-RNA that is essential for normal brain development, motile ciliogenesis and spermatogenesis<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. It was found to be hypermethylated in 100 out of 101 colon cancer cell lines<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. SDC2 is a transmembrane proteoglycan that mediates cytoskeletal organization and adhesion to the ECM<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. SDC2 acts as a positive regulator of growth factor signals whose aberrant expression correlates with tumor size<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. <i>SDC2</i> promoter is hypermethylated in cancer<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>. SPG20 regulates cytokinesis and may alter cell division when aberrantly methylated in CRC<sup><a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>. TLX1<i></i> functions as a transcription factor<sup><a href="#ref-23">23</a></sup> and its gene promoter is frequently hypermethylated in CRC<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a>,<a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. While it is overexpressed and demethylated in leukemia<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup> aberrant hypermethylation may also promote growth, as reviewed in <a href="#ref-25">25</a>.</p><p class="" id=d322e493> We predicted that decitabine will have a negative effect on HCT-116 proliferation, and will decrease hypermethylation at these DMRs. Using the UCSC genome browser, we observed that DMR methylation inversely correlated with transcriptional activator binding across multiple cell lines. Using bisulfite PCR we observed that decitabine inhibits methylation across each DMR.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d322e499>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d322e502 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Transcriptional enhancer binding analysis</h3><p class="" id=d322e507>DMRs were selected from Simmer <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. Genomic regions from table S4 of that publication were aligned to human genome 19 (hg19) using <a target=xrefwindow href="https://genome.ucsc.edu/cgi-bin/hgLiftOver" id=d322e515>liftOver</a><sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>. UCSC genome browser was configured to visualize transcription factors and methylation status according to the following configuration: <a target=xrefwindow href="http://genome.ucsc.edu/s/williamhconrad/hg19-all-cell-methylation" id=d322e521>http://genome.ucsc.edu/s/williamhconrad/hg19%2Dall%2Dcell%2Dmethylation</a>. Transcription factors were selected randomly from the track UCSC genome browser track "Transcription Factor ChIP-seq (161 factors) from ENCODE with Factorbook Motifs". 10 cell lines with transcription factor binding were recorded and 10 cell lines lacking transcription factor binding were recorded at each DMR. Cell lines were selected using a random number generator. Each transcription factor was recorded as a transcriptional repressor or enhancer according to <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.uniprot.org/" id=d322e524>UniProt</a><sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. The methylation status was then recorded for each of those cell lines. Methylation was recorded as fully methylated, mostly methylated, majority methylated, half-methylated, minority methylated, mostly unmethylated, fully unmethylated, not detected, or not tested. The cell lines were then sorted into methylated or unmethylated. The number of repressors and enhancers was recorded, and a chi squared analysis was performed to compare differences in repressor and enhancer binding to methylated or unmethylated regions using excel Data are published as underlying data<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d322e537 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>HCT-116 cell culture</h3><p class="" id=d322e542>HCT-116 cells were obtained from American type culture collection (ATCC; CCL-247) and cultured in McCoy’s 5A media (ATCC; 30-2007) in 10% fetal bovine serum (ATCC; 30-2007) and penicillin streptomycin (Thermo; 15140122). Cells were maintained in tissue culture flasks at 37 °C and 5% CO<sub>2</sub> according to established protocols from ATCC<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d322e553 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Clonogenic survival assay</h3><p class="" id=d322e558>To test the effect of decitabine on the clonogenic survival of HCT-116 cells, we used protocols adapted from Franken <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup> and Palii <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. Cells were plated at a density of 200 per well in a 6-well tissue culture plate. After overnight incubation, cells were treated with vehicle (anhydrous DMSO; Sigma; 276855), 0.1, 0.25, or 1 µM decitabine (Sigma; A3656). Cells were treated again after 24 hrs and then replaced with 2 ml of complete McCoy’s 5A media. After 12 days of growth, cells were fixed and stained with 10% w/v glutaraldehyde (Sigma; 340855), 0.5% w/v crystal violet (Sigma; C6158) in PBS (Sigma; 1408). After extensive rinsing in tap water, colonies were counted by eye. The average number of colonies was compared across the four conditions using a one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s HSD <i>post hoc</i> test.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d322e577 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Decitabine treatment and genomic DNA isolation</h3><p class="" id=d322e582>HCT-116 cells were cultured as described above. Exponentially growing cells were passaged to a density 20% confluency. The day following passage, cells were treated with 1 µM decitabine or DMSO. After 24 hours, treatment was repeated. After four additional days of incubation, cells were collected by trypsinization. 400,000 cells for each condition were collected and genomic DNA was isolated using the PureLink genomic DNA mini kit according to the manufacturers instructions (Thermo Fisher; K182001).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d322e587 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Global demethylation analysis</h3><p class="" id=d322e592>400 ng of genomic DNA isolated as above was treated with 10 units of <i>HpaII</i> (NEB; R0171S) at 37 C for 1 hr. genomic DNA digestion was then evaluated by 1% agarose (VWR; 97062-244) gel electrophoresis. DNA was stained with 1x sybr safe (VWR; 470193-138) and imaged on a Bio-rad chemidoc imaging system.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d322e599 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Bisulfite PCR</h3><p class="" id=d322e604>400 ng of genomic DNA isolated as above was bisulfite converted using the EZ DNA methylation kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions (zymo research; D5001). Bisulfite converted DNA was eluted in 10 µl at a concentration of approximately 40 ng / µl. Oligonucleotides for bisulfite PCR were designed using <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.urogene.org/methprimer/" id=d322e606>MethPrimer</a><sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>. The positive strand sequence for a DMR was collected from UCSC genome browser and primers were designed for an amplicon between 150 and 400 nucleotides. Optimal annealing temperature for each primer pair were determined by testing a range of annealing temperatures between 44 and 66 °C for each primer pair against fully unmethylated and fully methylated genomic DNA (zymo; D5014). Primer pairs (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>), optimal annealing temperatures (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>), and PCR reaction conditions (<a href="#T2">Table 2</a>), reaction master mix (<a href="#T3">Table 3</a>) are provided in the indicated tables. Amplified DNA was purified from oligonucleotide primers and dNTPs using zymo DNA clean and concentrator-5 according to the manufacturer’s instructions (zymo; D4013). Samples were eluted in 10 µl of elution buffer and submitted for sequencing at the University of Chicago Comprehensive Cancer Center DNA sequencing and genotyping facility (Chicago, IL). Percent methylation was calculated using the relative peak height of cytosine and uracil at a given CpG site, as described previously. Peak height was quantified using <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.thermofisher.com/order/catalog/product/A28220#/A28220" id=d322e625>Thermo Fisher Variant analysis app</a> on the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.thermofisher.com/us/en/home/digital-science.html" id=d322e628>thermo fisher connect web site</a>. Briefly, this cloud-based application processes .abi sequencing chromatogram files and returns base calls and peak height values for each peak on the chromatogram. The open source software Chromaseq can also extract identical base call and peak height values from .abi files<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>. Briefly, <a target=xrefwindow href="http://chromaseq.mesquiteproject.org/" id=d322e635>chromaseq</a> can be installed according to their <a target=xrefwindow href="http://chromaseq.mesquiteproject.org/download.html?ProcessingChromatoPanel=open&amp;ChromatogramViewerPanel=open&amp;CreditPanel=open&amp;HelpPanel=open&amp;OtherFeaturesPanel=open" id=d322e638>web site</a>. The .abi sequencing files can be viewed using this software. Selecting a base call will reveal the identical peak height value presented in the chromatogram as exported in the Thermo Fisher Variant analysis app.</p><a name=T1 class=n-a></a><div class="table-wrap panel clearfix"><div class=caption><h3>Table 1. For each of our six DMRs we observed TF binding across 20 cell lines selected by random number generator (see methods). class=section-title>Decitabine diminishes clonogenicity of HCT-116 cells and decreases global DNA methylation</h3><p class="" id=d322e1409>The ability of a cancer cell to form a colony has been a long-standing measure for its survival in the host. Interventions that ablate clonogenicity increase patient survival<sup><a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup>. Decitabine is known to inhibit clonogenic survival of HCT-116 cells<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>, and we observe inhibition of clonogenic survival at similar doses ranging from 1 µM to 100 nM (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>). Furthermore, decitabine treatment is known to increase sensitivity of HCT-116 genomic DNA to the restriction enzyme, <i>HpaII</i>, which is inhibited by CpG methylation<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. Likewise, we observe that genomic DNA collected from HCT-116 cells treated for 48 hr with 1 µM decitabine was digested by decitabine (<a href="#f3">Figure 3</a>). From these data, we conclude that decitabine inhibits the clonogenic survival of HCT-116 cells and also inhibits DNA methylation.</p><a name=f2 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/22473/ff6f2fa3-2e0b-4588-92db-280117ba48c2_figure2.gif"><img alt="ff6f2fa3-2e0b-4588-92db-280117ba48c2_figure2.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/22473/ff6f2fa3-2e0b-4588-92db-280117ba48c2_figure2.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 2. 1 µM Decitabine inhibits clonogenic survival.</h3><p id=d322e1444>Number of HCT-116 colonies detected by crystal violet staining 14 days after two 24-hour treatments of the indicated doses of decitabine. *p&lt;0.05 ANOVA, Tukey's post-hoc.</p></div></div><a name=f3 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/22473/ff6f2fa3-2e0b-4588-92db-280117ba48c2_figure3.gif"><img alt="ff6f2fa3-2e0b-4588-92db-280117ba48c2_figure3.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/22473/ff6f2fa3-2e0b-4588-92db-280117ba48c2_figure3.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 3. Using bisulfite PCR, we detected conversion of unmethylated cytosine to uracil (<a href="#f4">Figure 4a</a>). Importantly, we both identified methylated CpG sites previously detected by reduced-representation bisulfite sequencing as part of the ENCODE project, and we also identified novel CpG sites in the region, adding resolution to the methylation status of these select DMRs (<a href="#f4">Figure 4b</a>).</p><a name=f4 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/22473/ff6f2fa3-2e0b-4588-92db-280117ba48c2_figure4.gif"><img alt="ff6f2fa3-2e0b-4588-92db-280117ba48c2_figure4.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/22473/ff6f2fa3-2e0b-4588-92db-280117ba48c2_figure4.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 4. We observed statistically significant reductions in CpG methylation at all tested DMRs. From these data we can conclude that all colon cancer DMRs tested are hypermethylated in HCT-116 cells, that bisulfite PCR offers increased resolution at DMRs over HM450 array or RRBS, and that methylation at DMRs is reversable by 1 µM decitabine treatment.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d322e1529>Discussion</h2><p class="" id=d322e1532>From our results, we conclude that HCT-116 cells can serve as a model for investigating the role of high confidence DMRs in colon cancer. As observed previously, we also observed HCT-116 cells to be sensitive to the demethylating agent decitabine<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a>,<a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. Decitabine inhibited clonogenicity (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>) and demethylated genomic DNA (<a href="#f3">Figure 3</a>). By bisulfite PCR, we observed DMRs previously identified in patient colon cancer cells to be consistently hypermethylated in HCT-116 cells. Furthermore, we achieved single-nucleotide resolution of CpG methylation at these DMRs. We were able to confirm previously identified CpG sites as well as identify new ones (<a href="#f4">Figure 4</a>).</p><p class="" id=d322e1551>The HCT-116 model of DMR methylation has potential to shed light on the role of DMRs in colon cancer pathogenesis. Moving forward, it will be important to determine if (a) DMRs reduce nearby gene expression, (b) if DMR demethylation increases gene expression, and (c) if restoring expression of methylation-silenced genes affects HCT-116 growth or survival. In future, we plan to increase resolution at DMRs relevant to HCT-116 cell growth by sequencing across the entire DMR and by testing additional DMRs. The most updated data can be viewed using the UCSC genome browser public session found here: <a target=xrefwindow href="https://bit.ly/UCSC-DMR-methylation" id=d322e1553>https://bit.ly/UCSC-DMR-methylation</a>. The original data presented in this manuscript will be maintained as described in the data availability section<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a>,<a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d322e1564>Future work is needed to address the question if specific gene products suppressed by hypermethylation play a role in tumor growth. Nine frequently hypermethylated genes have been observed to slow cancer cell growth when heterologously expressed<sup><a href="#ref-42">42</a></sup>. Two additional frequently hypermethylated genes have been observed to inhibit cancer cell colony formation when heterologously expressed<sup><a href="#ref-43">43</a></sup>. Moving forward, we seek to heterologously express gene products suppressed by hypermethylation to test if such expression affects tumor cell growth and colony formation.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d322e1578>Data availability</h2><div class=section><a name=d322e1581 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Underlying data</h3><p class="" id=d322e1586>Zenodo: williamhconrad/HCT116-DMR-bisulfite-PCR: bisulfite PCR repository under CC0 license. <a target=xrefwindow href="http://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3948439" id=d322e1588>http://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3948439</a><sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup></p><p class="" id=d322e1594>This project contains the following underlying data:<div class=list><a name=d322e1596 class=n-a></a><ul><li><p id=d322e1600>“Underlying data for Figure 2 -
