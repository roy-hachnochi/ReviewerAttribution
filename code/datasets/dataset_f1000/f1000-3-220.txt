            <meta name="dc.description" content="In this study, Markov chain models of gene regulatory networks (GRN) are developed. These models make it possible to apply the well-known theory and tools of Markov chains to GRN analysis. A new kind of finite interaction graph called a combinatorial net is introduced to represent formally a GRN and its transition graphs constructed from interaction graphs. The system dynamics are defined as a random walk on the transition graph, which is a Markov chain. A novel concurrent updating scheme (evolution rule) is developed to determine transitions in a transition graph. The proposed scheme is based on the firing of a random set of non-steady-state vertices in a combinatorial net. It is demonstrated that this novel scheme represents an advance in asynchronicity modeling. The theorem that combinatorial nets with this updating scheme can asynchronously compute a maximal independent set of graphs is also proved. As proof of concept, a number of simple combinatorial models are presented here: a discrete auto-regression model, a bistable switch, an Elowitz repressilator, and a self-activation model, and it is shown that these models exhibit well-known properties.">

Therefore, there is a need to develop methods of computational and theoretical analysis of gene regulatory networks (GRNs). One of the most promising directions is to reduce the problem to the study of Markov chains generated in some way from the GRN<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. Usually, Boolean networks<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup> are used as a formal representation of a GRN. Classification of process states, studies of long-term behavior<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>, and development of optimal strategies for therapeutic intervention<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup> provide good examples of this approach<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. In contrast to Boolean networks, Hopfield networks are defined using arithmetic operations<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. They are a well-developed branch of science dealing with stochastic processes of asynchronous state switching as a result of interactions. As such, they are similar to Boolean networks. A Hopfield-like formalism also leads to the definition of a Markov chain. In the Hopfield network field, essential results have been obtained in the study of various update schemes<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>, network oscillations<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>, solutions of combinatorial optimization problems<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>, estimation of convergence rates, and many other problems. This makes it valuable to study the possibility of using Hopfield like-networks to construct Markov chains from GRNs and other interaction graphs. Here, a GRN is considered to be a kind of interaction graph. Interaction (regulatory) graphs have emerged in various fields of the life sciences<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. In recent years, their transition graphs have often been used to analyze the properties of interactions (regulations). One promising way to understand the nature of the regulations or interactions represented by interaction graphs is to analyze the Markov chain associated with their transition graphs.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d6532e250>Method</h2><p class="" id=d6532e253>The proposed method may be viewed as a version of the Hopfield-like network<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup> where groups of randomly selected unstable units are updated in parallel<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. </p><div class=section><a name=d6532e264 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Interaction graphs and non-steady-state vertices</h3><p class="" id=d6532e269>Let <i>G</i> = (<i>V</i>, <i>E</i>) be a directed graph, where <i>V</i> is a set of vertices and <i>E</i> is a set of edges. Let <i>B</i> = {0, 1} be a set of vertex states. It is said that the vertex is <i>active</i> if the state of this vertex is equal to 1, otherwise it is said that the vertex is <i>inactive</i>. The mapping function <i>M</i>: <i>V  B</i> gives the state of each vertex. For a given vertex <i>v</i>  <i>V</i>, <i>M</i>(<i>v</i>) is the state of vertex <i>v</i> that corresponds to map <i>M</i>. <i>M</i>(<i>v</i>) = 1 is equivalent to saying that vertex v is active under map M. <i>M</i>(<i>v</i>) = 0 is equivalent to saying that vertex v is inactive. The weighting function <i>W</i>: <i>E  R</i> gives value of each edge of graph <i>G</i>, which represents the power of interactions. If <i>e</i> = (<i>u</i>, <i>v</i>), <i>e</i>  <i>E</i>, then <i>u</i> is said to be a direct ancestor of <i>v</i> and <i>v</i> to be a direct descendant of <i>u</i>. The <i>influence</i> on <i>v under</i> the map <i>M</i> is defined as the sum of weights of edges from all direct active ancestors of vertex <i>v</i>. The influence on <i>v</i> under the map <i>M</i> is denoted by <i>I</i>(<i>v</i>, <i>M</i>) (also called the local field or the net input). That is,</p><p class="" id=d6532e401><div class="disp-formula panel"><a name=d6532e402 class=n-a></a><math display=block id=math1 overflow=scroll><mrow><mi>I</mi><mrow><mo>(</mo><mrow><mi>v</mi><mo>,</mo><mi>M</mi></mrow><mo>)</mo></mrow><mo>=</mo><mstyle displaystyle=true><munder><mo></mo><mrow><mrow><mo>{</mo><mrow><mrow><mi>u</mi><mo>|</mo></mrow><mrow><mo>(</mo><mrow><mi>u</mi><mo>,</mo><mi>v</mi></mrow><mo>)</mo></mrow><mo></mo><mi>E</mi></mrow><mo>}</mo></mrow></mrow></munder><mrow><mi>W</mi><mrow><mo>(</mo><mrow><mrow><mo>(</mo><mrow><mi>u</mi><mo>,</mo><mi>v</mi></mrow><mo>)</mo></mrow></mrow><mo>)</mo></mrow></mrow></mstyle><mo></mo><mi>M</mi><mrow><mo>(</mo><mi>u</mi><mo>)</mo></mrow><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy=false>)</mo></mrow></math></div></p><p class="" id=d6532e503>This influence is determined by the map function <i>M</i> and the weighting function <i>W</i>. Only if the weighting function <i>W</i> is assumed to be constant over time can it be said that the influence <i>I</i>(<i>v</i>, <i>M</i>) is the influence of map <i>M</i> on vertex <i>v</i>. If <i>I</i>(<i>v</i>, <i>M</i>)  0, it is said that the map <i>M</i> activates vertex <i>v</i>, otherwise that the map <i>M</i> represses (or inactivates) vertex <i>v</i>. Now the most important definition of a vertex steady state under map <i>M</i> can be provided. Let <i>v</i> be an active vertex under map M. If map <i>M</i> activates <i>v</i>, then the state of <i>v</i> is a steady state under map <i>M</i>, else the state of <i>v</i> is a non-steady state under map <i>M</i>. Now let <i>v</i> be an inactive vertex under map <i>M</i>. If map <i>M</i> inactivates <i>v</i>, then the state of <i>v</i> is a steady state under map <i>M</i>, else the state of <i>v</i> is a non-steady state under map <i>M</i>. If all vertices are in steady state under map M, then map M is called a steady-state map. At each time period <i>j</i> for each non-steady-state vertex <i>v<sub>i</sub></i> under map <i>Y<sub>j</sub></i>, the current vertex state is changed to a forced state with probability <i>p<sub>i</sub></i>, and the current state remains unchanged with probability 1  <i>p<sub>i</sub></i>. Let <i>S</i> = {<i>v<sub>1</sub></i>, <i>v<sub>2</sub></i>,  , <i>v<sub>n</sub></i>} be a set of all non-steady-state vertices at time period <i>j</i>. The vertices chosen to change their state in a one-step transition constitute a random set <i>X</i>  <i>S</i>. To create a new map that is directly accessible from the current map <i>Y<sub>j</sub></i>, all vertices in <i>X</i> simultaneously change their state, whereas other vertices remain unchanged. Let P={<i>p</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>p</i><sub>2</sub>,  , <i>p</i><sub>n</sub>} be some vector of numbers such that 0  <i>p<sub>i</sub></i>  1, where <i>p<sub>i</sub></i> is referred to as the probability of a state change (firing) of the non-steady-state vertex <i>v<sub>i</sub></i>. For any <i>X</i>  <i>S</i>, let 1<i><sub>X</sub></i>: <i>S  B</i> be an indicator function such that 1<i><sub>X</sub></i>(<i>v<sub>i</sub></i>) = 1 if <i>v<sub>i</sub></i>  <i>X</i>, otherwise 1<i><sub>X</sub></i>(<i>v<sub>i</sub></i>) = 0. Hence, it can be assumed that each <i>v<sub>i</sub></i> acts independently to create a random set <i>X</i>. In the combinatorial model, steady-state vertices cannot flip. To construct a transition graph, the full set of maps <i>M</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>M</i><sub>2</sub>,  that are directly reachable from map <i>M</i> should be defined. Each pair (<i>M</i>, <i>M<sub>i</sub></i>) will correspond to one edge in the transition graph. What is the set of maps <i>M</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>M</i><sub>2</sub>,  that can be directly reached (by a one-step transition) from map <i>M</i>? To represent independence and asynchronicity, it is assumed that any random set of non-steady-state vertices <i>X</i>  <i>S</i> can produce the next map from the current map. Let <i>M<sub>X</sub></i> be a map such that <i>M<sub>X</sub></i>(<i>v</i>) = <i>F</i>(<i>v</i>, <i>M</i>) if <i>v</i>  <i>X</i> and <i>M<sub>X</sub></i>(<i>v</i>) = <i>M</i>(<i>v</i>) if <i>v</i>  <i>X</i>; then <i>M<sub>X</sub></i> can be said to be produced by <i>X</i> from <i>M</i>. In other words, the random set <i>X</i> of non-steady-state vertices produces the directly reachable map <i>M<sub>X</sub></i> from a map <i>M</i>. The weights of the edges from map <i>M</i> to map <i>M<sub>X</sub></i> are given by the probability defined by <a href="#e4">Equation (4)</a>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d6532e1376 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Random-walk network dynamics</h3><p class="" id=d6532e1381>Assume that whenever the process is in state <i>M</i>, there is a probability <i>p<sub>X</sub></i> that at the next step, it will be in state <i>M<sub>X</sub></i>. This probability is defined for each random set <i>X</i> of non-steady-state vertices of map <i>M</i>.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d6532e1407>Generalized random set update rules</h2><p class="" id=d6532e1410>It is well known that asynchronous and random set update rules are equivalent in the sense of global stable states<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. However, in the sense of the reachability of one state from another, they are not equivalent. <a href="#f1">Figure 1</a> show a Mace combinatorial model that illustrates this fact. The vertex <i>e</i> provides a constant level of repression for vertex <i>c</i>, that is, equal to -2. Let vertex <i>d</i> of the Mace model be active at the start. Then it can activate both middle vertices <i>a</i> and <i>b</i>. Due to repression, vertex <i>c</i> of the Mace model can be activated only if both middle vertices <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> are active simultaneously. Asynchronous (one at a time) updating excludes simultaneous activation of these vertices, but the random set update rule does not. A synchronous update rule does not exclude simultaneous activation of <i>a</i> and <i>b</i>, but it makes the system deterministic. The random set update rule is more general than either synchronous or asynchronous update rules because it allows all possible system evolution paths. Therefore, the transition graphs of both synchronous and asynchronous update rules are subgraphs of the random set update graph.</p><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure1.gif"><img alt="2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. Such models are called combinatorial models. Each combinatorial model consists of the interaction graph (combinatorial net) and the corresponding transition graph.</p><div class=section><a name=d6532e1475 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>The combinatorial model of an auto-repression</h3><p class="" id=d6532e1480>A negative auto-regulation or an auto-repression occurs when the products of a certain gene represse their own gene. This form of simple regulation serves as a basic building block for most important transcription networks<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a>,<a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. Auto-repression can produce oscillations. For example, embryonic stem cells fluctuate between high and low Nanog expression, and Nanog activity is auto-repressive<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. The model of auto-repression presented here and shown in <a href="#f3">Figure 3</a> and <a href="#f2">Figure 2a</a> also exhibits stochastic oscillating behavior. <a href="#f2">Figure 2a</a> presents an interaction graph G = (V, E) of the auto-repression model. The set V contains a single vertex <i>v</i><sub>1</sub>, and the set E consists of a single edge <i>e</i>1 = (<i>v</i>1, <i>v</i>1) from vertex v1 to itself. The weight of e1 is equal to -1. Let <i>M<sub>0</sub></i> be a starting map, and let <i>M</i><sub>0</sub>(<i>v<sub>1</sub></i>) = 0, i.e., <i>v<sub>1</sub></i> is inactive under <i>M</i><sub>0</sub>. Therefore, the influence of <i>M</i><sub>0</sub> on vertex <i>v</i><sub>1</sub> equals 0, <i>I</i>(<i>v</i>1, <i>M</i><sub>0</sub>) = 0. By <a href="#e2">Equation (2)</a>, <i>F</i>(<i>v</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>M</i><sub>0</sub>) = 1, and the state of vertex <i>v</i><sub>1</sub> is non-steady under map <i>M</i><sub>0</sub>. Let <i>p</i><sub>1</sub> = 1/2; then the state of vertex <i>v</i><sub>1</sub> can change to an active state with probability 1/2 and can remain unchanged with probability 1/2. The other state of the model is also non-steady. This means that there are only non-steady states in the model. Therefore, it will oscillate infinitely between 0 and 1. <a href="#f3">Figure 3</a> shows the full transition graph of the auto-repression model.</p><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a name=f2 class=n-a></a><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure2.gif"><img alt="2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure2.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure2.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 2. </h3><p class=first id=d6532e1615>The interaction graph of: (<b>a</b>) The auto-repression model; (<b>b</b>) The bi-stable switch model; (<b>c</b>) The Elowitz repressilator model; (<b>d</b>) The self-activation model.</p></div></div><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a name=f3 class=n-a></a><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure3.gif"><img alt="2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure3.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure3.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 3. The transition graph of an auto-repression model.</h3></div></div></div><div class=section><a name=d6532e1649 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Combinatorial model of a bi-stable switch</h3><p class="" id=d6532e1654>A bi-stable switch is a bi-stable gene regulatory network that is constructed from two mutually repressive genes<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. These are very common in nature and extensively used in synthetic biology<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a>,<a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>. The ordinary differential equations (ODEs) used to construct their mathematical models are a convenient way to analyze some small circuits in detail. In this research, techniques have been developed that can be used to construct models of large networks of bi-stable switches and to prove some of their important properties. For this purpose, a probabilistic coarse-scale modeling approach<sup><a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup> has been used here instead of fine-scale ODE modeling. The proposed model of a bi-stable switch illustrated in <a href="#f2">Figure 2b</a> and <a href="#f4">Figure 4</a> exhibits two steady maps. <a href="#f2">Figure 2b</a> presents an interaction graph <i>G</i> = (<i>V</i>, <i>E</i>) of the bi-stable switch model. The set <i>V</i> = {<i>v</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>v</i><sub>2</sub>} contains two vertices, and the set <i>E</i> contains two edges with weights of -1. Let the probability of firing a non-steady-state vertex be 1/2. <a href="#f4">Figure 4</a> presents the transition graph of the model. Maps 00 and 11 are non-steady-state, whereas maps 01 and 10 are steady-state. To show that a map <i>M</i> is steady-state, it is necessary to show that each vertex <i>v</i> is steady-state under map <i>M</i>. To show that a vertex <i>v</i> is steady-state under map <i>M</i>, one must compute the value of the influence <i>F</i>(<i>v</i>, <i>M</i>) of map <i>M</i> on vertex <i>v</i> and compare it to the state of <i>v</i> under map <i>M</i>, i.e. <i>M</i>(<i>v</i>). constitutively expressed gene is an example of self-activation. Such genes do not require any interaction to be active. A combinatorial model of self-activation consists of one vertex and no edges. In any case, the influence on it equals 0 because there are no other vertices. Therefore, a forced state of the vertex equals 1. Therefore, 1 is a steady state and 0 is a non-steady state. A vertex starting in steady state will stay in it infinitely. A vertex starting in a non-steady state will flip to steady state with probability p and stay in non-steady state with probability 1-p. The amount of time <i>T</i> which the vertex spends in non-steady state is the random variable. The distribution of this random variable is a shifted geometric distribution with parameter p.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2099><div class="disp-formula panel"><a name=d6532e2100 class=n-a></a><math display=block id=math6 overflow=scroll><mrow><mtext>Pr</mtext><mrow><mo>(</mo><mrow><mi>T</mi><mtext>=</mtext><mi>k</mi></mrow><mo>)</mo></mrow><mo>=</mo><mi>p</mi><msup><mrow><mrow><mo>(</mo><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo></mo><mi>p</mi></mrow><mo>)</mo></mrow></mrow><mrow><mi>k</mi><mo></mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msup></mrow></math></div></p><p class="" id=d6532e2143><div class="disp-formula panel"><a name=d6532e2144 class=n-a></a><math display=block id=math7 overflow=scroll><mrow><mtext>E</mtext><mrow><mo>[</mo><mi>T</mi><mo>]</mo></mrow><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>/</mo><mrow><mo>(</mo><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo></mo><mi>p</mi></mrow><mo>)</mo></mrow></mrow></math></div></p><p class="" id=d6532e2175><a href="#f2">Figure 2d</a> and <a href="#f6">Figure 6</a> represent the combinatorial model of self-activation.</p><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a name=f6 class=n-a></a><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure6.gif"><img alt="2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure6.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure6.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 6. The transition graph of a self-activation model.</h3></div></div></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d6532e2202>A network of bi-stable switches</h2><p class="" id=d6532e2205>An independent set (IS) in a graph is a set of vertices no two of which are adjacent. An independent set is called maximal (MIS) if there is no independent set that it contains properly. A Hopfield network whose stable states are exactly maximal independent sets was developed by Shrivastava<sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup>. An independent set in a graph is a clique in the complement graph, and vice versa. Therefore, cliques can be used to find or to enumerate MISs<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a>,<a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>. Finding independent sets (or cliques) has applications in various fields<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>. Combinatorial nets can be used to compute maximal independent sets of graphs in a distributed self-organization model. Stable states of a network of bi-stable switches derived from a graph are exactly maximal independent sets of its underlying graph. Now consider a simple graph <i>H</i>, that is, a graph without directed, multiple, or weighted edges, and without internal loops. Let <i>C</i>(<i>H</i>) denote the graph obtained from <i>H</i> by deleting each undirected edge (<i>u</i>, <i>v</i>) of <i>H</i> and adding instead of this edge two new directed edges (<i>u</i>, <i>v</i>) and (<i>v</i>, <i>u</i>). Let the set of vertices of <i>C</i>(<i>H</i>) be the same as the set of vertices of <i>H</i>. Let -1 be the weight of each edge of <i>C</i>(<i>H</i>). The bi-stable switch can be seen as a combinatorial net derived from a simple graph of order 2. For example, <a href="#f7">Figure 7b</a> illustrates the combinatorial network derived from the graph shown in <a href="#f7">Figure 7a</a>. The first switch is formed by the subgraph induced by the {1,2} set of vertices of the C(H) network. The second switch is formed by the {2,3} set of vertices. Vertex 2 is a common member of these switches, and therefore they can interact by means of this vertex. Each edge of an underlying graph corresponds to a switch in a derived network. If two incident edges share a common vertex, then the corresponding switches interact because this vertex has the same state in both switches. C(H) is referred to as the derived network of a bi-stable switch, and H is referred to as the underlying graph.</p><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a name=f7 class=n-a></a><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure7.gif"><img alt="2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure7.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/4990/2e58972d-2627-4789-8d67-f0ead5c37029_figure7.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 7. </h3><p class=first id=d6532e2287>The network C(H) derived from a graph H: (<b>a</b>) The graph H; (<b>b</b>) The network C(H) derived from the graph H.</p></div></div><p class="" id=d6532e2300>Lemma 1. A steady-state map <i>M</i> of a combinatorial net derived from a simple graph does not have two adjacent active vertices <i>u</i> and <i>v</i>.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2312>Proof 1. Assume by contradiction that u and v are adjacent active vertices; then <i>I</i>(<i>u</i>, <i>M</i>) &lt; 0, and the forced state of <i>u</i> is inactive. If the map (the network state) <i>M</i> is steady and the forced state is inactive, then the state of the vertex <i>u</i> is inactive, whereas by the assumption <i>u</i> is active.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2338>Lemma 2. If a map <i>M</i> of a combinatorial net derived from a simple graph is steady-state and some vertex is inactive, then there is at least one active vertex adjacent to it.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2344>Proof 2. Assume by contradiction that there are no active adjacent vertices of inactive vertex <i>v</i>; then the influence <i>I</i>(<i>v</i>, <i>M</i>) = 0, and the forced state of <i>v</i> is active. Hence the map <i>M</i> is steady-state, the forced state of the vertex is equal to its current state, and the state of the vertex is active, which contradicts the assumption.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2366>Lemma 3. Under a steady-state map M of the combinatorial net <i>C</i>(<i>H</i>) derived from a simple graph <i>H</i>, the set of all active vertices is a maximal independent set of <i>H</i>.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2381>Proof 3. Let us prove independence first. Assume by contradiction that the set of active vertices of some steady-state map C(H) is not an independent set of vertices of graph H. Then there is a pair of adjacent vertices in H which are simultaneously active under this steady-state map M. However, by lemma 1, there are no two adjacent active vertices under a steady-state map. This contradiction proves the independence of the set of active vertices under the steady-state map. Now consider the maximality of the independent set of active vertices under a steady-state map. Assume that there is an inactive vertex which is not adjacent to any active vertex. Therefore, this vertex can be added to this set to form a bigger independent set. But if such vertex exists, then there is an active vertex adjacent to this, by lemma 2, which contradicts the independence.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2384>Lemma 4. Any maximal independent set of a simple graph <i>H </i> forms the full set of active vertices of some steady-state map of the combinatorial net derived from <i>H</i>.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2393>Proof 4. The desired map <i>M</i> can be constructed as follows. Let all vertices from the maximal independent set be active under <i>M</i>, but let another vertices be inactive under <i>M</i>. Evidently, this map <i>M</i> is a steady-state map.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2409>Theorem. Let <i>C</i>(<i>H</i>) be the combinatorial net derived from a simple graph <i>H</i>; then the map <i>M</i> is steady-state if and only if the set of all active vertices under map <i>M</i> is the maximal independent set of vertices of graph <i>H</i>.</p><p class="" id=d6532e2431>Proof 5. Lemmas 3 and 4 prove the theorem.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d6532e2438>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d6532e2441>A similar approach to constructing Markov chains for interaction graphs was developed in earlier works by the authors for neural and gene regulatory networks<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a><a href="#ref-42">42</a></sup>. Both approaches can be used to construct Markov chains for gene regulatory networks. Systems of mutually repressive elements are ubiquitous in nature. A network of bi-stable switches can be used to create models of their stable states and of the self-evolution of such systems toward stable states.</p></div><div id=article1-back class=generated-article-footer><div class=back-section><a name=d6532e1 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d6673>Author contributions</h2><p class=metadata-entry><a name=d6532e120 class=n-a></a><p id=d6532e122> VS and MS developed the framework of combinatorial models.
