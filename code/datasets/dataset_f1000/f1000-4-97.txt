            <meta name="dc.description" content="Selecting a representative core collection (CC) is a proven and effective strategy for overcoming the expenses and difficulties of managing genetic resources in gene banks around the globe. Because of the diverse applications available for these sub-collections, several algorithms have been successfully implemented to construct them based on genotypic, phenotypic, passport or geographic data (either by individual datasets or by consensus). However, to the best of our knowledge, no single comprehensive dataset has been properly explored to date. Thus, researchers evaluate multiple datasets in order to construct representative CCs; this can be quite difficult, but one feasible solution for such an evaluation is to manage all available data as one discrete signal, which allows signal processing tools (SPTs) to be implemented during data analysis. In this research, we present a proof-of-concept study that shows the possibility of mapping to a discrete signal any type of data available from genetic resource collections in order to take advantage of SPTs for the construction of CCs that adequately represent the diversity of two crops. This method is referred to as 'SPT selection.' All available information for each element of the tested collections was analysed under this perspective and compared, when possible, with one of the most used algorithms for CC selection. Genotype-only SPT selection did not prove as effective as standard CC selection algorithms; however, the SPT approach can consider genotype alongside other types of information, which results in well-represented CCs that consider both the genotypic and agromorphological diversities present in original collections.Furthermore, SPT-based analysis can evaluate all available data both in a comprehensive manner and under different perspectives, and despite its limitations, the analysis renders satisfactory results. Thus, SPT-based algorithms for CC selection can be valuable in the field of genetic resources research, management and exploitation.">

A significant effort has been made on a global scale to preserve, characterize, distribute and utilise genetic resources in order to understand their biological phenomena and to confront the vulnerable situation regarding the sustainability of future human development<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a>,<a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. As the size of germoplasm collections increase, it becomes difficult to appropriately manage and extensively evaluate them<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>; thus, the core collection (CC) concept<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup> has become a fundamental genetic resource management approach and exploits the potential of a complete collection in terms of viable data management and monetary expenses<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d36964e219>Different CCs have different purposes, characteristics and evaluation criteria<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a>,<a href="#ref-9">9</a><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>; thus, several different algorithms and informatics tools have been developed and implemented<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup> with different approaches for satisfying particular needs of each CC. Because these CCs are constructed mainly on the basis of genotypic, phenotypic, passport or geographic data (either by individual datasets or by consensus)<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>, there is a lack of all-inclusive datasets; this limits the possibility of generating a CC that may satisfy most basic and applied genetic resource research programs. To the best of our knowledge, no single comprehensive dataset has been properly explored to date. One possible method to create a comprehensive dataset is to represent the available data as numerical values. Several methods exist that represent genomic information into numerical values<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup> and agromorphological traits (ATs) into scores<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. Through this mapping process, treating each data vector as a discrete signal that can, in turn, be analysed by signal processing tools (SPTs) is possible, thus providing an effective tool for a comprehensive evaluation of datasets. We present a proof-of-concept study that shows the possibility of mapping to a discrete signal any type of data available from genetic resource collections in order to take advantage of SPTs for CC selections; this possibility provides new decision-making criteria for genetic resource management and research.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d36964e254>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d36964e257 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Mapping data</h3><p class="" id=d36964e262>Each input data must be mapped to a numerical value. This is a fundamental process of the algorithm because it enables different datasets to be analysed together, regardless of their nature. In this manner, dissimilar passport data, single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), restriction fragment length polymorphisms (RFLPs), geographic information and phenotypical traits can be included in one comprehensive dataset. To consistently represent each data type, reference tables are implemented according to the nature of each particular data: genetic information (originally represented as character elements) is now represented by a numerical vector, and trait variation, simple sequence repeat (SSR) molecular markers and passport data can be represented as either binary or normalized data depending on the quantitative/qualitative nature of the data. The original data and reference tables for this study are available in <a href="#SM1">supplementary material 1</a>. Data transformation for this study rendered a matrix containing the representation of MC samples (<i>i</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>i</i><sub>2</sub>, <i>i</i><sub>3</sub>, <i>i</i><sub><i>n</i></sub>) with (<i>j</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>j</i><sub>2</sub>, <i>j</i><sub>3</sub>,  <i>j</i><sub><i>m</i></sub>) elements each, where <i>n</i> is the total number of samples, and <i>m</i> is the number of included samples <i>characteristics</i>, represented by a numerical value as <i>data</i><sub>(<i>i, j</i>)</sub>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d36964e330 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Signal construction</h3><p class="" id=d36964e335>Numerical representations of each <i>j</i>th <i>data</i> element can be treated as frequency values in <i>m data</i> time in such a manner that each <i>i</i>th sample is treated as a discrete signal. The <i>i</i> signal corresponds to the information behaviour from each sample. This perspective will enable the implementation of SPTs such as the discrete Fourier transform and power spectrum comparison. Although SPTs can be implemented on all data available for each sample, not all data elements contain the same informativeness value to discriminate between samples. To overcome the informative difference in each <i>j</i> element of <i>data</i>, a principal component analysis (PCA) can be performed to rearrange <i>data</i> into a new matrix that has the high informative elements of <i>data</i> at the beginning and that arranges subsequent elements according to their informativeness, discarding those whose variance equals 0. This process renders two new matrices: the original <i>characteristics</i> mapped vectors matrix (<i>x</i>) and rearranged variance value matrix (<i>X</i>). Matrix <i>X</i>, therefore, contains <i>n</i> samples that are formed by a numerical vector with <i>m</i>=<i>m</i>-(non informative <i>characteristics</i>).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d36964e392 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Fast Fourier transform</h3><p class="" id=d36964e397>The main objective of Fourier transform is the decomposition of any signal into a complex histogram of frequencies. Signal function is then represented as a vectorial function whose angle and magnitude determine a sampled point in the signal<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. The original Fourier model is expressed as follows:</p><p class="" id=d36964e404><div class="disp-formula panel"><a name=e1 class=n-a></a><math display=block id=math1 overflow=scroll><mrow><mover accent=true><mi>f</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mi></mi><mo stretchy=false>)</mo><mo>=</mo><mstyle displaystyle=true><mrow><munderover><mo></mo><mrow><mo></mo><mi></mi></mrow><mi></mi></munderover><mrow><mi>f</mi><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy=false>)</mo><msup><mi>e</mi><mrow><mo></mo><mn>2</mn><mi></mi><mi>i</mi><mi>x</mi><msub><mi></mi><mrow><mi>d</mi><mi>x</mi></mrow></msub></mrow></msup></mrow></mrow></mstyle><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy=false>)</mo></mrow></math></div></p><p class="" id=d36964e482>where <i>x</i> is the temporal variable, <i></i> it the frequential variable, <i>i</i> is a -1 square root and <i>e</i> is the natural exponent. From <a href="#e1">equation 1</a>, a derivative can be determined for any point <i></i> sampled in the signal.</p><p class="" id=d36964e504><div class="disp-formula panel"><a name=e2 class=n-a></a><math display=block id=math2 overflow=scroll><mrow><mi>f</mi><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy=false>)</mo><mo stretchy=false>[</mo><msup><mrow><mi>cos</mi></mrow><mrow><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mn>2</mn><mi></mi><mi>e</mi><mi></mi><mo stretchy=false>)</mo></mrow></msup><mo>+</mo><mi>i</mi><mo>*</mo><mtext></mtext><msup><mrow><mi>sin</mi></mrow><mrow><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mn>2</mn><mi></mi><mi>e</mi><mi></mi><mo stretchy=false>)</mo></mrow></msup><mo stretchy=false>]</mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mn>2</mn><mo stretchy=false>)</mo></mrow></math></div></p><p class="" id=d36964e581>Fourier transform can be implemented into any complex numerical series, but in a practical sense, the computational cost increases exponentially. Thus, fast Fourier transform (FFT) is more often implemented and can be defined according to Cooley-Tukey algorithm<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup> as follows:</p><p class="" id=d36964e588><div class="disp-formula panel"><a name=e3 class=n-a></a><math display=block id=math3 overflow=scroll><mrow><msub><mi>X</mi><mi>k</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mstyle displaystyle=true><munderover><mo></mo><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>0</mn></mrow><mrow><mi>N</mi><mo></mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></munderover><mrow><msub><mi>x</mi><mi>n</mi></msub><msup><mi>e</mi><mrow><mo></mo><mi>i</mi><mi></mi><mi>k</mi><mfrac><mi>n</mi><mi>N</mi></mfrac></mrow></msup></mrow></mstyle><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mn>3</mn><mo stretchy=false>)</mo></mrow></math></div></p><p class="" id=d36964e655>where <i>N</i> is the vector length, <i>x</i> is the temporal variable, <i>i</i> is a -1 square root and <i>e</i> is the natural exponent; in such matter that an euclidean representation - with the angle, magnitude and phase that corresponds to their position in the signal - exists for any signal dot.</p><p class="" id=d36964e670>Therefore, mapping any signal into a vectorial representation that contains information from every original signal dot is possible. From this complex vector, useful data can be retrieved to establish a comparison between them that indirectly represents the original signals juxtaposition<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d36964e678 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Distance matrix computation</h3><p class="" id=d36964e683>Inspired by the genomic signal processing alignment-free distance (GAFD) model<sup><a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>, each signal corresponding to the PCA-mapped accessions data in a set <sub><i>i</i></sub> was converted into its frequency representation by applying discrete Fourier transform. Its power spectrum <math id=math11 overflow=scroll><mrow><msub><mover accent=true><mi>F</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mi>i</mi></msub></mrow></math> was then computed. Subsequently, the distance <i>d</i>(<i>i</i>, <i>j</i>) for a given pair of comprehensive data signals was calculated by obtaining the mean squared error (MSE) of their respective power spectra:</p><p class="" id=d36964e715><div class="disp-formula panel"><a name=e4 class=n-a></a><math display=block id=math4 overflow=scroll><mrow><mi>D</mi><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mi>i</mi><mo>,</mo><mi>j</mi><mo stretchy=false>)</mo><mo>=</mo><msup><mrow><mstyle displaystyle=true><munder><mo></mo><mi>x</mi></munder><mrow><mrow><mo>(</mo><mrow><msub><mover accent=true><mi>F</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mi>i</mi></msub><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy=false>)</mo><mo></mo><msub><mover accent=true><mi>F</mi><mo>^</mo></mover><mi>j</mi></msub><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mi>x</mi><mo stretchy=false>)</mo></mrow><mo>)</mo></mrow></mrow></mstyle></mrow><mn>2</mn></msup><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo></mo><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mn>4</mn><mo stretchy=false>)</mo></mrow></math></div></p><p class="" id=d36964e798>Finally, a distance matrix (DM) was created by performing a pairwise comparison of all sequences in the set.</p><p class="" id=d36964e801>In parallel, we constructed a point-to-point (RAW) DM on the basis of the MSE given to a pair of signals prior to the PCA analysis.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d36964e806 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Core collection selection</h3><p class="" id=d36964e811>Selecting a CC by this method requires the generation of a DM for each sample of the MC; this provides the interrelations among samples and enables adequate selection. Beauv.) accession data were retrieved from the National Institute of Agrobiological Sciences (NIAS) <a target=xrefwindow href="http://www.gene.affrc.go.jp/databases_en.php" id=d36964e1810>http://www.gene.affrc.go.jp/databases_en.php</a>.</p><p class="" id=d36964e1814>According to the available data, different datasets were assembled. The substitution tables used during this mapping are presented as <a href="#SM1">supplementary material 1</a>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d36964e1847 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Implementation</h3><p class="" id=d36964e1852>All methodological procedures (except for CH, which was implemented according to the softwares default parameters and which is available for download at <a target=xrefwindow href="http://www.corehunter.org" id=d36964e1854>www.corehunter.org</a>) were performed using FreeMat v4.2 (<a target=xrefwindow href="http://www.freemat.sourceforge.net" id=d36964e1857>www.freemat.sourceforge.net</a>). All original codes are available as <a href="#SM1">supplementary material 1</a>.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d36964e1868>Results and discussion</h2><div class=section><a name=d36964e1871 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Selection and evaluation</h3><p class="" id=d36964e1876>The selection criteria were chosen to look for the best possible distribution of selected CC elements within the DM. Although hierarchical clustering has proven to be an effective method for determining collection structure and sampling CC<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup> and although it has been implemented in different crops<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup> and included in various selection algorithms<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>, hierarchical reconstruction presents the challenge of selecting an appropriate model for biological interpretation that can be applied to everything from unweighted pair-group averages to Markov models in Bayesian estimations<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. To avoid the challenge of selecting a reconstruction model, we decided to work strictly with the DM. By selecting the items described in this methodology, we aimed to retrieve representative elements from among the distributions of collections; however, because of its iterative nature, this methodology may render high redundancy under certain data distributions. Despite this limitation, the methodology has proven to be capable of selecting representative elements of the MCs diversity.</p><p class="" id=d36964e1898>Evaluation criteria were applied according to Odong <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup> without excluding the classic criteria used in <a href="#ref-9">9</a>,<a href="#ref-10">10</a>. The selected CCs render proper results in general terms. As expected, selected CCs did not always reach for optimal values for MD and CR, this is due the fact that it is not the aim of the selection method to render a CC with similar distribution to that of the MC, but to make sure to include as much diversity as possible.</p><p class="" id=d36964e1913>It is our belief that scoring the CC sets obtained with these methodologies will enable genetic resource banks to provide clear descriptors of what their CC strengths and limitations are with respect to the MC from which they come and will provide adequate tools for determining the possible purposes of the selected CCs.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d36964e1917 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Mapping</h3><p class="" id=d36964e1922>Although several representations of genotypic characteristics (particularly those involving DNA sequences<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>) have been proposed, real-number-based mappings have not been discarded; indeed, this type of mapping has been highly studied for signal analysis even when they share two principal problems: the preferential magnitude of some nucleotides and the non-equidistance of all nucleotides<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a>,<a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>. The arbitrary values selected for SNPss numerical representation of genotypes aim to maintain equidistant relations among purines and among pyrimidines in such a manner that the same distance is also preserved between at least one of them and the undetermined values. ATs are represented as binary data. This representation may prove useful for discrete data but requires a clustering procedure for continuous data. In this study, we arbitrarily generated clusters for the latter and then represented them as the former. Although this implementation may not be the most accurate regarding biological or agronomical significance, it serves as the first approach for testing the feasibility of the use of signal processing techniques when merging several datasets to construct one CC.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d36964e1940 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>RAW <i>versus</i> FFT</h3><p class="" id=d36964e1948>The RAW comparison establishes a distance value on the basis of the average distance between each mapped value on each element while the FFT power spectra implementation compares the signals in the frequency domain. Using FFT, establishing a DM on the basis of how data shifted rather than on the basis of average point-to-point comparisons was possible. The FFT approach provides a different DM, where its compared elements are clustered based on the similarity of the shift among data, regardless of whether the shift is in the opposite phase. We believe that this procedure may reveal additional information about the relations not only between elements but also between the individual components within each element.</p><p class="" id=d36964e1951>FFT comparisons of signals without PCA are a good approach for CC selection. Nevertheless, PCA implementation enables us to avoid possible misleads in random data arrangements, as, for example, palindromic data that could result in the same power spectra. Moreover, through PCA, we could organize data according to their levels of impact on the difference between accessions, which - when their magnitudes were obtained - inherently rendered a representation of informativity relations among values. This data behaviour was used as the element for pairwise comparisons, and although this approach clusters differently from RAW comparisons, we believe that it will provide a new perspective for CC selection and open the possibility of further data exploration.</p><p class="" id=d36964e1954>Our first approach was to measure the comparisons under different <i>K</i> values. On the contrary, there was a better overall score in phenotypic-only CCs when compared against phenotypic-only data. In the latter case, it should be kept in mind that comprehensive data also consider genotypic data; this could explain why better selections are made when only phenotypic data are considered because genotypic variations may reduce the impact of some phenotypic traits in the PCA analysis.</p><p class="" id=d36964e4321>The generation of a DM based on signal comparisons originating from mixed data construction enables us to explore one of the most interesting applications of this algorithm. By mapping genotypic and AT data, constructing a single signal with all data available for a particular accession is possible. The possibility of including genotypic data with phenotypic traits, geographical locations, climates, habitats, nutritional requirements, symbiotic relationships and so forth provides an opportunity for determining the best information to be included in the selection process in order to cope with the particular objectives for which that CC is being selected. This concept, in addition to adequate scoring systems, may prove useful in designing tailored CCs that comply with specific research/breeding objectives.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d36964e4328>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d36964e4331>The use of SPTs in CC selection, as presented in this algorithm, enables us to analyse all available data comprehensively and from different perspectives. Despite its limitations, this signal construction makes it possible to analyse all available data regarding each accession in CC selection with good results. The efficiency of SPTs in CC selection suggests that the use of these tools in MC analysis may provide useful information not only for CC but also for other purposes. The implementation of current and other SPTs in all-inclusive MC-mapped signals is worth further exploration, and we believe that it will be an important asset to genetic resource management and exploitation.</p></div><div id=article1-back class=generated-article-footer><div class=back-section><a name=d36964e1 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d37166>Author contributions</h2><p class=metadata-entry><a name=d36964e102 class=n-a></a><p id=d36964e104> EB conceived and designed the algorithm, performed the implementation, analysed the data and wrote the manuscript.
