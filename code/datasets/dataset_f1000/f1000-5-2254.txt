The traditional model for such training is typically a short (5–10 minute) warm up followed by 20–60 minutes of sustained moderate-intensity aerobic activity at 40–85% maximal aerobic capacity<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>. Such programs employing moderate-intensity continuous training (MICT) typically increase peak oxygen consumption (VO<sub>2</sub>) by 10–30%, with greater improvements associated with lower baseline fitness, higher intensity exercise, and longer program duration<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>. Additional benefits of MICT include modest blood pressure (BP) reduction, improved glucose and lipid metabolism, and mood elevation<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d16134e161>When performing aerobic exercise outside of a gym or cardiac rehabilitation (CR) program, individuals often vary the exercise intensity with short sprints as during a soccer or basketball game or in ascending hills on a bicycle. A program with such alternating short periods of high-intensity exercise at &gt;85–90% peak VO<sub>2</sub> interspersed among periods of lower intensity is known as high-intensity interval training (HIIT). Although HIIT has received relatively little attention as a therapeutic modality until the past decade, numerous recent reports have documented increases in peak VO<sub>2</sub> and improvement in several CV risk factors similar to or greater than those observed with MICT. This brief review will summarize these salutary effects of HIIT in healthy adults, those with CV risk factors, and those with established CVD.</p><p class="" id=d16134e170>HIIT can be divided into two distinct subtypes. Sprint interval training (SIT) typically consists of 4–6 cycles of 20–40-second “all out” sprints followed by 4–5 minutes of recovery. Because of SIT’s extreme intensity, most SIT studies have been limited to healthy young individuals. In older populations and those with CVD, HIIT has been employed in the form of aerobic interval training (AIT), which is performed at lower intensities than SIT but for longer periods. A typical AIT protocol consists of 4–6 cycles, each comprising 4 minutes of exercise at 80–95% peak capacity followed by 3–4 minutes of recovery, performed on a treadmill or cycle ergometer<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. The current review focuses on studies that have employed the AIT form of HIIT, given its greater applicability to the general population and individuals with CV risk factors and CVD. An advantage of both SIT and AIT over traditional MICT is their shorter time requirement to achieve a similar energy expenditure.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d16134e180>Animal studies</h2><p class="" id=d16134e183>Aerobic exercise programs using HIIT in animal models have shown large improvements in peak VO<sub>2</sub> similar or superior to those elicited by MICT<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. In addition, studies in mice and rats have demonstrated reductions in body mass, BP, and oxidative stress; augmented endothelial function; and improved metabolism in fat, liver, and skeletal muscle<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a>,<a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. Of note, Wistar rats that underwent 5 consecutive days of HIIT on a treadmill up to 7 days before experimental ischemic reperfusion injury sustained 35–50% smaller myocardial infarctions (MIs) than did sedentary rats<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. A similar reduction in infarct size has been demonstrated after MICT protocols<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d16134e211>Human studies</h2><div class=section><a name=d16134e214 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Healthy adults</h3><p class="" id=d16134e219>In healthy adults, a meta-analysis that included 723 healthy adults aged 18–45 years old from 28 controlled trials of 3–24 weeks’ duration showed a large absolute increase in peak VO<sub>2 </sub>of 4.9 mL/kg/minute following HIIT, which was greater than that seen with traditional MICT<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>. Larger increases were observed among individuals with lower baseline fitness and in interventions longer than 10 weeks. Improved skeletal muscle mitochondrial function and pulmonary oxygen uptake kinetics have been demonstrated after only six HIIT sessions over 2 weeks in healthy untrained men<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. Although occasional studies have failed to show superior effects of HIIT over MICT on peak VO<sub>2</sub>, these studies generally employed a program duration shorter than 10 weeks<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. Because equivalent energy expenditure can be accomplished in less time with HIIT than with MICT, this may allow shorter exercise sessions with HIIT, thereby improving adherence.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d16134e241 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Obesity</h3><p class="" id=d16134e246>Given the frequent association of obesity with low fitness and with CV risk factors such as hypertension, glucose intolerance, and dyslipidemia, a strong rationale exists for aerobic exercise training in such individuals. In three studies 3–6 months in duration, similar improvements in body mass index and waist circumference were observed in overweight/obese participants following both MICT and HIIT<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a>–<a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. The equivalent anthropometric changes might be attributed to the similar total energy expenditure with the two programs in those studies.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d16134e257 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Hypertension</h3><p class="" id=d16134e262>Traditional MICT exerts significant reduction of BP in hypertensive adults, especially those with suboptimal baseline BP control<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. In such individuals who were not receiving antihypertensive drugs, HIIT for ≥12 weeks appears to provide a similar antihypertensive effect<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. For example, 16 weeks of MICT or HIIT led to similar reductions of systolic (10 mmHg) and diastolic (6 mmHg) BP in middle-aged adults with elevated BP at baseline<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. A 12-week HIIT program in overweight/obese adolescents with borderline hypertension also elicited significant reductions in both systolic BP (9 mmHg) and diastolic BP (6 mmHg)<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d16134e282 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Lipids</h3><p class="" id=d16134e287>Among individuals with dyslipidemia, traditional MICT typically elicits modest increases in high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C) but exerts no consistent effect on low-density lipoprotein cholesterol or triglycerides<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. Similar effects on blood lipids are observed with HIIT<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. Modest increases of HDL-C with HIIT required 8 or more weeks of training and occurred in only 3 of 10 such studies<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. Given these inconsistent effects on HDL-C without benefit on other lipid fractions, neither HIIT nor MICT can generally be recommended as primary therapy for the treatment of dyslipidemia.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d16134e304 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Glucose metabolism</h3><p class="" id=d16134e309>Substantial evidence suggests that HIIT improves glucose metabolism to a similar or greater extent than does MICT. Augmented insulin sensitivity occurs as early as 2 weeks after the initiation of HIIT<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup> and has been shown in both healthy adults<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup> and those with metabolic syndrome<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. Improved oral glucose tolerance test results have been consistently reported after HIIT<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a>,<a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. Fasting blood glucose did not change in studies of less than 12 weeks’ duration but declined in four of seven longer studies<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. Small studies have found improved glycemic control in patients with type 2 diabetes<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a>–<a href="#ref-23">23</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d16134e347 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Adults with heart disease</h3><p class="" id=d16134e352>Incontrovertible evidence for the benefits of MICT in patients with CVD has accumulated over the past four decades through hospital-based CR programs involving patients after acute MI, coronary revascularization, valvular surgery, and those with chronic heart failure (CHF). Similar, though less extensively studied, salutary effects of HIIT have been demonstrated in patients with CVD and are reviewed below.</p><p class="" id=d16134e355> <b> <i>Coronary artery disease.</i> </b> Multiple studies have documented the benefit of HIIT in patients with coronary artery disease (CAD), the most prevalent form of heart disease in developed countries. A meta-analysis of 229 patients with CAD demonstrated a 1.5 mL/kg/minute larger mean increase in peak VO<sub>2</sub> with HIIT than with MICT<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>. A larger meta-analysis that included 472 CAD patients showed a similarly augmented gain in peak VO<sub>2</sub> (1.8 mL/kg/minute mean difference) compared with MICT<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. In this latter analysis, no differences in blood glucose or lipids were observed between the groups. However, resting heart rate (-1.8 beats/minute) and body weight (-0.5 kg) declined slightly after MICT but not HIIT. A recently published randomized trial also showed a significantly larger increase in peak VO<sub>2</sub> after 8 weeks of HIIT compared to MICT (4.5 versus 2.5 mL/kg/minute), respectively<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d16134e386> <b> <i>Chronic heart failure.</i> </b> Although CR was not approved until 2014 by the Center for Medicare and Medicaid Services for patients with CHF, considerable evidence has accrued on the benefits of HIIT in this population. One of the first studies to examine the effect of HIIT in CHF patients showed a remarkable 46% increase in peak VO<sub>2</sub> in nine older patients (mean 75 years) with post-MI CHF versus an increase of only 14% with MICT<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. Favorable left ventricular remodeling and increased left ventricular ejection fraction occurred only after HIIT. Three recent meta-analyses have confirmed a larger increase in peak VO<sub>2</sub> with HIIT than MICT in CHF patients<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a>–<a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. Haykowsky <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup> showed a mean 2.1 mL/kg/minute larger increase in peak VO<sub>2</sub> with HIIT in seven studies encompassing 168 patients. In a meta-analysis by Smart <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup> involving 212 patients who performed HIIT and 66 who performed MICT, the mean improvement in peak VO<sub>2</sub> was 1.0 mL/kg/minute larger after HIIT. A very large meta-analysis that included 74 exercise-based CR trials involving 5877 participants showed a 23% mean increase in peak VO<sub>2</sub> among patients receiving HIT versus a 13% increase in those undergoing MICT<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. Of note, withdrawal from the CR program was lower with HIIT. It should be noted that all of the studies and meta-analyses cited above excluded patients with CHF and preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF), who comprise 30–40% of the CHF population. Studies of HIIT are clearly needed in this latter group.</p><p class="" id=d16134e439> <b> <i>Heart transplant recipients.</i> </b> Although data remain limited regarding the effects of HIIT in heart transplant recipients, available evidence suggests a similar benefit as seen with MICT. Compared to a usual care control group, 24 stable heart transplant recipients 1–8 years post transplantation demonstrated a 3.6 mL/kg/minute higher peak VO<sub>2</sub> after 1 year of HIIT<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. In a study of 16 patients ≥12 months post transplantation, 8 weeks of HIIT induced a larger increase in peak VO<sub>2</sub> than did MICT (4.9 versus 2.6 mL/kg/minute)<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d16134e463 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Quality of life</h3><p class="" id=d16134e468>Although quality of life (QOL) is often considered a “softer” outcome variable than changes in physiological indices, it is probably a more relevant variable to patients. In this context, both MICT and HIIT have elicited improvement in QOL in a variety of clinical settings. For example, improvement in QOL after HIIT has been reported in patients with CAD<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup> and CHF<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>, cardiac transplant recipients<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>, and generally healthy older adults<sup><a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>. Such improvement in QOL may reinforce the patient’s motivation to continue exercise participation.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d16134e488 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Safety of HIIT</h3><p class="" id=d16134e493>An important concern in prescribing any exercise program, especially in older adults and patients with CVD or other comorbidities, is safety. To date, the evidence suggests that HIIT is well tolerated to a similar extent as MICT. In the large meta-analysis of CHF patients previously mentioned, no deaths were attributed to exercise training regardless of intensity<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. A similar finding was reported in an analysis of 4846 CAD patients from three Norwegian CR centers<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. In this study, major complications were one per 129,456 hours of MICT and one per 23,182 hours of HIIT. Nevertheless, HIIT may not be appropriate for frail older adults, those with significant orthopedic, neurological, or balance difficulties, those with marked obesity, or those with severe deconditioning. In such individuals, an initial low-to-moderate-intensity exercise program appears to be preferable.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d16134e505 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Mechanistic insights</h3><p class="" id=d16134e510>Given the substantial benefits of HIIT in a wide variety of settings, insights into the biochemical and molecular adaptations accompanying HIIT are worthy of inquiry. In patients with obesity, metabolic syndrome, or CHF, HIIT induced increases in peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor γ co-activator (PGC)-1α, a master regulator of oxidative phenotype<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a>,<a href="#ref-13">13</a>,<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup> and muscle mitochondrial capacity<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. In addition, sarcoplasmic reticulum calcium uptake was augmented by HIIT but not MICT, likely owing to an increased ability to perform high-intensity muscle contractions<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a>,<a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. Another salutary effect of HIIT is to enhance flow-mediated dilation of the brachial artery, a measure of vascular endothelial function mediated in part by increased nitric oxide availability<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a>,<a href="#ref-13">13</a>,<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. Finally, increased cardiac stroke volume has been reported after HIIT in obese women<sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup>, sedentary middle-aged adults<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>, and patients with CHF<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d16134e567>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d16134e570>Although less thoroughly investigated than traditional aerobic training programs using MICT, HIIT appears to share similar salutary effects, with generally larger increases in peak VO<sub>2</sub>, without a significant difference in serious adverse events in appropriately selected individuals. Future studies should explore the long-term effects of HIIT on health outcomes as well as its effects when combined with dietary, drug, and cardiac device interventions in patients with CV disease.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d16134e579>Disclaimer</h2><p class="" id=d16134e582>The views expressed are those of the author and do not necessarily represent those of the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute or the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services.</p></div><div id=article1-back class=generated-article-footer><div class=back-section><a name=d16134e1 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d16277>Competing interests</h2><p class=metadata-entry><a name=d16134e75 class=n-a></a><p id=d16134e77> The author declares that he has no competing interests.</p></p></div><div class=back-section><a name=d16134e1 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d16279>Grant information</h2><p>The author(s) declared that no grants were involved in supporting this work.</p></div><div class=back-section><a name=d16134e589 class=n-a></a><span class="research-layout prime-recommended-wrapper reference-heading"><span class="f1r-icon icon-79_faculty_recommended_badge red vmiddle default-cursor"></span><span class="prime-red big">F1000 recommended</span></span><h2 class=main-title id=d16397>References</h2><div class="section ref-list"><a name=d16134e589 class=n-a></a><ul><li><a name=ref-1 class=n-a></a><span class=label>1. </span>&nbsp;<span class=citation><a name=d16134e596 class=n-a></a>Pollock ML, Gaesser GA, Butcher JD, <i> et al.</i>: American College of Sports Medicine Position Stand.
