The most predominant Gram-negative bacilli isolates was E.coli (54.4%), followed by Klebsiella species (29.5%). More than fifty percent of the isolates were Carbapenem resistant. E.coli was the most bacteria that harbored the blaoxa48 followed by Klebsiella species. blaIMP gene was harbored only by E.coli.
Conclusion: The percentage of resistance to Carbapenems due to production of Carbapenemase enzymes is very high in Sudan.BlaOXA48 gene is more predominant than blaIMP in this study."

Beta-lactamases can be classified according to their functional and molecular properties. Functional classification divides beta-lactamases into four major functional groups (1, 2, 3, and 4), whereas in molecular classification Ambler and others have classified beta-lactamases into four classes (AD) based on amino acid sequence. In addition, based on the active site of the enzyme, they classified beta-lactamases into serine-beta-lactamases (class A,C and D) and metallo-beta-lactamases (class B)<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.microrao.com/micronotes/pg/carbapenemases.pdf" id=d95454e246>https://www.microrao.com/micronotes/pg/carbapenemases.pdf</a></p><p class="" id=d95454e249>Oxacillinases (OXAs) (class D), so called because of their ability to hydrolyze Oxacillin, are classified according to their hydrolysis spectrum. Broad-spectrum OXA enzymes are able to hydrolyze carbapenems. This family is plasmid encoded and they have been identied mainly in <i>Enterobacteriaceae</i> and <i>Pseudomonas aeruginosa</i>. Currently, there are 239 OXA enzymes, of which at least 37 are carbapenemases and at least 9 are extended spectrum beta-lactamases. The first OXA enzyme with carbapenemase activity was detected in 1985 in an <i>Acinetobacter baumanii</i> isolate from Scotland, and OXA-48 cluster is the most important among class D carbapenemases<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a>,<a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.microrao.com/micronotes/pg/carbapenemases.pdf" id=d95454e267>https://www.microrao.com/micronotes/pg/carbapenemases.pdf</a></p><p class="" id=d95454e270>IMP (for active on Imipenem) a metallo-beta-lactamase (class B), was first detected in 1990 in <i>P. aeruginosa</i> isolate from Japan, and then reported in <i>Serratia marcescens</i> isolate in Japan in 1991, IMP-2 was observed in Italy in <i>A.baumannii.</i> Currently there are 37 known IMP types which are most commonly seen in <i>P. aeruginosa and A.baumannii</i> isolates, but have also been reported in most <i>Enterobacteriaceae</i> members<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.microrao.com/micronotes/pg/carbapenemases.pdf" id=d95454e292>https://www.microrao.com/micronotes/pg/carbapenemases.pdf</a></p><p class="" id=d95454e295>One of the risk factors of acquiring carbapenem resistant bacteria is the increased consumption of carbapenems . Many patients are most likely affected by carbapenem resistant bacteria, including those who have poor immunity, prolonged hospitalization, admission to ICU, an indwelling medical device, and multiple exposures to different antibiotics. Also the increased frequency of international travel for work, leisure and migration contributes to their spread, and most of the carbapenemases are found on transferable plasmids, which are highly transmissible<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.safetyandquality.gov.au/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/MRGN-Guide-Enterobacteriaceae-PDF-1.89MB.pdf" id=d95454e301>https://www.safetyandquality.gov.au/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/MRGN-Guide-Enterobacteriaceae-PDF-1.89MB.pdf</a></p><p class="" id=d95454e305>The prevalence of carbapenemase producing gram negative bacteria is increasingly reported in Sudan and Africa. Manenzhe <i>et al</i>. (2014) reported about 83 studies conducted in Africa which showed that the prevalence of carbapenemase producer isolates in hospital settings ranged from 2.3% to 67.7% in North Africa and from 9% to 60% in sub-Saharan Africa. Oxacillinases especially bla<sub>OXA48</sub> was the most predominant in the whole country<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d95454e318> In Sudan there are limited reports of carbapenemase producers, therefore the present study was performed<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>. This study aimed to detect carbapenem resistant Gram-negative bacilli (using conventional biochemical tests and chromogenic agar media), determine the antimicrobial susceptibility pattern of the isolates against carbapenems, detect carbapenemase enzyme production for the resistant isolates (using phenotypic methods, Modified Hodge Test), and detect bla<sub>OXA 48</sub> and bla<sub>IMP</sub> genes for carbapenem resistant isolates (using PCR, genotypic methods).</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d95454e334>Methods</h2><p class="" id=d95454e337>This was cross sectional laboratory based study, conducted in Khartoum state in the period from February to August 2016. Ethical clearance was obtained from SUMASRI (University of Medical Sciences and Technology Sudanese Institute of Medical and Scientific Research) Institutional Review Board (SIRB); (IRB No: 00008867), which ensures that all ethical considerations for conducting the research in a way that protects patients confidentiality and privacy are followed. Informed consent was obtained from the hospital laboratories (laboratory manager) after providing them with the ethical clearance to collect samples during routine procedures from the microbiology laboratories. Participants privacy and confidentially was protected for all samples; personal information was not of great value in the current study and was thus not taken.</p><p class="" id=d95454e340>One hundred and forty nine Gram-negative bacilli were isolated from 147 different clinical specimens that were collected from patients attending different hospitals in Khartoum state. Blood agar, MacConkey agar, CLED media, and XLD media were used for primary plating depending on the type of clinical specimens, cultures were examined macroscopically for colonial morphology, and Gram stain was performed from suspected colonies. All Gram-negative bacilli isolates were selected then subcultured on MacConkey agar media for purity and further identification tests, and incubated at 37<sup></sup> overnight. Chromogenic agar media and different standard biochemical reactions, including oxidase, Kligler Iron Agar (KIA), urease production, citrate utilization, indole production, and motility tests were performed for their identification (all media mentioned above were obtained from LAB M, UK, except chromogenic media which was obtained from Laboratories Flow Media, Sudan)<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>.</p><div class=section><a name=d95454e350 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Standard antimicrobial susceptibility testing</h3><p class="" id=d95454e355>Antimicrobial susceptibility testing to carbapenem antibiotic was performed for all Gram-negative bacilli isolates using the disc diffusion method, according to the Clinical Laboratory Standards Institute Guidelines<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>. Bacterial colonies were suspended in sterile normal saline and compared with McFarland standard and cultured in Muller-Hinton agar media (media were obtained from Pronadisa Laboratories Conda., Spain) using a sterile cotton swab. After overnight incubation at 37<sup></sup>C, zone of inhibition was measured and the reading was compared to the sheet provided by manufacturer<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d95454e369>Gram-negative isolates showing resistant zones to Meropenem were tested for carbapenemase enzyme production using MHT and PCR.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d95454e373 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Modified Hodge Test (MHT)</h3><p class="" id=d95454e378>MHT was used to detect carbapenemase enzyme production<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. McFarland 0.5 dilution of <i>E.coli</i> (ATCC 25922) was prepared in 5 ml of sterile saline. The suspension was diluted to 1:10 by adding 0.5 ml of the 0.5 McFarland (Oxoid, UK) to 4.5 ml of sterile saline. A lawn of the 1:10 dilution of <i>E.coli</i> was streaked onto a Mueller Hinton agar plate using a sterile cotton swab. A 10 g Meropenem susceptibility disk (Hi media, India) was placed on the center of the test area. The tested bacilli was streaked in a straight line from the edge of the disk to the edge of the plate. The plate was incubated overnight at 37C in ambient air (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>)<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>.</p><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/13462/c2349353-df07-4451-9f7f-ff7fc19f2bc5_figure1.gif"><img alt="c2349353-df07-4451-9f7f-ff7fc19f2bc5_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/13462/c2349353-df07-4451-9f7f-ff7fc19f2bc5_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. The pellet was resuspended in 100 l of phosphate buffer saline. The tubes were placed at 95C for 15 minutes then centrifuged at &gt;10.000 rpm for 5 minutes to pellet the cellular debris. Then the supernatant (lysate) was transferred into a labelled 1.5 ml safe-lock. The lysate was stored at -20 to 4C. <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.dkfz.de/gpcf/fileadmin/ccontrol/lysate_Protocol_DKFZ.pdf" id=d95454e465>https://www.dkfz.de/gpcf/fileadmin/ccontrol/lysate_Protocol_DKFZ.pdf</a></p><p class="" id=d95454e468><b><i>PCR.</i></b> A multiplex conventional PCR was designed to detect bla<sub>IMP</sub> and bla<sub>OXA 48</sub> using specific primers for each. This may be due to the difference in sample size and study area.</p><p class="" id=d95454e1638>The resistance to carbapenem antibiotics in this study was 50.3%, which is not in agreement with a previous study conducted in India by Henkhoneng Mate, <i>et al</i>., the study conducted by Hayajneh, <i>et al.</i> in Jordan, and the study conducted by Gladstone <i>et al.</i>, which showed that resistance to carbapenem antibiotics was 30%, 1.6%, and 12.2% respectively<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-13">13</a>,<a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. This may be due to the uncontrolled and misuse of antibiotics including broad spectrum antibiotics in our study area (Sudan)<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d95454e1664>Urine samples had the maximum number of carbapenem resistant isolates, which was similar to the study conducted by Henkhoneng Mate, <i>et al</i> in India<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d95454e1674>carbapenemase production constituted 42 isolates (56%) of the total carbapenem resistant isolates, which disagreed with the study conducted in Tanzania 2013. That study showed that 35% of the resistant isolates were carbapenemase producers; this may be to the difference in the methods used for enzyme detection, since the previous study used only genotypic methods, while the present study used both phenotypic and genotypic methods<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. A study conducted in India 2015 by Panduragan, <i>et al</i> reported that 62% of isolates produced carbapenemases, found using both phenotypic and genotypic methods, which is agreed with the current study, which reported a near percentage (56%)<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d95454e1689>In this study, the most carbapenemase producing organism was <i>E. coli</i>, which disagrees with a previous study done by Mushi <i>et al</i>, who reported that <i>K. pneumoniae</i> was the most predominant carbapenemase producer<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d95454e1705>Regarding MHT, 50.6% of the total carbapenem resistant isolates were positive for carbapenemase production. This was not in agreement with a study conducted in India 2015 by Pandarangan, <i>et al</i>, who reported lower percentage (30.5%), and also disagreed with the study conducted by Henkhoneng Mate, <i>et al</i> in the same country, which reported a higher percentage (60.4%)<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>. This may be due to the difference in study area, which may affect the types and percentage of carbapenemase enzymes according to their spread.</p><p class="" id=d95454e1721>Regarding bla<sub>OXA48</sub> gene, 17.3% of the isolates harboured this gene, which disagreed with a previous study done by Memish, <i>et al</i> in Saudi Arabia 2015. This previous study showed a higher percentage (61.3%) of isolates with the bla<sub>OXA48</sub> gene, which may be due to the different study area<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d95454e1737>bla<sub>IMP</sub> gene was harboured by 6.7% of the resistant isolates, which also disagreed with the study done in Tanzania (2013) and Thailand (2008). These studies showed that 21.6% and 15.4% of the resistant isolates, respectively, harboured the bla<sub>IMP</sub> gene<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a>,<a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. In addition, a study performed by Nasr El-din in Egypt 2014 revealed that the bla<sub>IMP</sub> gene was absent from isolates<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. This may be due to the different study area, the difference in sample size and the five different PCR assays that were performed in the study done in Tanzania, thus increasing the sensitivity and specificity compared to the present study.</p><p class="" id=d95454e1760>In this study, bla<sub>OXA 48</sub> gene was the most predominant carbapenemase harboured by the resistant isolates. This is in agreement with the study performed by Memish <i>et al</i> in Saudi Arabia 2015<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. However, our results disagreed with the study performed in Tanzania 2013, which showed that bla<sub>IMP</sub> gene was the most predominant<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. This may be due to the difference in sample size and the five different PCR assays that were performed in this study.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d95454e1783>Conclusion</h2><p class="" id=d95454e1786>This study concludes that the percentage of resistance to carbapenems in Khartoum state, Sudan, is very high and must be taken in consideration by the Sudanese government, and any large organization that can help in the control and prevention of carbapenemases, such as the World Health Organisation. <i>E. coli</i>, followed by <i>Klebsiella species</i>, were the most carbapenem resistant organisms and the largest carbapenemase producers<i>.</i> MHT is a simple method that can detect many types of carbapenemase enzymes, but it cannot detect some types of and does not specify the type of enzymes produced. In contrast, PCR is more sensitive, rapid and specific method for detection of specific types of carbapenemase enzymes. Additionally in the present study, the percentage of Gram-negative bacilli that produce bla<sub>OXA48</sub> gene was more than those producing bla<sub>IMP</sub>; however, one of the isolates harboured both bla<sub>OXA48</sub> and bla<sub>IMP</sub> genes. In this study, some Gram-negative bacilli were positive for carbapenemase enzyme production by both MHT and PCR, some were negative by MHT and positive by PCR, and some of them were positive by MHT and negative by PCR (this study did not detect all carbapenemase genes using PCR; thus those positive by MHT but negative by PCR may process other type of carbapenemase enzymes rather than OXA48 and IMP. Although this may also be because the gene is not expressed).</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d95454e1815>Recommendations</h2><div class=list><a name=d95454e1818 class=n-a></a><ul><li><p id=d95454e1822>A larger sample size should be tested to cover a wider range of isolates.</p></li><li><p id=d95454e1828>Other specific tests for detection of carbapenemase enzymes should be used, such as EDTA disc synergy test, MDI, RDS, MBL E test, more primers should be used to detect most types of carbapenemase enzymes using PCR, and different types of PCR assays should be coupled with each other to increase the sensitivity for enzyme detection.</p></li><li><p id=d95454e1834>Detection of carbapenemase producers should be introduced as routine tests in microbiology labs for rapid detection of resistant isolates and to control their spread, especially for newly admitted patients to the hospitals.</p></li><li><p id=d95454e1840>Prevention and control programs of carbapenem resistant Gram-negative bacteria should be performed to prevent the spread of carbapenemase producers, which includes appropriate use of antimicrobials and facility-level prevention strategies, as recommended by the CDC.</p></li></ul></div><div class=section><a name=d95454e1845 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Data availability</h3><p class="" id=d95454e1850>Dataset 1: Raw data for the 147 specimens analysed showing the results of the genotypic and phenotypic tests.
