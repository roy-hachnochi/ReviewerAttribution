Discovery of targetable lesions in subsets of patients enables the introduction of new treatment modalities into clinical practice and stimulates the transfer of modern methods from research laboratories to routine practice. </div> <div class=abstract-for-mobile> <div class="margin-top-30 padding-bottom-30 research-layout is-centered"> <button class="primary orange-text white-bg bigger-text abstract-expand-button-mobile with-border show" style="display: none;"> READ ALL <span class="f1r-icon icon-14_more_small orange vmiddle big"></span> </button> <button class="primary orange-text white-bg bigger-text abstract-expand-button-mobile with-border hide"> READ LESS <span class="f1r-icon icon-10_less_small orange vmiddle big"></span> </button> </div> </div> </div> <div class=clearfix></div> <div class="article-context no-divider"> <div class="article-abstract article-page-general-text-mobile research-layout generated-article-body"> <h2 class=main-title>Keywords</h2> <p class="u-mb--0 u-pb--2"> acute lymphoblastic leukemia, children, massive parallel sequencing, new BCP-ALL subtypes </p> </div> </div> <div class=article-information> <span class="info-separation padding-bottom"> <div id=corresponding-author-icon class="email-icon float-left"> <span class="f1r-icon icon-6_email orange"></span> <div id=corresponding-author-window class="margin-top-20 popup-window-wrapper is-hidden"> <div class="popup-window corresponding-authors-popup"> <div class=corresponding-author-container> <div class="popup-window-title small">Corresponding Author(s)</div> <div class=authors> Jan Star&yacute; Six major genetic subtypes, altogether accounting for 70% to 75% of BCP-ALL, have been defined by high hyperdiploidy (51 to 67 chromosomes per leukemic cell), hypodiploidy (fewer than 45 chromosomes per leukemic cell), and <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>, <i>TCF3-PBX1</i>, <i>BCR-ABL1</i>, and <i>KMT2A</i> gene-involving fusions. These genetic aberrations have been characterized as first leukemogenic hits which are present in all cells comprising the leukemic clone, defining its key biological features as well as impacting the clinical character of respective BCP-ALL subtypes. Their identification has remained important until today for diagnosis specification and, in some of them, for risk classification and targeted therapy<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20239e215>Rapid progress of modern genetic techniques yielded important discoveries in various human diseases, including BCP-ALL. Genome-wide profiling using array-based comparative genomic hybridization, single-nucleotide polymorphism arrays, and massive parallel sequencing (MPS) of whole genomes, whole exomes, and whole transcriptomes (RNA sequencing, or RNA-seq) resulted in the identification of novel recurrent genetic aberrations and patterns. Among them, several prognostically significant and druggable aberrations which have started to be implemented into risk-stratification algorithms and targeted therapy have been described<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. Moreover, together with genome-wide gene expression profiling on microarrays and by RNA-seq, modern genomic studies led to the identification of novel biologically and clinically relevant BCP-ALL subtypes<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. The so-called B-other ALL, a genetically and clinically heterogeneous subset of leukemias accounting for up to 30% of BCP-ALL (herein defined by negativity for all six above-mentioned classifying aberrations), was further dissected and better characterized. This review focuses on recent findings related to five novel BCP-ALL subtypes and selected clinically relevant genetic aberrations/patterns.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d20239e229>New BCP-ALL subtypes</h2><p class="" id=d20239e232>Similar to the above-mentioned classical BCP-ALL subtypes, the five novel subtypes are distinguishable by their gene expression signatures; however, only three of them are also defined by the presence of a subtype-specific genetic aberration.</p><p class="" id=d20239e235>The first subtype, <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like/Philadelphia chromosome-like (Ph-like) ALL (hereafter <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like ALL), was already described in the pre-MPS era thanks to gene expression profiling on microarrays<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a>,<a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. It is defined by a gene expression signature similar to that of <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-positive ALL. This gene expression signature likely results from the activation of kinase signaling pathways, which, however, is not triggered by a single specific genetic aberration as in the case of <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-positive ALL. On the contrary, a very wide spectrum of different genetic aberrations likely inducing this signature has been described in <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like ALL<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>. The aberrations affect genes encoding kinases (for example, <i>JAK1</i>, <i>JAK2</i>, <i>JAK3</i>, <i>ABL1</i>, <i>ABL2</i>, and <i>TYK2</i>), cytokine and growth factor receptors (for example, <i>CRLF2</i>, <i>PDGFRB</i>, <i>EPOR</i>, <i>IL7R</i>, <i>CSF1R</i>, <i>NTRK3</i>, and <i>FLT3</i>), and signaling mediators and regulators (<i>KRAS</i>, <i>NRAS</i>, <i>BRAF</i>, <i>PTPN11</i>, <i>NF1</i>, and <i>SH2B3</i>). These aberrations can be of various types, including both small-scale (deletions, insertions, substitutions, and complex mutations) and large-scale (chromosomal translocations and deletions) mutations which may generate fusion genes; of note, the majority of kinase/cytokine-receptor genes (<i>JAK2</i>, <i>ABL1</i>, and so on) can be fused to several different fusion partners. Such kinase-signaling aberrations can be found in 39% to 91% of <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like ALL<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-12">12</a>,<a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>; however, some of them also occur in B-other ALL not classified as <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a>,<a href="#ref-14">14</a>,<a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20239e361>This ALL subtype was discovered independently by two studies. Although <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like and Ph-like ALL, described by Dutch and American investigators, respectively, are now considered a single subtype, their definition is not fully consistent<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a>,<a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. Some patients can be classified discordantly on the basis of classification approaches used by these two studies<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>. Several factors may contribute to this discrepancy, including methodological differences in gene expression data analysis, different composition of discovery cohorts (with respect to representation of various BCP-ALL subtypes, risk groups, and ethnicities), and the limited specificity of <i>BCR-ABL1</i> gene expression signature.</p><p class="" id=d20239e381> <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like ALL is the only novel subtype that was already added as a provisional entity into the 2016 update of World Health Organization (WHO) ALL classification<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. It occurs in about 10% to 15% of pediatric BCP-ALL and accounts for about 15% to 35% of the B-other ALL group and its frequency increases with age. Both above-mentioned <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like discovery studies reported its inferior outcome<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a>,<a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. Although recent studies demonstrate that the overall survival of children with Ph-like ALL treated according to protocols employing minimal residual disease (MRD)-based risk stratification is not significantly inferior to that of non-<i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like cases, such patients commonly exhibit inferior early response to therapy requiring treatment intensification<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. Of note, it has been shown that outcome depends on the genomic context: the type of kinase aberration or presence of the <i>IKZF1</i> gene deletion (which has been recognized as an unfavorable prognostic factor in BCP-ALL as detailed below)<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20239e420>The <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>-like subtype was described within B-other ALL on the basis of tight co-clustering of some <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>-negative cases with <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>-positive ALL according to gene expression profiling<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. All cases belonging to this ALL subtype which have been described so far have a common genetic denominator: aberrations of the <i>ETV6</i> gene<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>; however, these are not specific for the <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>-like ALL and occur in other subtypes as well<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. The <i>ETV6</i> aberrations are usually deletions, but other gene-disrupting structural aberrations that may result in fusion genes were also described. Various alterations of <i>IKZF1</i> are also frequently detected within this subtype<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. Biological proximity to <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>-positive ALL is further supported by a similar expression pattern of two cell surface markersCD27 and CD44which can be measured by flow cytometry. Unlike the vast majority of other BCP-ALL subtypes, both <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>-like and <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>-positive ALL express CD27 but are negative or only partially or weakly positive for CD44<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a>,<a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. Such genotypephenotype correlation is rather rare in BCP-ALL. Two studies describing this subtype so far indicate that it represents 5% to 12% of B-others and 1% to 3% of all BCP-ALL cases<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>-like ALL does not appear to be associated with inferior prognosis; however, additional studies on larger cohorts are needed to reliably determine whether the prognosis of this novel subtype is as favorable as that of <i>ETV6-RUNX1</i>-positive ALL<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20239e508> <i>DUX4</i>-rearranged ALL was originally described as a B-other ALL subset with specific gene expression profile and frequent deletions of the ETS transcription factor gene <i>ERG</i><sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a>,<a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>. Only recently, these leukemias have been characterized by a unique genetic aberration: rearrangements of the gene encoding transcription factor DUX4 (<i>DUX4</i>r)<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-23">23</a><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. These rearrangements are most frequently insertions of <i>DUX4</i> into the <i>IGH</i> gene, resulting in the <i>IGHDUX4</i> fusion. The expression of <i>DUX4</i>, which is physiologically silenced in somatic tissues, is activated in <i>DUX4r</i>-ALL by its juxtaposition under the control of an ectopic regulatory element. The deletions of <i>ERG</i> can be detected in about 50% to 63% of cases<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. Although the rearrangement of <i>DUX4</i> is an early, leukemia-initiating event<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a>,<a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>, the <i>ERG</i> deletion is frequently a subclonal, thus secondary, aberration<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. It has been demonstrated that DUX4 binds to and deregulates the transcription of <i>ERG</i> in <i>DUX4</i>r-ALL; it induces the expression of alternative <i>ERG</i> variant and perhaps also renders the <i>ERG</i> gene prone to deletions<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. Owing to the small size of the inserted chromosomal fragment<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>, the <i>IGHDUX4</i> fusion (and other <i>DUX4</i>r) cannot be easily screened by FISH. Similarly, high variability of genomic breakpoints makes a potential fusion screening by PCR challenging. Thus, so far, this ALL subtype has been determined on the basis of its unique expression signature or presence of <i>DUX4</i> fusion transcripts detected by RNA-seq<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-23">23</a><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. <i>DUX4</i>r-ALL represents 4% to 8% of BCP-ALL and 15% to 30% of B-others. Before the <i>DUX4</i>r-ALL discovery, it had been shown that, despite the association with slow early treatment response and with prognostically unfavorable <i>IKZF1</i> deletions, <i>ERG</i> deletions were associated with favorable outcome<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. Two recent studies reported favorable outcome also for <i>DUX4</i>r-ALL<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. However, although the favorable outcome of BCP-ALL with <i>ERG</i> deletions seems to derive from that of the <i>DUX4</i>r-ALL subtype, the outcome of <i>DUX4</i>r-ALL and potential prognostic impact of <i>ERG</i> deletions within this subgroup should be further studied in larger and uniformly treated cohorts of patients. Interestingly, <i>ERG</i> deletions have been associated with an aberrant expression of CD2 and the tendency of leukemic blasts to switch immunophenotype from BCP to monocytoid at the beginning of treatment<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a>,<a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. During this lineage switch, B lineage or progenitor markers such as CD19 and CD34 (or both) are lost, possibly hampering B-cell-oriented flow cytometric detection of minimal residual disease. Similar to the outcome, the likely association of lineage switch with the <i>DUX4</i>r-ALL subtype and impact of the <i>ERG</i> deletions on this association remain to be elucidated by future studies.</p><p class="" id=d20239e686>ALL with the <i>ZNF384</i> gene-involving fusions (<i>ZNF384</i>r) represents another novel subtype of BCP-ALL<sup><a href="#ref-23">23</a>,<a href="#ref-24">24</a>,<a href="#ref-29">29</a>,<a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. The <i>ZNF384</i> gene, encoding transcription factor zinc-finger protein 384, can be fused to at least nine different partners (most frequently <i>TCF3</i>, <i>EP300</i>, or <i>TAF15</i>) in BCP-ALL. <i>ZNF384</i>r-ALL represents 1% to 5% of BCP-ALL and 5% to 10% of B-others. The unique gene expression signature of this ALL subtype is enriched for hematopoietic stem cell and immature myeloid lineage features and reflects upregulation of the JAK-STAT signaling pathway<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a>,<a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. <i>ZNF384</i>r-ALL also frequently displays a distinct immunophenotype compared with the majority of other BCP-ALL subtypes; the immunophenotypically distinct <i>ZNF384</i>r-ALL cases do not express (or only weakly express) CD10 marker but express myeloid markers CD13 or CD33 (or both) and may be even classified as mixed-phenotype acute leukemias on the basis of EGIL (European Group for the Immunological Characterization of Leukaemias) or WHO criteria<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a>,<a href="#ref-32">32</a><a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>. The clinical features of <i>ZNF384</i>r-ALL may vary depending on <i>ZNF384</i> fusion partner<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. However, based on published studies reporting relatively small numbers of patients so far, <i>ZNF384</i>r-ALL does not appear to be a high-risk subtype in children<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a>,<a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20239e768>The last of the five novel ALL subtypes is defined by rearrangements of the <i>MEF2D</i> gene (<i>MEF2D</i>r)<sup><a href="#ref-23">23</a>,<a href="#ref-24">24</a>,<a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. So far, at least six distinct <i>MEF2D</i>-involving fusion genes (with <i>BCL9</i> gene being the most frequent fusion partner) have been described in this ALL subtype<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-23">23</a>,<a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. <i>MEF2D</i> encodes a transcription factor that plays a role in muscle and neuronal cell differentiation but is also expressed throughout B-cell differentiation<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. The specific gene expression signature suggests a later differentiation stage of <i>MEF2D</i>r-ALL compared with other BCP-ALL subtypes<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. Immunophenotyping by flow cytometry may also reveal specific features pointing toward this ALL subtype; <i>MEF2D</i>r-ALL cells typically have relatively weak expression of CD10 and high expression of CD38<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. <i>MEF2D</i>r-ALL represents 1% to 3% of childhood BCP-ALL and 2% to 5% of B-others and is diagnosed more often in adolescents than in children. The outcome of <i>MEF2Dr-</i>ALL seems to be inferior to that of other ALL subtypes<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a>,<a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup>. Alternative therapy employing histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitors which target <i>HDAC9</i> (one of the <i>MEF2D</i>r-ALL signature genes) and which were already successfully tested <i>in vitro</i> could potentially improve its outcome in the future<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20239e853>About 40% of B-other ALL cannot be classified into any of the five novel BCP-ALL subtypes. Various recurrent genomic aberrations can be found in patients belonging to this subset; some of them were already identified decades ago and others more recently. These include, for example, intrachromosomal amplification of chromosome 21 (iAMP21)<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>, dic(9;20)<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a></sup>, <i>IGHMYC</i><sup><a href="#ref-39">39</a></sup> and <i>TCF3HLF</i><sup><a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup> fusions, <i>CRLF2</i><sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a>,<a href="#ref-42">42</a></sup>, <i>NUTM1</i><sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>, or <i>PAX5</i><sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-23">23</a>,<a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup> gene-involving fusions, and intragenic amplification of <i>PAX5</i> (<i>PAX5</i>amp)<sup><a href="#ref-43">43</a></sup>. Although some of them likely represent primary lesions and could be considered subtype-defining aberrations, others frequently represent secondary aberrations or co-occur with established primary lesions or across established BCP-ALL subtypes. Consequently, certain heterogeneity exists in the classification of BCP-ALL into genetic/biological subtypes throughout the literature as well as in the definition of B-other ALL, which thus should always be explicitly described when used. One example of a potentially confusing situation is the classification of ALL with iAMP21. This ALL subset was added provisionally into the WHO 2016 ALL classification together with <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like ALL<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>, although it has been shown that these two ALL subsets partly overlap<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>. Moreover, though it has been suggested to represent a primary lesion<sup><a href="#ref-44">44</a></sup>, iAMP21, in a minor proportion of patients, co-occurs with other primary aberrations such as <i>BCRABL1</i> or <i>ETV6RUNX1</i> fusions. Similarly, <i>CRLF2</i> rearrangement (<i>CRLF2</i>r) that is sometimes used to define the subset of patients within B-other ALL<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup> is frequently a secondary aberration<sup><a href="#ref-46">46</a></sup> and also occurs across several BCP-ALL subtypes (<i>BCR-ABL1</i>-positive ALL, <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like ALL, and hyperdiploid and hypodiploid ALL)<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a>,<a href="#ref-41">41</a>,<a href="#ref-47">47</a>,<a href="#ref-48">48</a></sup>. Nevertheless, identification of these aberrations is clinically relevant, as some of them were proven to have unfavorable prognostic impact and thus influence risk stratification and therapy (for example, iAMP21<sup><a href="#ref-49">49</a>,<a href="#ref-50">50</a></sup> and <i>TCF3HLF</i><sup><a href="#ref-51">51</a></sup>) whereas in others the suggested prognostic impact needs further validation (for example, <i>PAX5</i>amp<sup><a href="#ref-52">52</a></sup>). Importantly, various aberrations may qualify patients for a targeted therapy (for example, <i>CRLF2</i>r) as further discussed below.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d20239e998>New therapeutically relevant aberrations/categories</h2><p class="" id=d20239e1001>A variety of novel druggable lesions, especially of kinase-activating aberrations, have been described thanks to the introduction of MPS in recent years. As already mentioned above, the kinase-activating lesions are highly enriched in the <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like ALL subtype<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a>,<a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>. A large proportion of these aberrations can be assigned into one of two functional classes according to the affected signaling pathway: JAK/STAT class and ABL class<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-17">17</a>,<a href="#ref-53">53</a></sup>. The more frequent JAK/STAT class aberrations comprise aberrations affecting <i>CRLF2</i>, <i>JAK1</i>, <i>JAK2</i>, <i>JAK3</i>, <i>EPOR</i>, <i>IL7R</i>, and <i>SH2B3</i> genes and can be targeted by the JAK inhibitor ruxolitinib. The most frequently affected <i>CRLF2</i> gene encodes one of two subunits of heterodimeric receptor for thymic stromal lymphopoietin. In addition to constitutively activating F232C mutation<sup><a href="#ref-54">54</a>,<a href="#ref-55">55</a></sup>, two types of <i>CRLF2</i>-involving fusion have been described: <i>IGHCRLF2</i>, which results from interchromosomal translocation, and <i>P2RY8CRLF2</i>, which is caused by deletion in the PAR1 region on gonosomes<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a>,<a href="#ref-42">42</a></sup>. Both fusions lead to an overexpression of intact CRLF2 protein on the cell surface, which can be reliably detected by flow cytometry<sup><a href="#ref-42">42</a>,<a href="#ref-56">56</a></sup>. Thus, with the use of anti-CRLF2 antibody, <i>CRLF2</i>r-positive patients (who represent up to 17% of B-other ALL<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup> and who might be considered for targeted therapy by ruxolitinib) can be easily identified during diagnostic immunophenotyping. The second class of kinase-activating aberrations, the ABL class, comprise <i>ABL1</i>, <i>ABL2</i>, <i>PDGFRB</i>, <i>PDGFRA</i>, and <i>CSF1R</i> gene-involving fusions. These aberrations can be inhibited by tyrosine-kinase inhibitors (TKIs) such as imatinib or dasatinib<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>, both of which have already become an inherent component of the treatment of pediatric <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-positive ALL<sup><a href="#ref-57">57</a></sup>. Aberration of additional receptor and non-receptor kinase genes (for example, <i>NTRK3</i>, <i>TYK2</i>, <i>DGKH</i>, and <i>PTK2B</i>) that do not belong to these two classes but that at least in some cases can be targeted by known inhibitors can be also detected, though rarely, in <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like ALL<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. In addition to these lesions associated with the <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like phenotype, aberrations resulting in the activation of the Ras/Raf/MAPK pathway (hereafter, Ras pathway) occur frequently across various BCP-ALL subtypes<sup><a href="#ref-58">58</a><a href="#ref-62">62</a></sup>. Activating point mutations of the <i>KRAS</i> and <i>NRAS</i> genes are the most abundant, whereas mutations of the <i>FLT3</i>, <i>PTPN11</i>, <i>NF1</i>, and <i>BRAF</i> genes occur less frequently. It has been shown that leukemic cells with Ras pathway mutations are sensitive to MEK inhibitors <i>in vitro</i><sup><a href="#ref-63">63</a>,<a href="#ref-64">64</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20239e1177>Sensitivity tests performed <i>in vitro</i> and in patient-derived xenografts provided a solid rationale for prospective clinical testing of TKIs in BCP-ALL with novel kinase-activating aberrations<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a>,<a href="#ref-53">53</a></sup>. However, a relatively limited number of such children already treated by TKIs have been reported in the literature so far<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-14">14</a>,<a href="#ref-65">65</a><a href="#ref-73">73</a></sup>. Although several case reports described good response to ABL class inhibitors in patients with <i>ABL1</i>/<i>ABL2</i> or <i>PDGFRB</i> gene-involving fusions<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-66">66</a>,<a href="#ref-67">67</a>,<a href="#ref-70">70</a><a href="#ref-72">72</a>,<a href="#ref-74">74</a></sup> and overall results are generally encouraging, in some patients the TKI-involving treatment failed to induce long-term remission. This can be at least partially due to the fact that TKIs were added to treatment only upon diagnosis of disease resistance/relapse. Additionally, cases of secondary TKI resistance caused by mutations in targeted kinase (that is, by the most common mechanism of resistance known in <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-positive leukemias) have already been described<sup><a href="#ref-75">75</a>,<a href="#ref-76">76</a></sup>. The first clinical trials have been initiated recently which use frontline TKIs added to chemotherapy backbone for selected groups of patients with kinase-activating lesions (ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT03117751 sponsored by St. Jude Childrens Research Hospital, Memphis, TN, USA; ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT02420717 sponsored by MD Anderson Cancer Center, Houston, TX, USA; ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT02883049 sponsored by the National Cancer Institute, Rockville, MD, USA; and ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT03020030 sponsored by the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute, Boston, MA, USA). Importantly, several clinical and biological aspects should be considered in the strategies for prospective TKI testing in newly diagnosed BCP-ALL. It should be discussed carefully whether it is justified to use TKIs in all children with any targetable kinase-activating aberration, whether they should be used only in children harboring a lesion unambiguously associated with unfavorable outcome (similar to <i>BCRABL1</i> fusion), or whether the TKIs should be reserved for patients with worse early response to treatment or with resistant disease, where the potential benefits most likely outweigh an increase of treatment toxicity<sup><a href="#ref-77">77</a></sup>. Moreover, it is important to consider that while some of the kinase and cytokine-receptor gene alterations are supposed to represent founding lesions present in all leukemic cells and essential for their survival<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-78">78</a><a href="#ref-80">80</a></sup>, some aberrations may be either founding or secondary lesions (for example, <i>CRLF2</i>r) and others are typically secondary subclonal lesions (for example, <i>JAK</i>/<i>RAS</i> gene mutations), possibly without a resistance- or relapse-driving role, thus probably representing less-suitable therapeutic targets<sup><a href="#ref-46">46</a>,<a href="#ref-81">81</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20239e1276>Deletions of the <i>IKZF1</i> gene (<i>IKZF1</i>del) encoding lymphoid transcription factor Ikaros occur in 9% to 15% of BCP-ALL and more frequently in B-other, <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-positive, and hypodiploid ALL compared with remaining subtypes<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a>,<a href="#ref-43">43</a>,<a href="#ref-59">59</a>,<a href="#ref-82">82</a>,<a href="#ref-83">83</a></sup>. In 2009, the <i>IKZF1</i>del was associated with poor outcome<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>; subsequently, multiple studies confirmed its negative prognostic impact in BCP-ALL<sup><a href="#ref-82">82</a><a href="#ref-92">92</a></sup>. These findings stimulated the incorporation of <i>IKZF1</i>del into risk-stratification algorithms as a factor qualifying for treatment intensification in some ALL trials (ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT03020030 sponsored by the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute and ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT02716233 sponsored by Assistance Publique - Hpitaux de Paris, France). However, partially discrepant findings of individual studies showed that the prognostic impact of <i>IKZF1</i>del (its strength and independence on other known risk factors) depends on, for example, risk-stratification algorithms, applied therapy, and early treatment response. Moreover, it was revealed that it is modified by the presence of other genetic lesions. First, it was shown that deletions in the <i>ERG</i> gene (<i>ERG</i>del) attenuate the negative prognostic impact of <i>IKZF1</i>del<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>; a subsequent study demonstrated that other recurrent deletions may have the opposite effect<sup><a href="#ref-93">93</a></sup>. These findings led to the establishment of the <i>IKZF1</i>plus category<sup><a href="#ref-93">93</a></sup>, defined as <i>IKZF1</i>del with concurrent deletion of <i>PAX5</i> or <i>CDKN2A</i> or <i>CDKN2B</i> genes or deletion of PAR1 region (resulting in <i>P2RY8CRLF2</i> fusion gene) or with a combination of these, in the absence of <i>ERG</i>del. The <i>IKZF1</i>plus genomic pattern occurs in 6% of BCP-ALL, is associated with inferior outcome in patients with detectable minimal residual disease at the end of induction treatment, and will be used for risk stratification in the upcoming AIEOP-BFM (Associazione Italiana di Ematologia ed Oncologia PediatricaBerlin-Frankfurt-Mnster) ALL trial<sup><a href="#ref-93">93</a></sup>. <i>IKZF1</i>plus occurs predominantly in B-other ALL; although we can assume its enrichment in subtypes with higher frequency of <i>IKZF1</i>del, such as <i>BCR-ABL1</i>-like ALL, its distribution and prognostic role across novel BCP-ALL subtypes remain to be elucidated by future studies. These studies could also help to clarify the strong association between <i>IKZF1</i>plus prognostic value and the early therapy response.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d20239e1395>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d20239e1398>Herein, we briefly reviewed some of the most important recent findings in pediatric BCP-ALL, demonstrating that the use of modern high-throughput technologies not only advanced our insight into the genetics and biology of BCP-ALL but also paved the way for novel treatment options. However, with growing knowledge, we have begun to recognize more extensively that the significance (for example, clinical relevance) of individual factors may vary substantially depending on additional genetic/biological contexts. Enormous effort will still be needed to further improve our understanding of the considerably complex relationships between genetic/biological and clinical aspects of BCP-ALL and to better translate this knowledge into further improvements in patient outcome.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d20239e1404>Abbreviations</h2><p class="" id=d20239e1407>ALL, acute lymphoblastic leukemia; BCP, B-cell precursor; FISH, fluorescence <i>in situ</i> hybridization; MPS, massive parallel sequencing; PCR, polymerase chain reaction; Ph-like, Philadelphia chromosome-like; RNA-seq, RNA sequencing; TKI, tyrosine-kinase inhibitor; WHO, World Health Organization</p></div><div id=article1-back class=generated-article-footer><div class=back-section><a name=d20239e1 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d20536>Grant information</h2><p>Institutional or infrastructure support were provided by the Ministry of Education, Youth and Sports of the Czech Republic (NPU I nr.LO1604), Charles University (UNCE 204012), the European Regional Development Fund  OP Prague (CZ.2.16/3.1.00/24022), and the National Center of Medical Genomics (CZ.02.1.01/0.0/0.0/16_013/0001634  modernization of infrastructure and research of genetic variation in the population, OP RDE).
