It uses thousands of tons of synthetic dyes (azo) annually<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>. A large portion of those goes into water bodies untreated<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. Carcinogenic and recalcitrant molecules present in the dye penetrate into the ecosystem and harm every member of the system<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. Humans use the polluted water directly for daily necessities which may result in diseases such as cancer, new genetic mutations or changes in the DNA etc. becoming an epidemic.</p><p class="" id=d7259e270>The textile industry in Bangladesh accounts for 45% of all industrial employment and contributes 5% to the total national income<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. The industry employs nearly 4 million people, mostly women<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. Despite the significant economic contribution, it has brought with it a range of environmental problems, mostly pollution of water resources. The textile industry consumes large quantities of water for various processes and discharges equally large volumes of wastewaters containing a variety of pollutants and coloring agents such as the azo dye<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a>,<a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d7259e288>It is estimated that over 2,80,000 tons of textile dyes are discharged in industrial effluent every year, worldwide. Therefore, pollution from these discharges contaminated with dyestuff is becoming alarming<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a>,<a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>. This sector is placed as a major source of water pollution in Bangladesh<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d7259e302>Textile wastewater is highly colored, resulting in the blocking of the majority of sunlight, thereby retarding the growth of aquatic animals and plants; it also contains the dissolved toxic substance and carcinogens<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a>,<a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>. The serious damage of pollution is caused mainly due to the durability of the dyes in wastewater<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. Azo dyes are widely known coloring agents used in industries and hence commonly released in the environment<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. The dye wastewaters are extremely toxic to both aquatic fauna and flora, crop plants, and human beings<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d7259e325>At present, there are several techniques that can be employed in dye removal from effluents. However, these methods are varied in efficiency due to the variety of existent dyes and to the effluents complexity, and the combination of various methods may be considered since each method showed its limitations. There are three categories of existing methods: physical, chemical and biological. Physical methods like Coagulation/Flocculation, Adsorption, Membrane filtration, and Ion exchange are expensive. Chemical methods like Fentons reagent, Ozone, Photochemical, Sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl), Electrolysis and wet air oxidation are not cost effective and produce toxic byproduct.</p><p class="" id=d7259e328>Biological treatment, in the form of bacterial degradation, has been mainly applied in the removal of azo dyes<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>, which generally is resistant to aerobic degradation<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. However, its degradation was observed in anaerobic conditions, but aromatic amines are formed as a final product, which can be toxic, mutagenic and carcinogenic<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. Under these anaerobic conditions, it is not possible to degrade the aromatic amines formed, which in turn are only degraded in an aerobic environment. Thus, to achieve a complete degradation of azo dyes a method that combines anaerobic treatment of the dyes with the mineralization of aromatic amines under aerobic conditions should be applied<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a>,<a href="#ref-22">22</a></sup>. This research aimed to identify effective dye degrading bacteria from effluent soil samples and optimize the physiochemical condition for their optimum growth<sup><a href="#ref-23">23</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d7259e363>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d7259e366 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Soil sample and azo dye collection</h3><p class="" id=d7259e371>Four soil samples (A: <i>235652.6N, 901532.6E</i>; B: <i>234741.7N, 901534.1E</i>; C: <i>234654.0N, 902005.3E</i>; D: <i>235652.8N, 901601.2E</i>) were aseptically collected from a textile effluent disposal area, in Savar, Hemayetpur and Aminbazar in June 2015. Sterile plastic containers were used to carry the soil samples. The samples were stored at Microbiology and Biotechnology Research Laboratory under Dpt. of MNS, BRAC University in sterile plastic bags at 4C (to keep the microorganism viable) for later use<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>. Commercially available azo dyes (Meera Dyestuff Industries, India) were collected from Mitford dye market in Dhaka.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d7259e391 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Inoculation in dye-containing media, isolation and screening</h3><p class="" id=d7259e396>One gram of each of the soil (effluent) samples (A, B, C, D) were taken to prepare a homogenous suspension. The suspension of each sample was individually applied to sixteen 1% dye containing (eight Orange M2R - OM2R and eight Green GS - GGS) SM broth media (glucose - 10 g/L; #G8270, dipotassium phosphate - 0.6 g/L; #1551128 USP, peptone - 10 g/L; #P7750, monopotassium phosphate - 1.9 g/L; #1551139 USP, magnesium sulfate - 1 g/L; #M2643, yeast extract - 1 g/L; #Y1625, pH: 6.0 - 6.4; Sigma Aldrich, St Louis, MO, USA) to detect the dye degrading capability. 1% solution of OM2R and GGS was made by adding 0.5g of dye into the 50ml distilled water. After 5 days, each conical flask was compared with the control and decolorization was observed. As each of the samples (A, B, C and D) contained a highly concentrated mixture of different types of microorganism, up to 10<sup>-4</sup> and 10<sup>-5</sup> dilutions were performed, and spread plate technique applied on NA plates followed by incubation for 24 hours at 37C to obtain eight soil isolates (AO, BO, CO, DO, AG, BG, CG, DG). Selected isolates, based on morphology, were enriched in NA media and incubated for 24 hours at 37C. The plates were sealed, refrigerated at 4C and were frequently subcultured.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d7259e406 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Media optimization</h3><p class="" id=d7259e411>Optimum growth conditions for the isolates were identified applying different physiochemical state (dye concentration, pH, NaCl concentration, temperature) to the time of growth. For GGS dye, BG isolate was superior to all other isolates, achieving 89% decolorization in 2% NaCl. The optimum temperature was 37C in all four isolates. AO achieved 93% decolorization and BG 90% in two different dye-containing media. The decolorization rate gradually decreased with temperature elevation (<a href="#T3">Table 3</a>; <a href="#DS1">Dataset 2</a><sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>). Average O.D. calculated from 1st and 2nd O.D. </div></div><div class="flashwrapper is-hidden"><div class=flashContent id=DS1></div></div></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d7259e2852>Discussion</h2><p class="" id=d7259e2855>Most of the isolates from the different soil samples were identified as <i>Bacillus</i> species. Dye decolorizing ability of isolates was investigated independently. CO (<i>B. farraginis</i>) showed the highest dye decolorization capacity (98%) in SM broth media containing 1% OM2R dye for 5 days at 37C. However, as the concentration of dye increased up to 3% and 5% the decolorization rate decreased to 94% and 90% respectively, because of the intensity of azo dyes. On GGS dye degradation BG (<i>P. macerans</i>) showed 97% decolorization. This was similarly effective at 3% dye concentration (94%), however, the rate was found to slump at 5% dye concentration (81%).</p><p class="" id=d7259e2867>pH is one of the important abiotic factors that affect the growth and metabolic homeostasis. The effect was studied at different pH values (5  8). At pH 7.0 AO (<i>E. termitis</i>) gave maximum decolorization (90%). A similar rate was observed over the pH range of 5.0 and 6.0, with a swift reduction (59%) observed at pH 8 by AO (<i>E. termitis</i>). These results suggest that acidic pH values may influence the stability of the enzyme causing denaturation. Chang <i>et al.</i> (2001)<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup> found that azo reductase performance was affected by pH, with 2.5 times better dye reduction at pH 7 - 9 than below pH 7. These findings corresponded well to the best decolorization found between pH 7 - 9.5<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d7259e2887>In the case of GGS, the maximum decolorization rate was attained at pH 7.0 by BG (<i>P. macerans</i>) at 92%. The majority of the azo dye reducing bacterial species reported so far were able to reduce the dye at pH near 7<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>. The requirement of near neutral pH for optimum growth had been reported in several studies<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. Results indicate that a pH increase from 5.0 to 7.0 enhanced the decolorization of GGS dyes. At pH 5 the decolorization rate was 80% of dye by BG (<i>P. macerans</i>). A small increase was observed at pH 6 (85%) with an abrupt decrease at pH 8.0 (73%). It was observed that better decolorization rates were around pH 6 - 7 bands for both of OM2R and GGS dye by the selected isolates.</p><p class="" id=d7259e2910>Decolorization percentage of OM2R by selected isolates was found to vary with different concentration (2 - 8 g/L) of NaCl when studied for 120 hours at 37C. Maximum decolorization of OM2R by AO (<i>E. termitis</i>) was observed as 81% at 2% NaCl, but the percentage decolorization was found to decrease with increases of NaCl concentration (<a href="#T3">Table 3</a>; <a href="#DS1">Dataset 2</a><sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>). The decolorization attained by AO (<i>E. termitis</i>) at 37C for 4%, 6% and 8% NaCl was 60%, 43%, and 37%. Kargi and Dincer (1996)<sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup> mention that high salt concentrations (&gt;1% salt) are known to cause plasmolysis and/or loss of cell activity.</p><p class="" id=d7259e2933>Similarly, at 2% NaCl concentration the degradation percentage of GGS dye was 89% by BG (<i>P. macerans</i>). The decolorization attained by BG (<i>P. macerans</i>) at 37C for 4%, 6% and 8% NaCl was 72%, 62%, and 40% (<a href="#T3">Table 3</a>; <a href="#DS1">Dataset 2</a><sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>).</p><p class="" id=d7259e2951>To determine the optimum temperature for dye decolorization a temperature range of 30C 55C was examined. As seen in <a href="#T3">Table 3</a> the optimum temperature for OM2R dye decolorization was 37C for the AO (<i>E. termitis</i>) attained a maximum decolorization of 93%. Angelova <i>et al.</i> (2008)<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup> found that the azo bond reduction rate rose with an increased temperature, a maximum rate of around 40C, 35 times faster than at 20C. At 30C and 45C the degradation rate for OM2R by AO <i>E. termitis</i> was 82% and 72% of dye. A low decolorization of 59% of the dye was detected at 55C by AO (<i>E. termitis</i>) isolate. Temperatures above 55C were not studied since results shown that the increase from 37C to 45C promoted a marginal decrease in dye decolorization (<a href="#T3">Table 3</a>; <a href="#DS1">Dataset 2</a><sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>).</p><p class="" id=d7259e2983>The optimum temperature for GGS dye decolorization was 37C for the BG isolate (<i>P. macerans</i>), attaining a maximum decolorization of 90% of dye. In the case of BG, at 30C and 45C the decolorization percentage was 86% and 84% of dye respectively. No improvement in dye decolorization was observed at temperatures above 45C, with low decolorization of 58% and 45% of dye detected at 55C by BG (<i>P. macerans</i>) isolate as evident from <a href="#T3">Table 3</a>. BG (<i>P. macerans</i>) has a broad range of compatibility from 30C to 45C. Within the optimal values of temperature, the lowest temperature was selected as the optimum temperature since this leads to lower energy costs.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d7259e3001>Conclusion</h2><p class="" id=d7259e3004>Traditional wastewater treatment is inefficient and remains a threat to environment<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a></sup>. Biotreatment offers an easy, cheap and effective alternative for color removal of textile dyes<sup><a href="#ref-39">39</a></sup>. Hence, economic and eco-friendly techniques using bacteria can be an alternative method. The present study strongly concluded that the bacterial isolates <i>E. termitis</i>, <i>E. camelliae</i>, <i>B. decolorationis</i>, <i>P. macerans</i> species were a good microbial source for textile effluent treatment, in biological degradation of textile dye. However, decolorization potential of the isolates needs to be validated by demonstration in appropriate bioreactors before its application.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d7259e3031>Data availability</h2><p class="" id=d7259e3034>Dataset 1: Results of optical density (O.D) &amp; de-colorization (%) with different concentrations of dye.
