Conclusions: It can be concluded that the obtained results are in accordance with other studies that examined different extracts, indicating that if a sample is non-toxic to Artemia salina, then its effects will also be similar in humans."

Abstract </h2> <div class="article-abstract article-page-general-text-mobile research-layout"> <div class="abstract-text is-expanded"> <b>Background: </b>Medicinal plants have been used since ancient times, receiving interest in their healing potential because of their active components. The Amazon rainforest in the east of Peru has great diversity of flora, especially monocotyledons (<i>Cocos nucifera</i>, <i>Mauritia flexuosa and </i><i>Coffea </i>spp.) and dicotyledons (<i>Theobroma cacao </i>L. <i>and Musa </i>spp. The toxicity of the hydroalcoholic extract of plant leaves from the Peruvian Amazon in <i>Artemia salina</i> was evaluated in this study.<br/> <b>Methods: </b>The leaves of the plants <i>Cocos nucifera</i>, <i>Mauritia flexuosa</i>, <i>Theobroma cacao </i>L., <i>Coffea sp, and Musa sp</i> were collected in the district of Cacatachi, San Mart&iacute;n. Phytochemical analysis of the leaves was carried out to identify their active components. The eggs of <i>Artemia salina</i> were provided by the Department of Animal Physiology of Universidad Nacional de Trujillo. The hydro-alcoholic extraction was carried out via the maceration method using 300 g of leaves and 500 mL of 96% ethanol for 15 days in agitation. The solutions were taken to a vertical rotavapor to obtain dry extracts and concentrations of 10, 100 and 1000 μL/ml were prepared. To test toxicity, 10 larvae were given the extract for each plant species and concentration in triplicate. The CL<sub>50 </sub>toxicity of <i>Artemia salina</i> samples was classified as: ˃ 1000 μL/ml (non-toxic), 500˂CL<sub>50</sub> ≤ 1000 (low toxicity), 100˂CL<sub>50</sub> ≤ 500 (moderate toxicity), CL<sub>50</sub>˂100 (high toxicity).<br/> <b>Results: </b>It was observed that <i>Mauritia flexuosa</i> and <i>Musa </i>sp.<i> </i>at concentrations of 10, 100 and 1000 μL/ml have high, medium and low toxicity, respectively. However, only low toxicity was observed in <i>Cocos nucifera</i>, <i>Theobroma cacao </i>L<i>. and Coffea </i>sp<i>. </i><br/> <b>Conclusions: </b>It can be concluded that the obtained results are in accordance with other studies that examined different extracts, indicating that if a sample is non-toxic to <i>Artemia salina</i>, then its effects will also be similar in humans. </div> <div Some developing countries use plants in primary health care, and in 2015 it was estimated that 25.6 billion dollars were spent worldwide on plant consumption and its derivatives, expected to increase to 35.4 billion dollars by 2020<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a>,<a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. Due to the high consumption of plants for medicinal purposes, it is necessary to study their toxicology in order to avoid side effects.</p><p class="" id=d66612e265>The Amazon rainforest in the east of Peru has great diversity of flora, especially monocotyledons (<i>Cocos nucifera</i>, <i>Mauritia flexuosa and Coffea</i> spp.) and dicotyledons (<i>Theobroma cacao</i> L. <i>and Musa</i> spp.);<i></i> species which are used as an alternative to medicine due to their chemical properties and active components, such as steroids, phenolic compounds, flavonoids and lactones<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a>–<a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. Studies have shown that the leaf extracts of some species are toxic for the human consumption because of the combination of their chemical compounds<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a>–<a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d66612e297> <i>Artemia salina</i> is a species of shrimp belonging to the Crustaceae family. Ranging from 1 to 7 mm in size, these shrimps have a cosmopolitan distribution and live in saltwater at a temperature of 6 to 35°C<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>. Its biology was studied as a preliminary test for ecotoxicological investigations associated with low cost and easy handling, in addition to its potential use as a practical and sustainable method for the identification of the pharmacological potential of synthetic and natural compounds and to measure their toxicity in animals<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a>–<a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>. The determination of plant toxicity against <i>Artemia salina</i> involves only one parameter: life or death, and the absence of toxicity is an indicator that the plant can be tolerated by biological systems<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. Therefore, the aim of this study was to evaluate the toxicity of the hydro-alcoholic extract of fruit leaves from the Peruvian Amazon in <i>Artemia salina</i>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d66612e328>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d66612e331 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Source and husbandry of Artemia salina</h3><p class="" id=d66612e336>The eggs (20 days old) of <i>Artemia salina</i> were donated by the Department of Animal Physiology of Universidad Nacional de Trujillo, washed with filtered seawater and dried in dehydrated environment and then transported (with oxygen supply). One gram of eggs was used (equivalent to 700-800 eggs), left to hatch in 5 liters of filtered sea water under fluorescent artificial light (110 watts) at a temperature of 25°C for 24 h. The eggs were fed on commercial yeast extract and kept in a glass incubation chamber with abundant oxygenation to allow them to hatch and continue their biological cycle for approximately seven days. Stage III larvae (seven days old) were used as toxicity markers due to their high sensitivity and 10 larvae were used to test each concentration (10, 100 and 1000 μg / ml).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d66612e343 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Source of plant samples</h3><p class="" id=d66612e348>A total of 500 grams of plant samples from each species (<i>Cocos nucifera</i>, <i>Mauritia flexuosa, Theobroma cacao</i> L.<i>, Coffea</i> sp<i>, and Musa</i> sp) were collected by researchers (AI and AS) in the district of Cacatachi, San Martín at 295 m above sea level and 12 km to the north of Tarapoto (6°29'40" south latitude and 76°27'57" west longitude). The specimens were transferred in vacuum bags that were labeled with their respective scientific names at a temperature of 37°C to the Herbarium Truxillense of Universidad Nacional de Trujillo for their identification and given a registration code.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d66612e364 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Preparation of plant samples</h3><p class="" id=d66612e369>Leaves were washed with distilled water, disinfected with 96% ethanol and fragmented to an approximate size of 3mm. Extraction of the hydro-alcoholic extract was carried out by maceration with 300 g of leaves and 500 mL of 96% ethanol over 15 days, with agitation carried out using a vertical rotavapor (Scilogex RE-100), run at 70rpm for 10 minutes every four hours (between 7am and 10pm) and a dry extract dissolved in 96% ethanol was obtained.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d66612e373 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Phytochemical analysis of plant samples</h3><p class="" id=d66612e378>Phytochemical analysis of the leaf samples was carried out to identify their active principles according to the protocol described by Miranda <i>et al.</i><sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. Briefly, each sample was subjected to solvents of increasing polarity, in order to obtain secondary metabolites according to their solubility, using reagents and dyes to determine the presence or absence of active components such as terpenes, flavonoids, reducing sugars, among others. The assays used to determine the presence of each type of secondary metabolite are listed in <a href="#T1">Table 1</a>. To each test tube, 1ml of pure extract was added and reagents for each assay were added that identify secondary metabolites through color change. The results of the color change were judged by eye according to the method described by Miranda and Cuellar<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup> and classified as light, moderate or strong.</p><a name=T1 class=n-a></a><div class="table-wrap panel clearfix"><div class=caption><h3>Table 1. strong.</p></div></div></div></div><div class=section><a name=d66612e733 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Toxicity tests</h3><p class="" id=d66612e738>Concentrations (using filtered seawater) of 10, 100 and 1000 µg/ml were than prepared according to the protocol described by Seremet <i>et al.</i> 2018<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>: 5μg of extract was diluted in 5ml of filtered seawater, equivalent to 10μg/ml, 50μg of extract was diluted in 5ml of filtered seawater, equivalent to 100μg/ml, and 500μg of extract was diluted in 5ml of filtered seawater, equivalent to 1000μg/ml. The larvae were added to a test tube containing 10ml of filtered seawater and 0.5ml of the hydroalcoholic extract. A total of 10 larvae were used for each plant species and concentration and each test was performed in triplicate. A control group of 10 larvae in 10ml of filtered seawater without extract was used. The tests were performed in the biological chemistry laboratory of the Universidad Nacional de Trujillo and larvae were observed for 24 hours before the number of live larvae were counted using a stereoscope. The lethal concentration (CL<sub>50</sub>) for <i>Artemia salina</i> samples was used to classify toxicity as follows: ˃ 1000 µg/ml (non-toxic), 500 ˂ CL<sub>50</sub> ≤ 1000 (low toxicity), 100 ˂ CL<sub>50</sub> ≤ 500 (moderate toxicity), CL<sub>50 </sub>˂ 100 (high toxicity)<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d66612e768>The toxicity percentage was calculated as follows:</p><p class="" id=d66612e771><a name=e1 class=n-a></a><div class="disp-formula panel"><math display=block id=math1 overflow=scroll><mrow><mi>T</mi><mi>o</mi><mi>x</mi><mi>i</mi><mi>c</mi><mi>i</mi><mi>t</mi><mi>y</mi><mtext>(</mtext><mi>p</mi><mi>e</mi><mi>r</mi><mi>c</mi><mi>e</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>t</mi><mtext>)</mtext><mo>=</mo><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mi>T</mi><mi>N</mi><mi>A</mi><mo>−</mo><mi>A</mi><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy=false>)</mo><mo>/</mo><mi>T</mi><mi>N</mi><mi>A</mi><mo>*</mo><mtext>100</mtext></mrow></math></div></p><p class="" id=d66612e842>Where TNA = Total number of <i>Artemia</i> and AA = Number of alive <i>Artemia</i><sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d66612e855 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Statistical analysis</h3><p class="" id=d66612e860>The statistical analysis was carried out using SPSS version 23, applying the student's t-test to calculate toxicity at different concentrations, with p&lt;0.05 considered as statistically significant.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d66612e864 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Ethical statement</h3><p class="" id=d66612e869>Generally, permits are required from the Environmental Ministry to perform experiments in which animals are involved. However, a permit to investigate <i>Artemia salina</i> was not considered necessary because it does not appear on the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.iucnredlist.org/" id=d66612e874>International Union for the Conservation of Nature (IUCN)</a> red list and therefore it’s investigation does not represent a danger to the environment or to human beings. All efforts were made to ameliorate any suffering to the animals following the protocol described by the Ecuadorian code of practice for the care and use of animals for scientific purposes of the Ministry of Environment of Ecuador.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d66612e882>Results</h2><p class="" id=d66612e885>Qualitative presence of phenolic compounds can be observed in the hydro-alcoholic extract, such as flavonoids, steroids, triterpenes, reducing sugars, lactones and tannins (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>)<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d66612e895>In <a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>, it can be observed that there is high toxicity (10 µg/ml), moderate (100 µg/ml) and low (1000 µg/ml) in <i>Mauritia flexuosa</i> and <i>Musa sp</i>; however, only low toxicity (1000 µg/ml) is evidenced in <i>Cocos nucífera</i>, <i>Coffea</i> sp. and <i>Theobroma cacao L</i> (see <i>Underlying data</i>)<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>.</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/20822/c7e4b970-6586-4c26-b2e9-6389c41bfed2_figure1.gif"><img alt="c7e4b970-6586-4c26-b2e9-6389c41bfed2_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/20822/c7e4b970-6586-4c26-b2e9-6389c41bfed2_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. Toxicity percentage at different concentrations of hydro-alcoholic extract.</h3></div></div><p class="" id=d66612e938>The results obtained with <i>Artemia salina</i> indicate that both <i>Mauritia flexuosa</i> and <i>Musa sp</i> in the concentrations 10, 100 and 1000 µg/ml have low, moderate and high toxicity, respectively. However, in <i>Cocos nucífera</i>, <i>Coffea sp</i> and <i>Theobroma cacao</i> L., only low toxicity is observed at a concentration of 1000 µg/ml, indicating that toxicity was directly proportional to the concentration of the extract used; therefore, the highest toxicity could be related to the high content of phenolic compounds and flavonoids, among other components, in accordance with previous studies<sup><a href="#ref-22">22</a>–<a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>. Samples with values of LD<sub>50</sub> higher than 1,000 μg/ml were considered non-toxic, in accordance with Leite <i>et al.</i> 2009<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>, and values of LD<sub>50</sub> below 1,000 μg/ml were considered to be toxic according to previous studies<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a>–<a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. The characteristic phytochemical composition of each plant can change due to various factors such as room temperature, soil, pH and origin. These environmental conditions may influence the synthesis<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a>,<a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup> and expression of the phytochemical components in the plant, changing their toxicity.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d66612e998>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d66612e1001>Health treatments with medicinal plants are cheap and accessible to the population; however, their indiscriminate use is a risk due to the toxicity of some compounds within the plant. For this reason, it may be useful to study plant extracts with the aim of demonstrating the therapeutic or toxic action of their active components, using <i>Artemia salina</i> as an evaluation method. In this context, the toxicity of hydroalcoholic extracts of fruit leaves from the Peruvian Amazon was evaluated. It can be concluded that the obtained results are in accordance with other studies that examined different extracts as reported by Simões and De Almeida<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>, indicating that if a sample is non-toxic to <i>Artemia salina</i>, then its effects will also be similar to humans.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title
