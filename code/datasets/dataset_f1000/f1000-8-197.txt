Complications include post-extubation hypoxemia, reintubation, acute respiratory failure, pulmonary edema, pneumonia, and atelectasis. These increase hospital length of stay, unplanned ICU admissions, hospital readmissions, mortality, and costs<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a>–<a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>. For example, respiratory failure after abdominal surgery can increase 30-day mortality 10-fold<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d17452e223>Pathophysiology</h2><p class="" id=d17452e226>Pathologically, we can characterize respiratory complications as being due to respiratory muscle dysfunction or as a primary airway disease. The latter can in turn be subdivided into upper airway-related complications, such as reintubation of an obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) patient, or pulmonary complications, such as pulmonary edema.</p><p class="" id=d17452e229>Both respiratory muscle dysfunction and airway disease can develop as a consequence of an imbalance in ventilatory drive. Both increases and decreases in ventilatory drive are potentially harmful and may, for example, increase the risk of aspiration by negatively affecting the interaction between breathing and swallowing (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>). Sedation due to opioid and anxiolytic therapy commonly leads to upper airway dysfunction, resulting in insufficient respiration (hypopnea/apnea), but also affects the breathing–swallowing coordination and pharyngeal muscle strength, both of which contribute to pharyngeal dysfunction and increased risk of aspiration<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>. In turn, an increase in respiratory drive (e.g. during hypercapnic respiratory failure) can lead to high transpulmonary pressure during inspiration, which increases lung stress. Supplementation of inhaled carbon dioxide was shown to reverse upper airway collapsibility induced by propofol<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>, but excessive hypercapnia increases the likelihood of pathological swallowing<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. Thus, perioperative physicians need to balance their interventions to keep ventilatory drive within normal limits. Upper airway collapse can lead to desaturation, atelectasis, and respiratory failure. Patency of the upper airway depends on competing dilating versus collapsing forces<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>. The former includes the pharyngeal dilator muscles (genioglossus and tensor palatini) and caudal traction on the airway from lung expansion (which can be improved by positive end-expiratory pressure [PEEP]). Sedatives, opioids, or even delirium can decrease airway dilator muscle tone. Dilating forces are influenced by atelectasis or the inevitable supine position of surgery. In contrast, collapsing forces include external pressure from surrounding soft tissue, which is increased in the presence of edema, obesity, blood clots, and tumors or in the supine position.</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/18240/d9216310-16d4-44cb-aac8-c5dc26209865_figure1.gif"><img alt="d9216310-16d4-44cb-aac8-c5dc26209865_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/18240/d9216310-16d4-44cb-aac8-c5dc26209865_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. Supplementation of inhaled carbon dioxide was shown to reverse upper airway collapsibility induced by propofol<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>, but excessive hypercapnia increases the likelihood of pathological swallowing<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. Thus, perioperative physicians need to balance their interventions to keep ventilator drive within normal limits. ARDS, acute respiratory distress syndrome.</p></div></div><p class="" id=d17452e283>Remarkably, perhaps, significant postoperative pulmonary edema is reported in up to 1–2% of patients<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>, and causes include negative pressure pulmonary edema, fluid shifts, and, rarely, neurogenic edema in acute hypertension or after cerebral injury<sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d17452e295>More common than edema is atelectasis, and its pathophysiology starts minutes after induction<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. A reduced regional transpulmonary pressure in dependent lung areas is accentuated by inflammation induced by surgery, bacterial translocation, chest wall restriction, and cephalad diaphragm displacement by surgical retraction. This extends postoperatively, such that a restrictive pattern worsens respiratory mechanics and gas exchange. Pain, high inflation driving pressures, and inflammation all contribute.</p><p class="" id=d17452e302>Ventilator-induced lung injury has multiple causes. In addition to barotrauma, reduced lung compliance in unrecruited areas causes overinflation of aerated lung tissue in nondependent areas with subsequent “volutrauma”. Cyclical effects lead to “atelectotrauma”. As mentioned above, the release of local proinflammatory mediators also contributes to lung injury “biotrauma”<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a>,<a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d17452e315>Recommendations for patient management</h2><p class="" id=d17452e318>Modifiable perioperative factors in patient management are shown in <a href="#T1">Table 1</a>. end-expiratory pressure; SpO<sub>2</sub>, peripheral capillary oxygen saturation; RCT, randomized controlled trial; TOF, train of four.</p></div></div></div><p class="" id=d17452e952>The method of anesthesia induction can be preventative for postoperative complications. Keeping a patient as upright as possible during induction may help optimize mask ventilation and also help during extubation. This approach may prevent atelectasis, which may be especially important in patients with OSA<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a>,<a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d17452e962>After intubation, lung-protective mechanical ventilation aims to maintain lung recruitment by keeping transpulmonary pressures within the optimal (linear) part of the local pressure–volume curve. Results from ICU patients suggest reduced morbidity and mortality in the setting of acute lung injury<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. Typically, a PEEP of at least 5 cm H<sub>2</sub>O and a median plateau pressure of 16 cm H<sub>2</sub>O appear to be the most beneficial<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. However, protective effects of PEEP may be very procedure specific, as a PEEP of approximately 5 cm H<sub>2</sub>O in major abdominal surgery is beneficial, whereas this is not matched by effects of the same level of PEEP in neurosurgery<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. Also, PEEP must be patient specific: those with poor chest wall compliance need higher levels of PEEP<sup><a href="#ref-43">43</a></sup>. Although high FiO<sub>2</sub> is used to maintain oxygenation, it may also worsen pulmonary function, probably by promoting atelectasis<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d17452e1002>Interestingly, it has been found that an increased average minimum alveolar concentration of volatile anesthetics, including nitrous oxide, improves 30-day mortality and the risk of pulmonary complications<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>. The adverse influence of neuromuscular blocking agents (NMBAs) is now well established, especially when associated with inadequate reversal<sup><a href="#ref-34">34</a>–<a href="#ref-37">37</a>,<a href="#ref-44">44</a>,<a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup>. Monitoring of NMBAs along with reversal guided by neuromuscular transmission is now mandatory according to minimum monitoring guidelines in the UK<sup><a href="#ref-46">46</a></sup>. The choice of reversal agent remains controversial; while sugammadex was shown to reduce the incidence of postoperative residual paralysis compared with neostigmine in one randomized controlled trial<sup><a href="#ref-47">47</a></sup>, a recent multicenter observational study (POPULAR trial) found no association between the reversal agent used and postoperative respiratory complications<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d17452e1034>With regard to fluid administration, it is the most-restrictive and the most-liberal strategies that have been associated with respiratory complications, whereas moderate regimens appear to be optimal<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a>,<a href="#ref-39">39</a>,<a href="#ref-48">48</a></sup>. Pain is an adverse factor for respiratory complications, but very high doses of opioids are also potentially harmful<sup><a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup>. Neuraxial blockade may reduce postoperative morbidity and mortality in subpopulations<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a>,<a href="#ref-49">49</a></sup>, and laparoscopic surgery, which may contribute to better analgesia, further appears beneficial<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. Good pain relief also promotes early mobilization, which shortens patients’ length of stay<sup><a href="#ref-50">50</a></sup>. Monitoring is important in the detection of early signs of respiratory complications and the decision to admit and observe a patient in the ICU as opposed to the PACU<sup><a href="#ref-51">51</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d17452e1074>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d17452e1077>There is a considerable literature base supporting the individual results highlighted above. What is emerging is the need for the development and implementation of center-specific guidelines, based on algorithms, coupled with key performance indicators developed by multidisciplinary teams (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>). This can form the basis of a continuous quality improvement program. An important driver in achieving this goal is a local champion or “facilitator”, who can lead the integration of the needed processes.</p><a name=f2 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/18240/d9216310-16d4-44cb-aac8-c5dc26209865_figure2.gif"><img alt="d9216310-16d4-44cb-aac8-c5dc26209865_figure2.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/18240/d9216310-16d4-44cb-aac8-c5dc26209865_figure2.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 2.
