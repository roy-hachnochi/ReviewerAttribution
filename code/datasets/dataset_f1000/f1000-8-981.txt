Heart failure patients with left ventricular dysfunctionwith or without preserved ejection fractionare the most prevalent type of CSA and over one half of these patients show sleep-disordered breathing (apnea/hypopnea index [AHI] greater than 15 events per hour of sleep)<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a>,<a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. The periodic waxing and waning of tidal volume (Vt) followed by apneas of at least 10 seconds duration accompanied by intermittent hypoxemia and transient cortical electroencephalography arousal are typical of non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep in congestive heart failure (CHF) with periodic cycles of at least 50 seconds. CSA and cluster type periodic breathing with short periodic cycles (1025 seconds) are common in healthy sojourners during NREM sleep at high altitudes (&gt;3000 m, oxygen saturation [SaO<sub>2</sub>] of about 90%) and even at more moderate high altitudes in susceptible people, especially with prolonged residencies<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a>,<a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. This cluster-type periodic breathing with relatively short cycles is also common at sea level in over half of chronic opioid users, and the severity of CSA is proportional to opiate dose<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. A third type of CSAs consists of those immediately preceding an airway obstruction. In the example given here (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>, mixed), esophageal pressure is used as a highly sensitive means of distinguishing central (no inspiratory effort) from obstructive (increasing inspiratory effort) apneas. In clinical practice, less sensitive, indirect measures of inspiratory effort are used, thereby likely underestimating the prevalence of mixed apneas. Even more difficult detection problems occur in attempts to distinguish hypopneas of central versus obstructive origin. The measurements of suprasternal pressure or shape (or both) of the nasal pressure waveform during inspiration have shown favorable comparisons with esophageal pressure as a marker for increased upper airway resistance<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a>,<a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup> and should be explored further for use in the clinical polysomnogram.</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/20084/89e44b0f-4b6a-4542-a718-125f0df244a7_figure1.gif"><img alt="89e44b0f-4b6a-4542-a718-125f0df244a7_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/20084/89e44b0f-4b6a-4542-a718-125f0df244a7_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. Three common types of cyclical central sleep apneas.</h3><p id=d42307e191>The three types are congestive heart failure (CHF), high-altitude/chronic opioid use, and mixed-central followed by obstructive apneas. See text for detailed descriptions. EEG, electroencephalography; P<sub>es</sub>, esophageal pressure; SaO<sub>2</sub>, arterial oxygenated hemoglobin saturation; Vt, tidal volume. Adapted from<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>.</p></div></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e211>Central sleep apnea sequelae</h2><p class="" id=d42307e214>Cyclical CSAslike their obstructive counterpartsare pro-inflammatory with substantial long-term deleterious effects during wakefulness, including enhanced sympathetic vasomotor outflow and vascular endothelial dysfunction, neurocognitive deficits, and insulin insensitivity<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. Both the repeated arousals and especially the chronic intermittent hypoxemia (CIH) attending cyclical CSA have been implicated. However, when supplemental O<sub>2</sub> was used to eliminate the intermittent hypoxemia accompanying airway obstruction in a sleeping canine model, the transient arousals by themselves were not sufficient to elicit the significant increase in daytime mean arterial pressure observed when both CIH and arousals accompanied the OSA<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d42307e228>The amount of oxygenated hemoglobin (HbO<sub>2</sub>) desaturation for any given apnea length will depend upon the starting (pre-apneic) position on the sigmoid HbO<sub>2</sub> dissociation curve, the end-expiratory lung volume at which the apnea begins, and the individuals oxygen consumption, cardiac output, and resultant arterial-to-venous O<sub>2</sub> content difference<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a>,<a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. Importantly, the fast reoxygenation phase at apnea terminationas occurs in patients with sleep apneawas found to be especially pro-inflammatory<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>. The molecular basis for the highly inflammatory response to intermittent (as opposed to constant) hypoxemia has been explained by Semenza and Prabhakar as an upregulation of both pro- and anti-oxidant transcription factors, hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) 1 and 2 in constant hypoxemia but a suppression of the anti-oxidant HIF 2 in intermittent hypoxemia<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. On the other hand, intermittent hypoxemia induced experimentally at very mild levels and for cycles that are several minutes in duration and for very brief periods of daily administration elicits a significant plasticity in phrenic motor neurons<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. This adaptive response to induced mild, brief CIH is in sharp contrast to that elicited via repetitive sleep apneas; the latter are unlikely to convey any significant beneficial biological benefit.</p><p class="" id=d42307e260>The amount of sleep apnea that may be clinically significant remains controversial; correlational studies claim that a frequency of as few as 5 to 10 events per hour has significant chronic cardiovascular consequences<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>. However, these claims have not been tested via interventional treatments in people with these lower levels of AHI. Alternatively, given the relative importance of the intermittent hypoxemia insult, it seems appropriate to define the severity of sleep-disordered breathing by using indices that are based on the degree of intermittent hypoxemia incurred<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e277>Mechanisms common to all types of central sleep apneas</h2><div class=section><a name=d42307e280 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Removal of the wakefulness drive to breathe</h3><p class="" id=d42307e285>The transient cessation of the medullary respiratory pattern generator neurons requires an unmasking of a sensitized apneic threshold in NREM sleep, as induced by a transient ventilatory overshoot involving both mild to moderate hypocapnia plus augmented Vt values. Carotid body denervation studies in rodents and canines demonstrate that the carotid bodies are required for sensing the low partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO<sub>2</sub>) and causing ventilatory instability and cyclical apneas<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a>,<a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. However, studies in the sleeping canine with isolated, perfused carotid chemoreceptors also showed that hypocapnia induced only at the level of either peripheral or central chemoreceptors was insufficient to elicit apnea<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup> and that hypocapnia induced at the level of the isolated, perfused carotid chemoreceptor caused a marked inhibitory effect on central CO<sub>2</sub> sensitivity. Thus, interdependence of function between peripheral and central chemoreceptors was an essential mediator of the apnea elicited via transient hypocapnia<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. Furthermore, vagal blockade in sleeping animals showed that inhibitory feedback from the lung stretch accompanying transient increases in Vt also contributes to the apnea following a ventilatory overshoot<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d42307e320>These inhibitory effects on breathing are opposed by excitatory central short-term potentiation mechanisms which preserve ventilatory drive immediately following chemoreceptor-driven ventilatory overshoots while awake but apparently not sufficiently to prevent apnea or hypopnea during NREM sleep<sup><a href="#ref-22">22</a>,<a href="#ref-23">23</a></sup>. So clearly, a significant wakefulness drive to breathe exists. It is manifested in sleep-induced inhibition of medullary inspiratory activity as well as withdrawal of tonic hypoglossal neuronal activity<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a>,<a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup> and removing it in NREM sleep also appears to compromise the control systems vigilance in protecting against chemo- and mechanoreflex-induced apneas. An additional example of an NREM sleep-induced compromise of control system vigilance occurs with respiratory compensation for loads induced by increased airway resistance which occur immediately to preserve ventilation during wakefulness but are absent in NREM sleep<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>. Groups of neurons within the ponto-medullary axis which might mediate these influences of wakefulness on respiratory control have been postulated, although definitive evidence on this complex problem remains elusive<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d42307e345>Two additional mechanisms to enhance post-apneic ventilatory overshoots include the following: (a) apneas are commonly prolonged until PaCO<sub>2</sub> rises above its normal pre-apneic, eupneic level<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>; and (b) transient arousals at end apnea are common and will enhance the magnitude of the transient ventilatory overshoot response to chemoreceptor stimulation.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d42307e356 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>High loop gain</h3><p class="" id=d42307e361>The unmasking of these reflex mechanisms underlies a sleep-induced central apnea. However, the repeated cyclical occurrence of transient ventilatory undershoots (apneas/hypopneas) and overshoots requires that respiratory control system loop gain to be elevated. Loop gain is a dynamic measure of how close a physiologic control system is to instability.</p><p class="" id=d42307e364><a name=d42307e365 class=n-a></a><div class="disp-formula panel"><math display=inline id=math1 overflow=scroll><mrow><mtable columnalign=left><mtr><mtd><mrow><mtext>Loop</mtext><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mtext>Gain</mtext><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mn>=</mn><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mtext>controller</mtext><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mtext>gain</mtext><mspace width="0.7em"></mspace><mo></mo><mspace width="0.7em"></mspace><mtext>plant</mtext><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mtext>gain</mtext></mrow></mtd></mtr><mtr><mtd><mrow><mtext>Loop</mtext><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mtext>Gain</mtext><mspace width="0.1em"></mspace><mo>=</mo><mrow><mo>[</mo><mrow><mtable><mtr><mtd><mrow><mtext>VE</mtext><mo>/</mo><mtext></mtext><mo>PaCO2</mo></mrow></mtd></mtr><mtr><mtd><mrow><mtext>(&gt;</mtext><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mtext>&lt;</mtext><mspace width="0.3em"></mspace><mi>e</mi><mi>u</mi><mi>p</mi><mi>n</mi><mi>e</mi><mi>a</mi><mtext>)</mtext></mrow></mtd></mtr></mtable></mrow><mo>]</mo></mrow><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mo></mo><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mrow><mo>[</mo><mrow><mfrac><mrow><mtext>PaCO2</mtext></mrow><mrow><mtext>lung</mtext><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mtext>vol</mtext><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace></mrow></mfrac><mo></mo><mtable><mtr><mtd><mrow><mtext>transit</mtext><mspace width="0.2em"></mspace><mtext>time</mtext></mrow></mtd></mtr><mtr><mtd><mrow><mtext>delay</mtext></mrow></mtd></mtr></mtable></mrow><mo>]</mo></mrow></mrow></mtd></mtr></mtable></mrow></math></div></p><p class="" id=d42307e551>The principal component of a high loop gain is an excessive chemosensitivity to CO<sub>2</sub> both above and below the level of eupneic ventilation. This high gain means that both ventilatory undershoots in response to a transient hypocapnia and ventilatory overshoots in response to a combination of apnea-induced hypoxemic and hypercapnic chemoreceptor stimuli are excessive, thereby precipitating the continued breathing periodicity<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a>,<a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. The concept of loop gain and its two principal componentscontroller (CO<sub>2</sub> chemosensitivity or VE/PaCO<sub>2</sub> slope) and plant (or PaCO<sub>2</sub>/VE) gainare illustrated in <a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>. (We acknowledge that the concept of loop gain was developed to characterize the dynamic behavior of linear systemsyet substantial non-linearities exist in virtually all components of the respiratory control system, especially when apnea occurs in the hypocapnic range<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. Furthermore, our schematic of loop gain in <a href="#f2">Figure 2</a> portrays only steady-state conditions for variables in the alveolar gas equation. Despite these limitations, considerable experimental evidence has accumulated in sleeping animals and humans to demonstrate that altering one or more components of loop gain elicits predictable influences on ventilatory stability/instability. See text and <a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>.)</p><a name=f2 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/20084/89e44b0f-4b6a-4542-a718-125f0df244a7_figure2.gif"><img alt="89e44b0f-4b6a-4542-a718-125f0df244a7_figure2.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/20084/89e44b0f-4b6a-4542-a718-125f0df244a7_figure2.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 2. The top panel shows effects of changing plant gain (PaCO<sub>2</sub>/VA) with steady-state hyper- or hypo-ventilation along the iso-metabolic hyperbola. Coincidentally, the apneic threshold which resides within 2 to 5 mm Hg PaCO<sub>2</sub> below normal awake levels in healthy individuals in NREM is not readily demonstrable in phasic REM sleep<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. The ventilatory response above eupnea to added CO<sub>2</sub> is also blunted in NREM (versus wakefulness) in part due to a truly reduced CO<sub>2</sub> chemoreceptor sensitivity<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup> but also to loss of tonic neural motor input to pharyngeal dilator muscles resulting in increased upper airway resistance<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>. Transient arousal at apnea termination temporarily restores this tonic input and reduces airway resistance, thereby contributing to ventilatory overshoot prior to sleep restoration. Similarly, in REM sleep, ventilatory responsiveness to CO<sub>2</sub> above eupnea is reduced in slope and shows an almost random Vt or diaphragmatic electromyography responses (or both) to increasing PaCO<sub>2</sub> (with or without coincident airway occlusion) rather than an orderly dose response as in quiet wakefulness or NREM sleep<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. Perhaps the erratic, sporadic increases in medullary inspiratory neuronal drive as observed during phasic REM in the sleeping cat override hypocapnic inhibition or hypercapnic stimulation of central respiratory motor output<sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup>.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e738>Central sleep apnea in congestive heart failure</h2><p class="" id=d42307e741>The key characteristics of CHF present an almost perfect storm to promote periodic breathing. We summarize the following key contributions:</p><div class=list><a name=d42307e744 class=n-a></a><ul><li><p id=d42307e748>Controller (and therefore loop) gain is elevated because of enhanced carotid chemosensitivity, leading to a highly sensitized apneic threshold (within 1 to 2 mm Hg of eupneic PaCO<sub>2</sub>) and an enhanced ventilatory overshoot to transient reductions in ventilation<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>. In turn, the pioneering work of Schultz <i>et al</i>. in animal models of CHF has demonstrated the critical importance of a reduced carotid sinus blood flow and shear stress in eliciting the enhanced carotid chemosensitivity<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a>,<a href="#ref-39">39</a></sup>.</p></li><li><p id=d42307e771>Elevated pulmonary vascular pressures in CHFespecially during recumbency with central fluid shifts<sup><a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup> contribute to lung C fiber and ventilatory stimulation, an increased CO<sub>2</sub> response slope sensitivity, and a narrowed CO<sub>2</sub> reserve<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>. This extra respiratory stimulus might also prevent mild hypoventilation and CO<sub>2</sub> retention in CHF which normally occurs at sleep onset<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>.</p></li><li><p id=d42307e799>Patients with CHF commonly have a compromised vasodilatory and vasoconstrictive cerebrovascular response to transient hypercapnia and hypocapnia, respectively<sup><a href="#ref-42">42</a></sup>. This means that medullary CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sup>+</sup> levels are less protected and more labile in response to transient changes in systemic PaCO<sub>2</sub>; thus, the ventilatory response slopes to PaCO<sub>2</sub> above and below eupnea are sensitized.</p></li><li><p id=d42307e821>Low cardiac output in CHF means prolonged circulation times, thereby prolonging the delivery of altered blood gases from lung to carotid body (CB) and prolonging the periodic respiratory cycles.</p></li></ul></div><p class="" id=d42307e826>A common misconception particularly with reference to causes of periodic breathing in CHF is that a reduced steady-state PaCO<sub>2</sub> will precipitate periodic breathing, presumably because the patients eupneic PaCO<sub>2</sub> is moved closer to their apneic threshold<sup><a href="#ref-43">43</a>,<a href="#ref-44">44</a></sup>. This is a misconception because (as shown in <a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>) the position of the apneic threshold relative to eupneic PaCO<sub>2</sub> is labile; that is, the CO<sub>2</sub> reserve below eupnea is determined by the chemosensitivity and slope of the CO<sub>2</sub> response<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup>. Importantly, a reduced steady-state PaCO<sub>2</sub> actually reduces plant gain, meaning that a larger transient ventilatory response is required to lower PaCO<sub>2</sub> to reach the apneic threshold (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>, top panel). Thus, the reduced steady-state PaCO<sub>2</sub> is an important protector against apnea; alternatively, steady-state hypoventilation and hypercapnia elevate plant gain and precipitate ventilatory instability<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup> (also see examples under the Non-positive airway pressure (PAP) treatments of CSA section below).</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e879>Central sleep apnea in hypoxic environments</h2><p class="" id=d42307e882>Most sojourners with a wide spectrum of hypoxic chemosensitivity experience periodic breathing in hypoxic environments<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>; exceptions include healthy high-altitude natives with markedly depressed hypoxic chemosensitivity<sup><a href="#ref-46">46</a></sup>. In the sojourner, the development of periodic breathing during NREM sleep in hypoxia occurs within a few minutes of hypoxic onset as an initial hyperventilation and reduced PaCO<sub>2</sub> evolve into progressively larger hyperpnea/hypopnea oscillations in Vt and thenfollowing an augmented inspiration when PaCO<sub>2</sub> reaches the apneic thresholdexpiratory time is prolonged and hyperpnea/apnea combinations ensue with a cycle period of 20 to 25 seconds. Loop gain is increased in hypoxia because chemoreceptor CO<sub>2</sub> sensitivity is increased more than plant gain is reduced (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>). Thus, the apneic threshold resides within 1 to 2 mm Hg of the eupneic PaCO<sub>2</sub> during NREM sleep, ensuring a significant ventilatory undershoot with even small levels of transient hypocapnia<sup><a href="#ref-47">47</a></sup>. Furthermore, the ventilatory overshoot at apnea terminations is amplified because of the synergistic stimulatory effects on carotid chemoreceptors of hypoxemia plus hypercapnia (that is, asphyxia). Chemoreceptor sensory inputs drive both medullary rhythm-generating neurons and arousal-producing cortical neurons<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>. These combined oscillating powerful drives and inhibitors to breathing likely explain, respectively, the abrupt large ventilatory overshoots at apnea termination and abrupt ventilatory undershoots at end hyperpnea in hypoxic environments, resulting in the cluster-type breathing pattern (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>).</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e927>Opiate-induced central sleep apnea</h2><p class="" id=d42307e930>Respiratory depression, first with hypoventilation and then outright apneas, occurs because of the actions of opioids on mu and kappa opioid receptors, and the most profound respiratory depression effects occur during a background of anesthesia and NREM sleep<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. Opioid receptors located on both the rhythm-generating medullary neurons in the pre-Btzinger and RTN/pFRG complexes are inhibited, and both hypoxic and hypercapnic ventilatory response sensitivities are depressed upon acute intravenous or long-term oral opioid administration. Hypoglossal motor neurons are also depressed with high doses of opioids and upper airway resistance is increased<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a>,<a href="#ref-48">48</a><a href="#ref-51">51</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d42307e947>The dilemma in understanding the role of an enhanced loop gain in opioid-induced periodic breathing is thatunlike those cases with CHF or in hypoxiaperiodic breathing occurs in the face of depressed central respiratory neurons and compromised chemosensitivity. So, although we might expect apnea to occur at sleep onset with opioid use, there is no mechanism for repeated cyclical apneas based on the model of increased controller and loop gains. One possibility here is an increased plant gain secondary to opioid-induced steady-state hypoventilation and CO<sub>2</sub> retention during sleep with subsequent displacement of ventilation and PaCO<sub>2</sub> down the isometabolic hyperbola (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>, top panel). This means that the apneic threshold will be reached with extremely small transient increases in alveolar ventilation<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup>. The probability of steady-state hypoventilation has been documented indirectly in some chronic opioid users during wakefulness and sleep<sup><a href="#ref-52">52</a></sup>, but more studies quantifying CO<sub>2</sub> retention need to be conducted during sleep in order to determine whether there is a significant potential role for increased plant gain. If the higher plant gain was responsible for initiating apneas and some periodicity in chronic opioid users, CIH over time might be expected to sensitize the peripheral chemoreceptors and thereby exacerbate the periodic breathing in a positive feed-forward fashion<sup><a href="#ref-50">50</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e978>Mixed: central/obstructive apneas</h2><p class="" id=d42307e981>Centrally induced periodicities and apneas occurring in patients with underlying collapsible airways will elicit repeated obstructions at the nadir of an oscillating central respiratory motor output during sleep<sup><a href="#ref-53">53</a><a href="#ref-55">55</a></sup>. The neurophysiologic basis for this effect of central respiratory motor output on central and obstructive apnea resides in the influence of efferent central respiratory motor output on activation/inhibition of motor neurons serving both upper airway pharyngeal dilator musculature and the chest wall respiratory pump musculature<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>. Accordingly, it is common to see central, obstructive, and mixed apneas in patients with CHF, even within the same night, possibly secondary to factors such as fluid accumulation around the upper airway and changes in head, neck, and body position<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-56">56</a></sup>. Also, predominantly patients with OSA acquire CSA as chemoreceptor sensitivity increases and central respiratory motor output instability occurs during sojourn at high altitudes<sup><a href="#ref-57">57</a></sup>. Similarly, a residual CSA is often unmasked in patients with OSA during the early stages of continuous PAP (CPAP) treatment (that is, so-called complex sleep apnea). An increased prevalence of OSA (as well as CSA) in chronic opioid use<sup><a href="#ref-58">58</a></sup> may also reflect central instabilities in respiratory motor output superimposed on a collapsible airway.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e1013>Central sleep apnea treatment via positive airway pressure</h2><p class="" id=d42307e1016>The use of PAP is routinely the clinicians firstand often onlytreatment choice for any type of sleep-disordered breathing. However, evidence to date shows that this is not always the best approach for cases of predominant CSA. CPAP treats less than half of central apneas effectively and when it is applied in large clinical trials in patients with CHF, significant reductions in CSA did not appear in most patients until after the first several months of treatment. Patient survival was eventually compromised in these trials<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-56">56</a>,<a href="#ref-59">59</a></sup>. Low adherence to CPAP is always a major problem, especially if the failure to use CPAP occurs in the early morning hours when REM sleep is prevalent and accompanied by high levels of sympathetic nerve activity and cardiovascular stress<sup><a href="#ref-60">60</a>,<a href="#ref-61">61</a></sup>. Theoretically, assisted servo-ventilation should be an ideal approach to treat CSA, mixed apnea, and OSA because it delivers inspiratory pressure support to increase flow rate in the face of hypoventilation, also, it titrates expiratory PAP to eliminate airway obstructions<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-56">56</a></sup>. However, to date, adaptive servo-ventilation (ASV) produced no survival benefit and actually increased mortality in CHF patients with CSA<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-56">56</a></sup>. Speculation on the reasons for this ASV failure points to potential deleterious effects of PAP on left ventricular function, especially if the ASV device imposes excessive intrathoracic pressures in vulnerable patients<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. More thorough study of the effects of varying levels and types of PAP on left and right heart function is clearly indicated! In addition, alternative treatments to PAP must be explored.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e1057>Non-positive airway pressure treatments of central sleep apnea aimed at ameliorating one or more components of high loop gain</h2><div class=section><a name=d42307e1060 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Congestive heart failure</h3><p class="" id=d42307e1065>The use of nocturnal supplemental O<sub>2</sub> in the mildly hyperoxic range addresses the primary problem of excessive chemoreceptor sensitivity and has successfully reduced AHI and eliminated CIH in several descriptive studies using relatively small numbers of patients with CHF<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-62">62</a>,<a href="#ref-63">63</a></sup>. The durations of remaining apneas are lengthened. In select cases of OSA plus CSA, supplemental O<sub>2</sub> is also effective by itself<sup><a href="#ref-64">64</a><a href="#ref-66">66</a></sup> or in combination with CPAP or ASV devices<sup><a href="#ref-66">66</a>,<a href="#ref-67">67</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d42307e1098>Oral acetazolamide will reduce plant gain to varying extents, depending upon the magnitude of the coincident reduction in PaCO<sub>2</sub>, and eliminate significant amounts of CSA and also reduce fluid load and associated airway compression via its diuretic actions<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a href="#ref-68">68</a></sup>. Rebreathing added dead space with less than a 2 mm Hg increase in partial pressure of end-tidal carbon dioxide (PetCO<sub>2</sub>) reduces plant gain and markedly lowers CSA, transient arousals, and CIH in patients with<b></b>CHF<sup><a href="#ref-44">44</a>,<a href="#ref-69">69</a></sup>; even OSA is markedly reduced via supplemental CO<sub>2</sub> in select cases<sup><a href="#ref-64">64</a>,<a href="#ref-70">70</a></sup>. Importantly, augmenting central respiratory motor drive to reduce CSA must also weigh the potential negative effects of raising chemosensory input on arousals, sleep state, or autonomic cardiovascular regulation (or a combination of these) which may occur if the imposed hypercapnia is excessive<sup><a href="#ref-71">71</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d42307e1138>Exercise training has also been shown to reduce chemosensitivity in animal models and patients with CHF<sup><a href="#ref-72">72</a>,<a href="#ref-73">73</a></sup>, presumably because of the intermittent increase in blood flow and shear stress in the carotid bifurcation<sup><a href="#ref-39">39</a></sup>. Chronic statin therapy in rodents with CHF has also shown promise as a means of reducing chemosensitivity and CSA<sup><a href="#ref-74">74</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d42307e1156>It is clearly time to introduce clinical trials using these non-PAP approachesalone or in combinationto reducing loop gain<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. Hopefully, such trials will also include assessments of loop gain components, arousal threshold sensitivity, and airway collapsibility so that investigators will have the tools to determine the causes of inter-individual variations in response to these proposed treatments<sup><a href="#ref-64">64</a>,<a href="#ref-67">67</a></sup>. To address future potential treatments, we note that pharmacologic and even gene transfer approaches are under development in animal models for reducing carotid chemoreceptor hypersensitivity. Specific targets include downregulation of carotid chemoreceptor excitatory neurotransmitters or adrenergic receptors as well as upregulation of shear stresssensitive transcription factors to enhance carotid sinus blood flow<sup><a href="#ref-39">39</a>,<a href="#ref-75">75</a>,<a href="#ref-76">76</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d42307e1181 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Hypoxic environments</h3><p class="" id=d42307e1186>PAP administered via servo-ventilator or CPAP was not consistently effective in reducing CSA in hypoxia<sup><a href="#ref-57">57</a>,<a href="#ref-77">77</a></sup>. Nocturnal administration of supplemental O<sub>2</sub> sufficient to return SaO<sub>2</sub> within 2 to 3% of sea level values eliminated periodic breathing at high altitudes almost immediately<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a href="#ref-77">77</a></sup>. Oral acetazolamide elicits steady-state hyperventilation and reduces PaCO<sub>2</sub> and loop gain, thereby reducing periodic breathing, in sleeping sojourners, whereas adding 1 to 2 mm Hg PaCO<sub>2</sub>via increased fractional inspired carbon dioxide (FiCO<sub>2</sub>)is sufficient to completely eliminate periodic breathing via reduced plant gain<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a>,<a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. OSA patients sojourning at even moderate altitudes experience periodic CSA and adding oral acetazolamide to their CPAP treatment significantly reduced both central and obstructive apneas<sup><a href="#ref-57">57</a></sup>. A limited amount of data have shown that these various means of reducing sleep-disordered breathing at high altitude also substantially reduce periodic transient arousals from sleep, increase the time spent in deep sleep, and diminish the carryover daytime sequelae of periodic breathing and CIH, such as increased systemic blood pressure, fatigue, neurocognitive impairment, and hypersomnolence<sup><a href="#ref-78">78</a>,<a href="#ref-79">79</a></sup>. The effect of preventing periodic breathing in hypoxia on nocturnal and daytime systemic and pulmonary vascular resistance at rest and during exercise needs to be addressed.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d42307e1238 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Chronic opiate use-induced sleep-disordered breathing</h3><p class="" id=d42307e1243>The aim here is to treat sleep-induced hypoventilation and any associated increase in airway resistance and apneic and hypopneic events. Opioid withdrawal or dose reductions in chronic users eliminates or significantly lowers the sleep-disordered breathing and CIH<sup><a href="#ref-80">80</a></sup>. Again, CPAP is not an effective means of treatment; however, non-invasive positive pressure ventilation devices with bilateral pressure support and backup respiratory rates should be ideal for this treatment. Accordingly, adaptive servo-ventilators were substantially more effective than CPAP in reducing sleep-disordered breathing and arousals in chronic opioid users with a relatively high adherence rate (compared with CPAP) followed over several months<sup><a href="#ref-48">48</a></sup>. Increasing the (suspected) reduced ventilatory drive and hypoventilation during sleep with opiate users might be achieved by reducing plant gain via ventilatory stimulation using oral acetazolamide or via dead space rebreathing just sufficiently to raise PetCO<sub>2</sub> 1 to 2 mm Hg. To date, these approaches to raise central respiratory motor output during sleep have not been attempted in chronic opioid users with CSA.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e1261>Summary</h2><p class="" id=d42307e1264>CSA either by itself or in combination with obstructive apnea is not uncommon among patients with sleep-disordered breathing, especially in heart failure, at high altitudes, and with chronic opioid use. The CIH accompanying CSA elicits cardiovascular consequences which carry over to the waking state. The underlying causes of CSA are complex and not completely understood, although increased loop gain is now well established as a common feature. Treating CSA effectively and safely has had little success to date, presumably because CPAP has been the automatic treatment of choice among sleep medicine clinicians. Accordingly, it is time to match treatments with causative mechanisms but with the understanding that not all patients with CSA will share the same underlying causes.</p></div><div id=article1-back class=generated-article-footer><div class=back-section><a name=d42307e1 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d42563>Grant information</h2><p>Original research cited in the article was supported by the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (R21 HL137874).</p><p> <i> The funders had no role in study design, data collection and analysis, decision to publish, or preparation of the manuscript</i> </p></div><div class=back-section><a name=d42307e1271 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d42307e1273>Acknowledgments</h2><p class="" id=d42307e1276>The author acknowledges the excellent assistance provided by Benjamin J.
