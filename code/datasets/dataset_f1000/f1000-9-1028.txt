immune microenvironment. iAtlas allows researchers to study immune response characterizations and patterns for individual tumor types, tumor subtypes, and immune subtypes. iAtlas supports computation To broaden the utility of these therapies to more patients, fundamental research is required to improve our understanding of tumor-immune interactions—allowing the next-generation of therapeutics and treatment strategies to emerge<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. Advances in the IO field are impeded by the inaccessibility of IO study data and results and lack of data standardization, limiting the ability to easily compare results across studies. This has led to the underutilization of existing data, unnecessary study duplication, and failure to achieve rapid consensus in the field<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. With the vast increase in the number and scope of IO projects expected in the coming years combined with widespread adoption of genomics and other high dimensional technologies, these problems will be compounded going forward.</p><p class="" id=d17589e318>We developed the Cancer Research Institute (CRI) iAtlas portal (<a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.cri-iatlas.org/" id=d17589e320>https://www.cri-iatlas.org</a>) to integrate IO research data, with the goal of providing an interactive, exploratory hub for the IO research community. In doing so, we hope to improve the accessibility and utility of critical resources generated from IO studies. iAtlas is a set of analytic modules—hosted on the web—for studying interactions between tumors and the immune microenvironment. These modules allow researchers to explore associations among a variety of immune characterizations as well as with genomic and clinical phenotypes.</p><p class="" id=d17589e324>The initial release of iAtlas (April 5, 2018) provided a rich resource to complement analysis results from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) Research Network on the TCGA data set comprising over 10,000 tumor samples and 33 tumor types<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup> (“The Immune Landscape of Cancer”; here referred to as “Immune Landscape”). This study identified six immune subtypes that span cancer tissue types and molecular subtypes, and found that these subtypes differ by somatic aberrations, microenvironment, and survival. Per-sample characterizations included total lymphocytic infiltrate (from DNA methylation as well as H&amp;E imaging data), estimated cell type fractions, immune gene signature expression, MHC/HLA type and expression, antigen presentation machinery, T cell and B cell receptor repertoire inference, viral/microbial characterization, associations with pathway disruption and activity, and other analysis results. The Immune Landscape<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup> manuscript reported on the most novel and potentially therapeutically salient statistical associations between these immune subtypes and the results of the immune characterization. We have continued to develop and evolve the CRI iAtlas application; here, we report the technical design and implementation of iAtlas up to and including the recently released version 1.2<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. This version includes new features requested by users including: (1) user-defined loading of sample cohorts, (2) a tool for classifying expression data into immune subtypes, and (3) integration of TIL mapping from digital pathology images.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d17589e342>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d17589e345 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Implementation</h3><p class="" id=d17589e350>iAtlas is a web-based application to enable data exploration for clinicians, biologists, and informaticists. The inputs and architecture of the application are described below.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d17589e354 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Data</h3><p class="" id=d17589e359>The iAtlas app uses structured data and outputs from the Immune Landscape<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup> study and the TCGA PanCancer Atlas initiative<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>, which harmonized TCGA data, ensuring uniform quality control and sample inclusion, batch effect detection, normalization across platforms, combination mutation calling from multiple centers, and robustly compiled clinical and outcome data. Each iAtlas <b>Analysis module</b> is designed as a Shiny module, allowing simple integration of new analytical functionality. iAtlas uses the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.tidyverse.org/" id=d17589e540>tidyverse</a><sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup> family of R packages (e.g., <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/package=dplyr" id=d17589e547>dplyr</a><sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/package=tidyr" id=d17589e553>tidyr</a><sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/package=purrr" id=d17589e559>purrr</a><sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/package=stringr" id=d17589e565>stringr</a><sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/package=tibble" id=d17589e572>tibble</a><sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a></sup>) as well as the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/package=wrapr" id=d17589e578>wrapr</a><sup><a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup> package to assist with tidy evaluation. These functions power the data transformations of internal tabular data that are then used to create the interactive plots (i.e., with the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://plotly.com/" id=d17589e584>plotly</a><sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup> graphing library) and data tables (via the <i>DT</i><sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup> wrapper to the <i>DataTables</i> library) seen through the iAtlas modules. We also make heavy use of the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://cran.r-project.org/package=crosstalk" id=d17589e600>crosstalk</a><sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup> package to enable event-driven updates to the application state. The core iAtlas application is hosted on <a target=xrefwindow href="https://shinyapps.io/" id=d17589e606>https://shinyapps.io</a>.</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/27745/607c142d-2100-40dd-8fd2-2b24ce789ca6_figure1.gif"><img alt="607c142d-2100-40dd-8fd2-2b24ce789ca6_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/27745/607c142d-2100-40dd-8fd2-2b24ce789ca6_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. iAtlas architecture overview.</h3><p id=d17589e620>(<b>A</b>) Structured data from immunogenomic analyses, including the Immune Landscape<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup> study and expanding over time, are organized and stored as flat (i.e., <i>feather</i>) files within Synapse and made available alongside the application code in GitHub. The layout of pages and sections within the iAtlas Explorer is driven by the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://rstudio.github.io/shinydashboard/" id=d17589e680>shinydashboard</a><sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup> package.</p><a name=f2 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/27745/607c142d-2100-40dd-8fd2-2b24ce789ca6_figure2.gif"><img alt="607c142d-2100-40dd-8fd2-2b24ce789ca6_figure2.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/27745/607c142d-2100-40dd-8fd2-2b24ce789ca6_figure2.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 2. iAtlas Explorer.</h3><p id=d17589e697>A range of <b>Analysis modules</b> (blue boxes above) are available that span from clinical to molecular and imaging data types. Modules in this space of the portal enable users to “bring their own data” for processing through immunogenomic algorithms that drive some of the results presented in the Analysis modules described above.</p><p class="" id=d17589e925><b>Immune Subtype Prediction:</b> This tool performs classification of RNA-seq data into one of six immune subtypes as described in the Immune Landscape<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup> study. Using a new ensemble model based on XGBoost<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>, researchers can upload their own data for classification<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. Each member of the ensemble was trained on a random subset of previously reported immune subtypes<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup> and features (described below) based on gene expression data from the TCGA PanCancer Atlas Initiative<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>. All code and methods have been confirmed as reproducible. An R package is available on GitHub (<a target=xrefwindow href="https://github.com/CRI-iAtlas/ImmuneSubtypeClassifier" id=d17589e950>https://github.com/CRI-iAtlas/ImmuneSubtypeClassifier</a>)<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d17589e958>The submitted expression data—subsetted to the 485 genes that comprised the 5 signatures that produced the immune subtypes—are used to generate robust features of three types: quartiles, binary gene-pairs, and signature-pairs. For example, given a single sample, genes are binned into quartiles and given a bin label (quartile features). Then, similar to the “Top Scoring Pairs” classifier<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>, genes are paired, and given binary values depending on whether (<i>g<sub>i</sub> &gt; g<sub>j</sub></i>) for two gene expression values, <i>g<sub>i</sub></i> and <i>g<sub>j</sub></i>. Lastly, signature-pair features are calculated using the five immune subtype signatures, where <i>s<sub>mn</sub></i> = ∑<sub><i>ij</i></sub>(<i>g<sub>im</sub> &gt; g<sub>jn</sub></i>)/<i>k</i>, where <i>g<sub>im</sub></i> is gene <i>i</i> from signature <i>m</i>, <i>g<sub>jn</sub></i> is gene <i>j</i> from signature <i>n</i>, and <i>k</i> is the number of gene pairs considered resulting in a value between 0 and 1. The features are computed independently for each sample, and do not require normalization across samples. These features are given to a trained XGBoost classifier which returns a probability of being in any of the six subtypes. Lastly, a “best call” is made with a final trained XGBoost classifier using the six probabilities as input. To validate the robustness of the classifier, TCGA data were processed using four different software pipelines and normalization, showing that classification performance was independent of the gene expression quantification method<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. Along with a downloadable table of results, visualizations are also provided. This tool is a convenient way for researchers to apply the methods of the Immune Landscape<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup> study to their own data without difficult statistical coding.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d17589e1042>Operation</h2><p class="" id=d17589e1045>To use iAtlas, access the web app via <a target=xrefwindow href="https://www.cri-iatlas.org" id=d17589e1047>https://www.cri-iatlas.org</a>. For example, we asked: “<i>What is the expression level of PD-L1, a therapeutically important protein, in subtypes of breast cancer?”</i> To answer this question, from the landing page, we first selected the <i>“TCGA Subtype”</i> sample group, followed by the <i>“Breast Invasive Carcinoma (BRCA)”</i> study subset. Next, we selected the <b>Immunomodulators</b> module (<a href="#f4">Figure 4</a>). Based on a very quick scan of the drop down, we didn’t see any names that matched our gene of interest, so we scrolled further down on the page to view the table of ‘Immunomodulator Annotations’. By typing in the first few letters of a gene name (e.g., “PD...”) into the ‘Search’ field, the table was filtered to a set of matching genes, and we could see that <i>“PD-L1”</i> is the <tt>Friendly Name</tt> for the gene <i>“CD274”</i> (the approved gene symbol on genenames.org). After returning to the <tt><b>Select or Search for Variable</b></tt> drop down menu above and selecting <i>“CD274 (PD-L1)”</i>, we were able to see a display of violin plots showing the distributions of gene expression across BRCA molecular subtypes. We could then visually compare distributions between subtypes, noticing for example the elevated expression level in the Her2 subtype compared to Basal breast cancer. These comparisons can guide further characterization not only of how gene expression can differ between TCGA subtypes of breast cancer, but also how these subtype-specific differences might correlate with clinical outcomes, as investigated in other studies<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a>–<a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>. In order to classify new data, we submitted data as a text file, in this case tab separated, with the first column containing gene IDs and later columns containing samples. A provided example file can be found in the description text. The first row of the data was a header containing sample IDs. Gene IDs can be either HGNC gene symbols (preferred), Entrez ID, or Ensembl identifiers. The locally available data was selected using the <i>Browse</i> button, and the file delimiter was selected, along with gene ID type, using drop down menus. Hitting the <i>GO</i> button produced classifications, signature scores, and cluster probabilities, which were reported in a table that was downloaded as a csv, xlsx, or pdf file. In addition, a barplot with the frequency of predicted subtypes for the submitted data was displayed.</p><p class="" id=d17589e1434>All data required to run the application and describe the Use Cases href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d17589e1448>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d17589e1451>CRI iAtlas is a platform that facilitates analysis and exploration of the tumor immune microenvironment by making IO-related data and tools accessible to the research community. iAtlas builds upon the comprehensive TCGA analysis of tumor-immune interactions on 10,000 tumors and illustrates how commonalities and differences of the immune response across 33 tumor types can provide clues for advancing therapeutics. iAtlas provides researchers with the tools to dive deeper into immunogenomic and clinical data and to develop and refine hypotheses regarding tumor-immune interactions that will empower researchers to gain insight and design the next generation of immuno-oncology treatment strategies.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d17589e1458>Data can be found in the NCI Genomic Data Commons (<a target=xrefwindow href="https://gdc.cancer.gov/about-data/publications/panimmune"
