ρ=0.994, Cohen’s&nbsp; κ=0.885), and the combination of the two methods identified samples (n=41) with aberrant amplification profiles.<br/> <b>Conclusions:</b> The combination of maxRatio and FP could improve the predictive value of the assay. </div> <div class=abstract-for-mobile> <div class="margin-top-30 padding-bottom-30 research-layout is-centered"> <button class="primary orange-text An accurate viral load (VL), typically carried out by quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR), is pivotal for addressing the efficacy of antiviral therapies<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. The threshold level of detection for the HIV-1 (VL) has been reported in the range 20–44 viral genomic copies per milliliter (c/mL)<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a>,<a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d18317e244>qPCR data are usually analyzed by the fit-point (FP) method, which assumes equal amplification efficiency between samples<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. However, anomalies in the background fluorescence at low template input, can affect the quantification<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a>–<a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>. An alternative method, maxRatio, was introduced to overcome these issues<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>. It has been reported that maxRatio conferred a marginal increase in assay accuracy over FP<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d18317e269>FP provides only a quantification cycle (Cq) value, which is then used to calculate VL. MaxRatio, instead, gives two parameters: one associated with the reaction’s efficiency (MR) and one equivalent to, albeit distinct from, Cq. These two parameters can be linked to bestow a quantitative cycle (FCNA) compensated for inhibition.</p><p class="" id=d18317e272>In the present work, we aimed to determine whether maxRatio could improve the determination of HIV-1 VL. We compared the quantification of HIV-1 VL computed by FP and maxRatio on a dataset generated with the QIAgen artus HIV assay, which has a reported limit of detection of 35.5 c/mL, and we showed that maxRatio could pinpoint samples with abnormal amplification profiles.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d18317e278>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d18317e281 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Dataset</h3><p class="" id=d18317e286>The amplification data (see <i>Underlying data</i><sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>) obtained with the QIAgen artus HI Virus-1 RT-PCR<i></i> kit were collected by the Public Health England Clinical Microbiology and Public Health Laboratory, Addenbrooke’s Hospital, Hills Road, Cambridge CB2 0QW, UK, during the year 2016. All data were anonymized before use. The reactions were subdivided into clinical samples, control dilutions (CDs), and non-template controls (NTCs). The CDs were based on known dilutions of <i>in vitro</i> transcribed HIV-1 RNA provided by the artus kit, corresponding to 405, 4,050, 40,500, and 405,000 c/mL.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d18317e301 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Data analysis</h3><p class="" id=d18317e306>The FP method generated the Cq by registering the fractional cycle where the fluorescence passed the threshold of 0.2 units. The maxRatio transform of the amplification data and determination of the cut-offs were computed as previously described<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a>,<a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup>. Different operators visually inspected the reaction’s profiles and classified each reaction as either passed or failed. Using R v.3.6, linear models (standard curves, SC) were built on the CDs and applied to calculate the copy numbers according to the formula 10<sup>(<i>x</i> – <i>b</i>)/<i>m</i></sup> where <i>x</i> is the quantitative cycle (either Cq or FCNA), <i>b</i> and <i>m</i> are the intercept and slope, respectively, of the linear models<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. VL correlation was obtained with the Pearson product-moment coefficient ρ<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup> and agreement between methods was tested with the Cohen’s κ<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>; both are reported with their 95% confidence interval (CI).</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d18317e353>Results</h2><p class="" id=d18317e356>The present dataset was derived from 122 individual <i>artus</i> HIV-1 runs, corresponding to 2,544 reactions (480 CDs, 122 NTCs and 1,931 clinical samples). Contrary to our expectation, the SCs we built with either FP or maxRatio were virtually the same. Both methods gave SD VLs significantly divergent from the expected copy numbers at the lower and upper CDs, and maxRatio was, in general, more discrepant from the expected copy numbers than FP. Even concerning the samples’ quantification, the two methods produced essentially the same VLs.</p><p class="" id=d18317e672>The main difference between the two methods was in terms of sample’s reactivity. By accepting the reactions identified as non-reactive by FP, but reactive by maxRatio, there would have been 18 false-negative results. Conversely, 15 samples identified as non-reactive by maxRatio showed aberrant IC that raised quality control, rather than false-positive, issues overlooked by FP.</p><p class="" id=d18317e675>The current use of maxRatio is to confirm the reactivity determined by FP on the Abbott m2000rt platform. Our data supported this combination as the most effective approach for screening purposes. Samples in disagreement between FP and maxRatio would require further assessment that could reduce the workload involved in issuing the results and minimize the risk of providing false results.</p><p class="" id=d18317e678>The present work had some limitations. Firstly, the sample size was small. Secondly, the CDs were prepared by diluting the control samples provided in the kit, but the actual concentration was not measured. Finally, we did not have access to the actual issued results; thus, we could not confirm the official VL values.</p><p class="" id=d18317e682>In conclusion, we compared FP and maxRatio in providing HIV-1 VL. Contrary to our expectations, maxRatio did not give a better quantification than FP, but combining the two methods could minimize issuing false results.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d18317e688>Data availability</h2><div class=section><a name=d18317e691 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Underlying data</h3><p class="" id=d18317e696>Harvard Dataverse: artusHIV_amplificationData. <a target=xrefwindow href="https://doi.org/10.7910/DVN/0QQNPF" id=d18317e698>https://doi.org/10.7910/DVN/0QQNPF</a></p><p class="" id=d18317e701>This project contains the following underlying data:</p><div class=list><a name=d18317e704 class=n-a></a><ul><li><p id=d18317e708>amplificationDataRaw.tab.
