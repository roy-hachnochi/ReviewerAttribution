            <meta name="dc.description" content=" Background : Strategies that involve manipulations of the odour-orientation of gravid malaria vectors could lead to novel attract-and-kill interventions. Recent work has highlighted the potential involvement of graminoid plants in luring vectors to oviposition sites. This study aimed to analyse the association between water-indicating graminoid plants (Cyperaceae, sedges), other abiotic and biotic factors and the presence and abundance of early instar Anopheles larvae in aquatic habitats as a proxy indicator for oviposition. 
 Methods : A cross-sectional survey of 110 aquatic habitats along the shores of Lake Victoria was done during the rainy season. Habitats were sampled for mosquito larvae using the sweep-net method and habitat characteristics recorded. 
 Results : Anopheles arabiensis was the dominant species identified from aquatic habitats. Larvae of the secondary malaria vectors such as Anopheles coustani, An. rufipes and An. maculipalpis were found only in habitats covered with graminoids, whereas An. arabiensis, An. ziemanni and An. pharoensis were found in both habitats with and without graminoid plants. The hypothesis that sedges might be positively associated with the presence and abundance of early instar Anopheles larvae could not be confirmed. The dominant graminoid plants in the habitats were Panicum repens , Cynodon dactylon in the Poaceae family and Cyperus rotundus in the Cyperaceae family. All of these habitats supported abundant immature vector populations. The presence of early instar larvae was significantly and positively associated with swamp habitat types (OR=22, 95% CI=6-86, P&amp;lt;0.001) and abundance of late Anopheles larvae (OR=359, CI=33-3941, P&amp;lt;0.001), whilst the association was negative with tadpole presence (OR=0.1, CI=0.0.01-0.5, P=0.008). 
 Conclusions : Early instar malaria vectors were abundant in habitats densely vegetated with graminoid plants in the study area but specific preference for any of the graminoids could not be detected. In search for oviposition cues, it might be useful to screen for chemical volatiles released from all dominant plant species.">

  "description": "Background: Strategies that involve manipulations of the odour-orientation of gravid malaria vectors could lead to novel attract-and-kill interventions. Recent work has highlighted the potential involvement of graminoid plants in luring vectors to oviposition sites. This study aimed to analyse the association between water-indicating graminoid plants (Cyperaceae, sedges), other abiotic and biotic factors and the presence and abundance of early instar Anopheles larvae in aquatic habitats as a proxy indicator for oviposition.

Recent work has highlighted the potential involvement of graminoid plants in luring vectors to oviposition sites. This study aimed to analyse the association between water-indicating graminoid plants (Cyperaceae, sedges), other abiotic and biotic factors and the presence and abundance of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae in aquatic habitats as a proxy indicator for oviposition.<br/> <b>Methods</b>: A cross-sectional survey of 110 aquatic habitats along the shores of Lake Victoria was done during the rainy season. Habitats were sampled for mosquito larvae using the sweep-net method and habitat characteristics recorded.<br/> <b>Results</b>: <i>Anopheles arabiensis</i> was the dominant species identified from aquatic habitats. Larvae of the secondary malaria vectors such as <i>Anopheles coustani, An. rufipes </i>and<i> An. maculipalpis </i>were found only in habitats covered with graminoids, whereas <i>An. arabiensis, An. ziemanni</i> and<i> An. pharoensis </i>were found in both habitats with and without graminoid plants. The hypothesis that sedges might be positively associated with the presence and abundance of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae could not be confirmed. The dominant graminoid plants in the habitats were <i>Panicum repens</i>, <i>Cynodon dactylon</i> in the Poaceae family and <i>Cyperus rotundus </i>in the Cyperaceae family. All of these habitats supported abundant immature vector populations. The presence of early instar larvae was significantly and positively associated with swamp habitat types (OR=22, 95% CI=6-86, P&lt;0.001) and abundance of late <i>Anopheles</i> larvae (OR=359, CI=33-3941, P&lt;0.001), whilst the association was negative with tadpole presence (OR=0.1, CI=0.0.01-0.5, P=0.008).<br/> <b>Conclusions</b>: Early instar malaria vectors were abundant in habitats densely vegetated with graminoid plants in the study area but specific preference for any of the graminoids could not be detected. In 2018, 213 million people were infected and 380,000 died<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>. The majority of people in sub-Saharan Africa live in poverty and in areas with suitable conditions for the proliferation of malaria vectors<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. The major malaria vectors are in the <i>Anopheles gambiae</i> and <i>An. funestus</i> species complexes, but a number of less efficient, so-called secondary vectors also contribute to malaria transmission<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a>,<a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20270e287>With growing physiological and behavioural resistance of malaria vectors to insecticides<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>, research efforts are geared towards additional, non-insecticidal vector control strategies<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a>,<a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>. Manipulations of the odour-orientation of adult vectors could lead to novel attract-and-kill interventions<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>. The gravid female searching for a suitable oviposition site is a desirable target for control. This strategy is specifically important as a single gravid mosquito may lay between 50 to 150 eggs<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>, hence killing a single gravid mosquito affects the growth of the population. Understanding the cues for habitat selection is of paramount importance for the development of such a tool. Recent work has highlighted the potential involvement of graminoid plants in luring vectors to oviposition sites<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. It has, for example, been shown that <i>Anopheles</i> mosquitoes respond to volatile chemical compounds that emanate from rice plants<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20270e327>Malaria vector mosquitoes lay their eggs in standing water and grass-like (graminoid) plants that often dominate wetlands associated with high <i>Anopheles</i> larval densities<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. Some graminoid plants, similar to lowland rice (<i>Oryza sativa</i>), are well adapted to wetlands. Most species in the sedge family, also known as swamp grasses, (Cyperaceae) are wetland indicators. One sedge species, <i>Cyperus rotundus</i>, was recently associated with the discovery of the oviposition attractant cedrol<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup> but its connection to the sesquiterpene compound was not clearly understood.</p><p class="" id=d20270e350>We considered it plausible to hypothesize that there might be an association between chemical cues released by water-indicating plants that are used by gravid malaria vectors in search of suitable oviposition sites. Therefore, we implemented this study driven by the hypothesis that sedges (Cyperaceae) are associated with the presence and abundance of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae, as a proxy indicator for oviposition, in western Kenya.</p><p class="" id=d20270e357>Swamp habitats are very common along lakeshores and serve as permanent or semi-permanent breeding sites for malaria vectors<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a></sup>. Studies support that the abundance of <i>Anopheles</i> larvae are associated with habitats surrounded by grass-like plants<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup>. In the current study, we investigated: (1) the distribution of graminoid plants associated with aquatic habitats along the shores of Lake Victoria in western Kenya, (2) the association of the graminoid plants with the occurrence and abundance of <i>Anopheles</i> larvae, and (3) the association of abiotic and biotic factors in aquatic habitats with the occurrence and abundance of <i>Anopheles</i> larvae.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d20270e380>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d20270e383 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Study area</h3><p class="" id=d20270e388>This study was conducted on Rusinga Island (021 and 026 south, 3413 and 3407 east) along the shore of Lake Victoria in Homa Bay County, western Kenya<sup><a href="#ref-21">21</a></sup> (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>). The area is endemic for malaria and the estimated prevalence of malaria in the population of Rusinga Island is around 10%<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-22">22</a>,<a href="#ref-23">23</a></sup>. Rusinga Island is only around 100 metres away from the mainland and connected via a bridge. The island has an area of 44 km<sup>2</sup> with altitude ranging from 1100 m to 1300 m above sea level and a population size of about 25,000<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>. The daily average temperatures of Rusinga Island range from 16C to 34C and peak in dry seasons<sup><a href="#ref-24">24</a></sup>. The area experiences bimodal rainy seasons with long rains from March to June and short rains from November to December. Malaria transmission peaks following the end of the long rainy season in June/July<sup><a href="#ref-25">25</a></sup>. The field survey was implemented between May and June 2018, towards the end of the long rainy season.</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/28336/31a929a2-5689-41fc-8989-d4fa920b0a82_figure1.gif"><img alt="31a929a2-5689-41fc-8989-d4fa920b0a82_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/28336/31a929a2-5689-41fc-8989-d4fa920b0a82_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. Map showing (A) Lake Victoria region, East Africa (B) the study clusters (rectangles in red) along the shores of Lake Victoria in Rusinga Island (Source: Google Earth).</h3></div></div><p class="" id=d20270e438>Habitat surveys were done along stretches of 700 m long and 300 m wide (clusters of approx. 0.2 km<sup>2</sup>). A total of 13 sampling clusters were selected around the lake shores of Rusinga Island (<a href="#f1">Figure 1B</a>). The areas were selected with the help of Google Earth, aiming at a homogeneous distribution around the island. Inaccessible areas with steep rocks at the shoreline were excluded. Within each sampling cluster all aquatic habitats locations were recorded using a smartphone, a unique identifier allocated and sampled as outlined below.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d20270e448 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Aquatic habitat surveys</h3><p class="" id=d20270e453>The aquatic habitat types were categorized as either swamp, puddle, fishpond, drainage/trench or artificial pit. The perimeter of every habitat was estimated by walking in large steps around the habitat. Water turbidity was measured using a turbidity meter (TRB 355IR, WTW Germany) and water pH and temperature were measured using a portable multi-parameter probe (Multi, WTW Germany).</p><p class="" id=d20270e456>Every aquatic habitat was inspected for the presence of larvae using the sweep-net method as described by Ndenga and others<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a></sup>. The sweep-net (40 cm  15 cm  30 cm) was made from fine cotton cloth with a 150 cm long handle. It was chosen for sampling due to its better efficiency in sampling the diverse aquatic fauna including freshly hatched mosquito larvae and mosquito pupae than the standard dipper<sup><a href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>. A dipper was used for sampling when the habitat was too small to be sampled by a sweep-net. Sampling of mosquito larvae using either sampling tools was randomly done at different points of the habitats since different species of malaria vectors prefer different conditions and vegetations. The duration of sweeping was dependent on the perimeter of the habitat. About 10 minutes were taken to sweep habitats with perimeters exceeding 10 metres, while 5 minutes were taken to sweep habitats &lt;10 m in perimeter. All sweeps were emptied into white trays and mosquito immature stages were counted separately for the two encountered genera, <i>Anopheles</i> and <i>Culex</i>. <i>Culex</i> and <i>Anopheles</i> larvae were identified morphologically. <i>Culex</i> larvae possess siphon on the posterior part of their abdomen for breathing through at the interface of the water surface during resting whereas <i>Anopheles</i> larvae have no siphon and rest horizontal to the water body<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. The larvae were grouped as early (1<sup>st</sup> and 2<sup>nd</sup> instar) and late (3<sup>rd</sup> and 4<sup>th</sup> instar) instars based on their body size. In addition, macroinvertebrates sampled from a habitat were grouped as Odonata (dragonfly and damselfly larvae), Coleoptera (water beetle larvae and adults), Heteroptera (Notonectidae, Naucoridae and Nepidae), fish and tadpoles. All late instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae and mosquito pupae were transferred to water bottles (1 L) containing habitat water and taken to the International Centre of Insect Physiology and Ecology-Thomas Odhiambo Campus (<i>icipe</i>-TOC) for rearing to adults. Rearing of the field collected larvae was done in 1 L plastic rectangle food mate (H67  W126  L184 mm, Kenpoly manufacturer, Nairobi, Kenya). Larvae were fed with a pinch of ground dry cat food (Nestl Purina PetCare Company, Nairobi, Kenya) once daily. The emerged adults were killed in a -20C refrigerator, sorted by genera and all <i>Anopheles</i> adults stored in Eppendorf tubes (1.5 ml) at -71C until they were identified morphologically using printed keys<sup><a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup> and molecularly using polymerase chain reaction (PCR) followed by gel-electrophoresis<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a></sup>. Randomly selected mosquito samples were used for molecular identification. Polymerase chain reaction was implemented for the amplification of the ribosomal internal transcribed spacer (ITS2) gene using primers<sup><a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. Positive controls of <i>An. gambiae s.s.</i> and <i>An. arabiensis</i> (from Mbita insectary colony) were analyzed with the samples from field. Extraction of genomic DNA was done for each mosquito separately using Tissue Kit (Quagen, GmbH Hilden, Germany). The PCR in a 10 l (per sample) was prepared by mixing PCR mix of 2 l of 5XHot Firepol Blended Master Mix (Ready to Load), primers (0.5 M each), DNA template (2 l) and nuclease-free water (5 l). The thermal recycling conditions involved initial denaturation for 5 min at 95C, after which 30 cycles of denaturation followed for 30 s at 94C, annealing for 30 s at 50C, extension for 30 s at 72C and final extension for 5 min at 72C. We used Kyratec Thermal Cycler (SC300T-R2, Australia) for the thermal reactions. Agarose gel-electrophoresis (2.0%) stained with 2 l ethidium bromide against a 100 bp DNA ladder (Bioline, A Maridian Life Science<sup>@</sup> Company, UK) and a positive control was conducted to identify the species.</p><p class="" id=d20270e537>Vegetation coverage, vegetation types and the dominant vegetation type were recorded separately for habitat edge and water surface. Habitat edge was defined as the area along the waterline, approximately 10 cm inside and/or outside the water. Vegetation coverage was estimated visually as the proportion of the habitats covered with vegetations and categorized as (1) 125% (2) 2550% (3) 5075% (4) 75100%. Graminoid plants across the edge and inside water were recorded as Poaceae, Cyperaceae, Juncaceae, Typhaceae. The graminoid plants were identified to family using morphology of their leaves (two or three-ranked; open or closed sheaths), and their stem type (three-sided or round; hollow or solid) using Revuelta<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup>. Furthermore, herbaceous (not woody and non-graminoid plants) were collectively recorded as forbs. The presence of water plants and algae in the aquatic habitats was also recorded. The percent coverage of water plants and algae on the water surface was visually determined as above. For each habitat, the dominant type of vegetation was identified and recorded. Full specimens of all dominant graminoid plants found in the aquatic habitats were collected and planted at <i>icipe-</i>TOC for further identification<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d20270e555 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Data analysis</h3><p class="" id=d20270e560>Generalised estimating equations (GEE) with Poisson distribution fitted to a log function and exchangeable correlation matrix were used to test for associations between biological and environmental factors and the abundance of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae. The cluster ID in which habitats were located was included in the model as repeated measurement. A GEE model was also used to analyse associations between factors and the presence of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae. Here we included the presence of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae as dependent variable in the model with binomial distribution fitted to a logit function and exchangeable correlation matrix to analyse its association with biotic and abiotic factors of the habitats (independent variables). The presence and abundance of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae (rather than eggs which are difficult to identify from field samples) were used as dependent variable as a proxy for oviposition. This is based on recent work confirming that early instar density correlates with the abundance of females selecting a habitat for oviposition<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. The statistical outputs were reported as incidence rate ratios (RR) for the abundance of the first instar larvae and odds ratios (OR) for the presence with their 95% confidence intervals (95% CI). R statistical software version 3.5.1<sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup> was used for the analyses.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d20270e585 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Ethics statement</h3><p class="" id=d20270e590>This field survey was largely descriptive and observational and had no human study participants. Habitat surveys on privately owned lands were made after seeking consent from the landowners and were implemented in their presence.</p></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d20270e597>Results</h2><div class=section><a name=d20270e600 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Aquatic habitat types</h3><p class="" id=d20270e605>A total of 110 aquatic habitats were identified during the survey<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>. As expected, given the targeted areas within 300 metres of the lake shore, the most prevalent aquatic habitat types were swamps (65.5%, n=72) defined as permanent or semi-permanent water-logged sections of land with tall graminoid vegetation and/or floating plants (<a href="#f2">Figure 2A</a>). The water sources of these were largely groundwater supplemented by rainwater. Other habitats (see <a href="#f2">Figure 2B</a>, <a href="#f3">Figure 3C, 3D and 3E</a>) included ponds formerly used for breeding fish but abandoned at the survey time (11%, n=12), rainfed puddles (9%, n=10), drainages (9%, n=10) and artificial pits (5.5%, n=6). Given that all non-swamp habitats were few in number, they were pooled for statistical analysis and the swamp habitats used as the reference group (<a href="#f3">Figure 3</a>). Early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae were found frequently during the survey in the habitat types: artificial holes (n=6, 100%), drainages (n=9, 90%), ponds (n=5, 42%), puddles (n=7, 70%) and swamps (n=61, 85%). The majority of these habitat types were characterized by possessing graminoid plants: graminoids dominated the vegetation in 100% of the swamps, in 83% of the ponds, in 80% of the puddles, in 70% of the drainages, and in 50% of the artificial pits.</p><a name=f2 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/28336/31a929a2-5689-41fc-8989-d4fa920b0a82_figure2.gif"><img alt="31a929a2-5689-41fc-8989-d4fa920b0a82_figure2.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/28336/31a929a2-5689-41fc-8989-d4fa920b0a82_figure2.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 2. (OR=22, 95%CI=6-86, P&lt;0.001), presence of late instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae (OR=359, CI=33-3941, P&lt;0.001), and presence of <i>Culex</i> larvae (OR=17, 95%CI=3-107, P=0.002) (<a In habitats containing pupae the odds of finding early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae was lower (OR=0.08, CI=0.01-0.42, P=0.003) than habitats without pupae. Notably, the majority of habitats with <i>Anopheles</i> larvae were also well colonised by other invertebrates, many of which are considered predators of mosquitoes, such as Odonatan, Notonecta, and Coleoptera larvae. However, the presence of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> was only significantly and negatively associated with presence of tadpoles (OR=0.09, 0.01-0.53, P=0.003). Correspondingly, the abundance of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae significantly decreased with the presence of tadpoles (RR=0.5, CI=0.2-0.9, P=0.03). Similarly, larval abundance was negatively associated with presence of Odonata (RR=0.5, CI=0.3-0.9, P=0.019) and presence of Coleoptera (RR=0.4, CI=0.2-0.8, P=0.004). id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d20270e2501>Discussion</h2><p class="" id=d20270e2504>The work presented here was done with the aim of identifying graminoid plants for further behavioural and chemical ecology studies due to their association with habitats used by gravid malaria vectors for egg-laying. However, the presence of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae in the majority of the surveyed habitats and the presence and high coverage of various graminoid plants did not allow us to analyse any statistically significant association. All the habitats surveyed provided excellent oviposition sites and favourable conditions for the development of immature stages based on the high and consistent number of early instar larvae as a proxy for oviposition and the associated high abundance of late instar larvae as an indicator for survival. The study, as implemented, did not allow us to infer specific plant-based factors with oviposition. Generally, the association between graminoid plants and <i>Anopheles</i> breeding sites as well as the presence and increased densities of <i>Anopheles</i> larvae in both temporary and permanent aquatic habitats have been shown before<sup><a href="#ref-16">16</a>,<a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>. It has been suggested that vegetation can protect mosquito immature stages from being washed off by running water<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a></sup> and from predation<sup><a href="#ref-39">39</a>,<a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup>. Our study has several limitations that might be responsible for the negative results. The timing of the survey towards the end of the rainy season meant that all potential habitats were flooded and vegetation thrived. Habitats for oviposition were not a limiting factor and likely easy to identify without major cues for orientation. This might have been different if the survey had been implemented during the dry season. Furthermore, this survey was limited to locations close to the lake shores, biasing the study towards swampy habitats. Lastly, due to high water levels during the peak rainy season, a number of habitats with swamp graminoids of the families Cyperaceae, Typhaceae, and Juncaceae were impossible to access, hence could not be sampled. This might also explain why only very few secondary malaria vector species and no <i>Anopheles funestus</i> were sampled, even though <i>An. funestus</i> is the major vector that was encountered in houses in the study area<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a><a href="#ref-43">43</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20270e2548> Not many strong associations were found with early <i>Anopheles</i> larvae presence or abundance and other observed factors that would allow conclusions on oviposition preferences. However, the odds of finding early instars increased when late instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae were present as opposed to when they were absent, potentially indicating that <i>Anopheles arabiensis</i> females oviposit in habitats containing late instar conspecific larvae as an indicator of suitable development conditions. This contrasts with experimental studies on <i>An. coluzzii</i><sup><a href="#ref-44">44</a>,<a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup>, where it has been suggested that late instar conspecific larvae repel gravid females potentially due to the risk of cannibalism<sup><a href="#ref-46">46</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20270e2573>The presence and abundance of early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae was negatively associated with the presence of tadpoles. It was previously shown that rainwater conditioned with tadpoles repelled gravid <i>An. gambiae</i> from oviposition in the laboratory<sup><a href="#ref-47">47</a></sup>. Mature tadpoles can prey on larvae<sup><a href="#ref-48">48</a></sup> and might compete for resources in aquatic habitats<sup><a href="#ref-49">49</a></sup>. Our field survey indicated that early instar larvae were cohabiting with predatory invertebrates in most habitats. Whilst <i>Anopheles</i> larvae might be reduced by these organisms, as suggested by the negative association between <i>Anopheles</i> density and the presence of Odonata and Coleoptera, gravid females nevertheless did not avoid these habitats for oviposition. This finding agrees with studies elsewhere that have shown strong associations between the presence of anopheline larvae and high invertebrate diversity<sup><a href="#ref-50">50</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20270e2605>Six species of <i>Anopheles</i> mosquitoes were identified from the samples which have all been reported in previous studies in western Kenya<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a href="#ref-51">51</a>,<a href="#ref-52">52</a></sup>. <i>Anopheles arabiensis</i> was the predominant malaria vector from both vegetated and non-vegetated aquatic habitats in the study area during the peak rainy season. <i>Anopheles arabiensis</i> has historically been the predominant vector species on Rusinga Island<sup><a href="#ref-53">53</a></sup>. In recent years however, <i>An. funestus</i> predominates indoor vector collections<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a>,<a href="#ref-43">43</a>,<a href="#ref-54">54</a></sup>, but larvae were not found during our survey. Breeding sites preferred by this mosquito species were inaccessible by the field team due to the large volumes of water in the lake after the long rains; <i>An. funestus</i> prefer breeding habitats that are covered by tall vegetations<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a>,<a href="#ref-55">55</a>,<a href="#ref-56">56</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d20270e2661>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d20270e2664>Our results did not support the hypothesis and nullified the aim of the research to identify graminoid plant species positively associated with malaria vector oviposition. Our results did not support our initial hypothesis and did not allow us to identify any association between <i>Anopheles</i> larvae and specific graminoid plants. However, <i>Panicum repens</i>, <i>Cynodon dactylon,</i> and <i>Cyperus rotundus</i> were the predominant graminoid plants found in the aquatic habitats. The habitats covered by this vegetation were abundantly colonized by early instar <i>Anopheles</i> larvae even though no specific preference for any of these could be detected, likely due to study limitations. We recommend further studies on the identification of oviposition cues from graminoid plants during the dry seasons when habitats are limited and water-levels low enough to provide access to most of them. Furthermore, it might be warranted to implement bioassays in the laboratory with the here identified grass-like plants, which will allow more standardised comparisons and sufficient replication.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d20270e2686>Data availability</h2><div class=section><a name=d20270e2689 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Underlying data</h3><p class="" id=d20270e2694>Harvard Dataverse: Association between graminoids and the prevalence of immature malaria. <a target=xrefwindow href="https://doi.org/10.7910/DVN/NAT0YY" id=d20270e2696>https://doi.org/10.7910/DVN/NAT0YY</a><sup><a href="#ref-57">57</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d20270e2703>This project contains the following underlying data:</p><div class=list><a name=d20270e2706 class=n-a></a><ul style="list-style-type: none"><li><p id=d20270e2713><span class=label>-</span> Bokore <i>et al.</i> 2020_F1000Research_All_Collected_Data.csv</p></li><li><p id=d20270e2725><span class=label>-</span> Bokore <i>et al.</i> 2020_F1000Research_Data_used_for_final_analysis.csv</p></li><li><p id=d20270e2737><span class=label>-</span> Bokore <i>et al.</i> 2020_F1000Research_Variable_Codes.csv</p></li></ul></div><p class="" id=d20270e2745>Data are available under the terms of the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://creativecommons.org/publicdomain/zero/1.0/" id=d20270e2747>Creative Commons Zero "No rights reserved" data waiver</a> (CC0 1.0 Public domain dedication).</p></div></div><div id=article1-back class=generated-article-footer><div class=back-section><a name=d20270e2756 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d20270e2758>Acknowledgements</h2><p class="" id=d20270e2761>We are grateful to the residents of Rusinga Island for allowing us to collect the field data in their farms.
