We proposed that this ensures that tissues and cell populations attain the appropriate size that allows fusion in the body midline and retain the size of cavities once formed. The morphology of these dying cells was found to be distinctly different than that observed in necrotic cells (for example, cells subjected to injury). Isolated cells or at most small clusters of cells undergo cell death during normal development. Cells undergoing cell death during embryonic development shrink rather than rupture as seen in necrosis (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>); cell nuclei condense in what is termed pyknosis (from the Greek <i>pykno</i>, meaning thick, compact, or dense) and ultimately fragment. The components of such dying cells are packaged into cytoplasmic membraneenclosed vesicles awaiting phagocytosis by other cells. In contrast, during necrosis, which can occur during injury and inflammation, larger areas of tissue are usually affected, cells swell, the plasma membrane and nuclear envelope rupture, and cell contents spill out.</p><a name=f1 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23770/e207170f-cf16-44ee-8b3e-c5fbdba45ba8_figure1.gif"><img alt="e207170f-cf16-44ee-8b3e-c5fbdba45ba8_figure1.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23770/e207170f-cf16-44ee-8b3e-c5fbdba45ba8_figure1.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 1. The morphological distinction of apoptosis and necrosis.</h3><p id=d26313e190>Schematic drawing and major characteristics. EM, electron microscopy.</p></div></div><p class="" id=d26313e197>The predictable time course and location of the dying cells during development suggested that it was a regulated process and the term programmed cell death was used to indicate this concept. Based on the time course and location, the essential roles of programmed cell death during developmentincluding requirement for tissue invagination and closure, union of body halves, lumen formation, bifurcation, regression of rudimentary organs, and cell differentiationwere inferred<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>. Later, the electron microscopic characteristics of the dying cells were described and the term apoptosis (from the Greek <i>apo</i> + <i>ptosis</i>, meaning from + falling) was coined (reviewed in <a href="#ref-2">2</a>). The cell membranebound fragments of apoptotic cells were termed apoptotic bodies. Apoptosis is thought to drive morphogenesis by regulating cell number, tissue sculpturing, and deleting structures, including the conversion of solid structures into tubes and vesicles<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26313e220>Regulation of apoptosis</h2><p class="" id=d26313e223>In the following decades, a large number of researchers worked to decipher the molecular mechanisms that initiate and execute apoptosis during development; they also determined the role of apoptosis in disease conditions (reviewed in <a href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a href="#ref-5">5</a>). Two distinct but ultimately converging pathways initiate apoptosis<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>: the mitochondrial, intrinsic, or B-cell lymphoma 2 (BCL-2)regulated pathway and the extrinsic or death receptor pathway (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>).</p><a name=f2 class=n-a></a><div class="fig panel clearfix"><a target=_blank href="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23770/e207170f-cf16-44ee-8b3e-c5fbdba45ba8_figure2.gif"><img alt="e207170f-cf16-44ee-8b3e-c5fbdba45ba8_figure2.gif" src="https://f1000researchdata.s3.amazonaws.com/manuscripts/23770/e207170f-cf16-44ee-8b3e-c5fbdba45ba8_figure2.gif"></a><div class=caption><h3>Figure 2. Simplified schematic drawing of the mitochondrial and death receptor apoptotic pathways.</h3><p id=d26313e249>MOMP, mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization.</p></div></div><p class="" id=d26313e256>The mitochondrial pathway is initiated during development by limiting levels of growth factors, metabolic stress, lack of neurotrophic support, lack of blood flow, or loss of substrate adhesion (<i>anoikis</i>, from the Greek <i>an</i> + <i>oikos</i>, meaning without + home) and in disease or experimental settings by DNA damage and diverse cytotoxic agents. The response to these stimuli is regulated by BCL-2 protein family members<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. In response to death-inducing stimuli, pro-apoptotic members of the family (BIM, PUMA, BID, BMF, NOXA, BIK, BAD, and HRK; collectively termed BH3 [BCL-2 homology domain 3]-only proteins) inhibit the anti-apoptotic BCL-2 family members (BCL-2, MCL-1, BCL-XL, BCL-W, and A1/BFL-1), which under steady-state conditions keep the pro-apoptotic effectors, the multi-BH domain proteins BAX and BAK, in an inactive state. In addition, some BH3-only proteins (for example, BIM and PUMA) have been reported to activate BAX and BAK directly<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. Once activated, BAX and BAK form pores within the mitochondrial outer membrane, which leads to mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP) and release of cytochrome C and other apoptogenic factors from the mitochondria into the cytoplasm (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>).</p><p class="" id=d26313e283>Cytochrome C associates with APAF-1 and caspase-9 to form the apoptosome and this stimulates caspase-9 activation, which in turn cleaves and thereby activates effector caspase-3, -6, and -7. These effector caspases cleave hundreds of proteins and in certain cases proteolytically activate or inhibit other enzymes. This includes the activation of endonucleases that drive DNA fragmentation and the inhibition of flippases, which causes exposure of eat me signals on the plasma membrane. These processes lead to chromatin condensation and packaging of the cell content into apoptotic bodies and removal by phagocytosis.</p><p class="" id=d26313e287>In the death receptor pathway, apoptosis is initiated by the binding of certain members of the tumor necrosis factor (TNF) family to their cognate receptors that belong to the TNF receptor (TNFR) family (in particular, binding of FAS ligand [FASL] to its receptor FAS). This can set in train two different series of events: if available, pro-caspase-8, which directly activates effector caspase-3, -6 and -7, is activated. The pro-apoptotic BH3-only protein BID, after proteolytic conversion to tBID, can also activate the BAX/BAK-dependent mitochondrial apoptotic pathway to thereby amplify the apoptotic cascade (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>). If caspase-8 is absent or inhibited (for example, by viral inhibitors of this protease, such as vFLIP), another form of regulated cell death, termed necroptosis<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>, can be initiated via receptor-interacting serine/threonine-protein kinase 1 (RIPK1), RIPK3, and mixed lineage kinase domain-like pseudokinase (MLKL) (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>). These alternative fates after the activation of FAS or certain other surface receptors (for example, TNFR1) result in either apoptotic or necroptotic cell death with discernible histological morphologies (<a href="#f1">Figure 1</a>). The form of cell death reported to occur during embryonic development resembles apoptotic cell death. Based on histological studies, necroptotic cell death, if it occurred during development, would not be common.</p><p class="" id=d26313e303>In the following section, we summarize originally proposed roles of apoptotic cell death during development and more recent detailed functional studies that have identified the much more restricted gambit of processes that critically depend on developmental apoptosis. In the conclusion, we propose why developmental apoptosis occurs in many tissues, seemingly without being essential.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26313e309>Apoptosis during embryonic development</h2><p class="" id=d26313e312>Histological analyses of developing embryos discovered that pyknotic (dying/dead) cells were present in certain locations at given stages of development. require apoptosis to proceed normally (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>), knockout mouse studies in recent years have singled out those developmental processes that do and those that do not require apoptosis (<a href="#T2">Table 2</a>). In particular, it has become clear that reducing apoptosis typically causes webbed digits, vaginal septa, and often lymphadenopathy; it commonly causes exencephaly and cleft face or palate and occasionally omphalocele. Therefore, it can be said that the removal of the interdigital webbing, vaginal septa, blood cell homeostasis, neural tube, palate, and body wall closure depends critically on developmental apoptosis. However, whether development leading up to these events or the processes themselves require apoptosis was not discriminated by these studies. Indeed, the sequence of events can be complex. For example, developing neurons can undergo apoptosis before or after target innervation<sup><a href="#ref-27">27</a>,<a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>. However, it appears that the lack or restriction of neurotrophic support may be the cause of neuronal apoptosis on both occasions<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a>,<a href="#ref-29">29</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26313e1551>Technical difficulties in enumerating apoptotic cells during development</h2><p class="" id=d26313e1554>As a corollary of identifying developmental processes that require apoptosis, those processes that occur normally without apoptosis might also have been defined. However, to make such a claim with certainty, the possibility of residual apoptosis occurring would have to be excluded. The difficulties in assessing absolute numbers of apoptotic cells during development can be illustrated by using the example of embryos lacking the tumor suppressor p53, which in response to a number of stress stimuli can induce either cell cycle arrest or apoptosis<sup><a href="#ref-30">30</a>,<a href="#ref-31">31</a></sup>. Male mice lacking a functional <i>Trp53</i> gene are born in the expected numbers, but female <i>Trp53<sup>/</sup></i> mice are under-represented because of a partially penetrant failure of neural tube closure<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a>,<a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>. Originally, electron microscopy<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a></sup> and terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase dUTP nick end labeling of DNA fragments (TUNEL) (a technique used to identify cells undergoing apoptosis) on sections<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup> were employed to determine whether loss of p53 affected developmental apoptosis, but neither method detected a difference between wild-type and <i>Trp53<sup>/</sup></i> mouse embryos. It was hypothesized that the methods employed may not have been sensitive enough to detect differences<sup><a href="#ref-33">33</a></sup>. More recently, developmental apoptosis in <i>Trp53<sup>/</sup></i> embryos was assessed by TUNEL flow cytometry on single cell suspensions and this method was sufficiently sensitive to detect a difference between <i>Trp53<sup>/</sup></i> and wild-type<i></i> embryos<sup><a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>. Flow cytometry proved to be a sensitive and precise method that also easily detected differences between wild-type and <i>Bax<sup>/</sup>;Bak<sup>/</sup></i> double knockout (DKO) embryos (total absence of apoptosis)<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup> or <i>Mcl1<sup>+/</sup>;Bclx<sup>+/</sup></i> double heterozygous mice (abnormally increased apoptosis)<sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup>. However, the low percentage of apoptotic cells in developing wild-type embryos (only about 1.5%<sup><a href="#ref-34">34</a><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup>) does not provide a large dynamic range for detecting a gradual reduction in apoptosis. Indeed, although the more severe developmental abnormalities in mice with combined loss of pro-apoptotic BAX, BAK, and BOK compared with loss of only BAX and BAK suggested that the <i>Bax<sup>/</sup>;Bak<sup>/</sup>;Bok<sup>/</sup></i> triple knockout mice (TKO) should have a greater reduction in developmental apoptosis than <i>Bax<sup>/</sup>;Bak<sup>/</sup></i> DKO mice, flow cytometric analysis was unable to detect significant differences between these two genotypes<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. Therefore, a claim of zero apoptosis is hard to support with the current methods. A more sensitive approach may be the use of mice carrying the <i>CAG-sA5-YFP</i> transgene, which encodes a fluorescently labeled, secreted form of annexin V. The fluorescently labeled annexin V accumulates to detectable levels when binding to phosphatidylserine exposed on the surface of apoptotic cells<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup>. A caveat here may be that necroptotic cells also stain positive for annexin V<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a></sup>.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26313e1684>Developmental processes less likely to require apoptosis</h2><p class="" id=d26313e1687>It was surprising that apoptosis, given its stereotypic occurrence, was not essential for <i>C. elegans</i> development<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup> and from time to time it was speculated that apoptosis in mammalian development, too, might be less critical than its prevalence might suggest<sup><a href="#ref-39">39</a></sup>. Throughout the decades of intense research on the role of apoptosis in a number of developmental processes, information surfaced indicating that, despite previous inferences, apoptosis was not required for specific processes. An example is the role of apoptosis in the formation of the pro-amniotic cavity, which was viewed as a model of cavity formation/tube formation by apoptosis and supported by work in <i>in vivo</i> and culture models<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a href="#ref-40">40</a>,<a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup> but was later called into question by the development of embryos past this point in the absence of detectable apoptosis<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>. Similarly, apoptosis had been observed in the ridge of the closing neural folds and therefore it had been proposed that neural tube closure required apoptotic cell death at the point where the two neural folds met. This was supported by the observation that the application of caspase inhibitors at a critical time point blocked neural tube closure<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. On the surface, this conclusion appears to be amply supported by the many knockout mouse mutants of apoptotic regulators that display exencephaly or spina bifida or both (<i>Bax;Bak;Bok</i> TKO<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>, <i>Trp53</i> KO<sup><a href="#ref-32">32</a><a href="#ref-34">34</a></sup>, <i>Apaf1</i> KO<sup><a href="#ref-42">42</a>,<a href="#ref-43">43</a></sup>, <i>Casp9</i> KO<sup><a href="#ref-44">44</a>,<a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup>, and <i>Casp3;Casp7</i> DKO<sup><a href="#ref-46">46</a></sup>). However, one study presented evidence that apoptosis is not actually required for the specific time of neural tube closure and that lack of apoptosis did not affect tissue remodeling and separation of the neural tube from the surface ectoderm at the fusion site<sup><a href="#ref-47">47</a></sup>. Similarly, it is not clear whether apoptosis is required at the specific time of palate fusion<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a>,<a href="#ref-48">48</a><a href="#ref-51">51</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d26313e1783>The studies showing that neural tube closure and palate fusion can occur without developmental apoptosis raise the question of why so many mouse mutants of critical constituents of the mitochondrial apoptotic pathway display exencephaly, spina bifida, and cleft palate (<a href="#T2">Table 2</a>). A possible explanation is that it is not the process of tissue fusion that requires apoptosis but rather that apoptosis is important to arrive at body halves that are of a size compatible with the fusion process.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26313e1792>Death receptor pathwayinduced apoptosis and necroptosis</h2><p class="" id=d26313e1795>Activation of the death receptor pathway can lead to apoptosis via caspase-8 activation or, when caspase-8 is inhibited, to necroptosis via RIPK1/RIPK3/MLKL activation (<a href="#f2">Figure 2</a>). Death receptor pathwaymediated apoptosis is morphologically similar to mitochondrial apoptosis, but necroptosis is distinct, resembling necrosis. Disturbance of the normal regulation of death receptorinduced cell death pathways can result in developmental lethality. Mouse embryos lacking caspase-8 display prenatal lethality<sup><a href="#ref-78">78</a></sup> and mice lacking RIPK1 die shortly after birth<sup><a href="#ref-79">79</a></sup> whereas RIPK3-deficient mice develop normally<sup><a href="#ref-80">80</a></sup>. However, if both death receptoractivated processes (apoptosis and necroptosis) are inactivated by simultaneously deleting genes necessary for each process, the resulting mice are viable<i></i> (for example, <i>Casp8</i>;<i>Ripk3</i> DKO<sup><a href="#ref-81">81</a>,<a href="#ref-82">82</a></sup>; <i>Casp8</i>;<i>Mlkl</i> or <i>Casp8</i>;<i>Fadd</i> DKO<sup><a href="#ref-83">83</a></sup>; and <i>Ripk1;Ripk3</i>;<i>Casp8</i> or <i>Ripk1;Ripk3</i>;<i>FADD</i> TKO<sup><a href="#ref-84">84</a></sup>). The studies using these compound mutant mice provided evidence that death receptormediated apoptosis is not required for normal mouse development; rather, a balance between caspase-8 and RIPK1/RIPK3 activity needs to be maintained to prevent MLKL activation and consequent necroptosis. The observation that mice lacking MLKL<sup><a href="#ref-85">85</a></sup>, the essential mediator of necroptosis, develop normally reveals that this programmed cell death process is not required for embryogenesis. In agreement with these findings, early histological studies did not detect substantial involvement of necrotic processes in mammalian development.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26313e1870>Other forms of cell death and cell removal mechanisms</h2><p class="" id=d26313e1873>The most abundant form of developmental cell death appears to be apoptosis, and more specifically mitochondrial apoptosis, as the death receptor pathway does not appear to be essential for normal development (discussed above). Furthermore, reduction of mitochondrial apoptosis to below current detection levels by simultaneous deletion of <i>Bax</i>, <i>Bak</i>, and <i>Bok</i> did not appear to cause a compensatory increase in necroptosis, pyroptosis, or autophagy<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>, suggesting that these other forms of cell death may not be poised to compensate for a deficiency of the mitochondrial apoptotic pathway.</p><p class="" id=d26313e1889>A number of alternative cell removal processes have been proposed to play a role during development, including entosis<sup><a href="#ref-86">86</a></sup>, autophagy<sup><a href="#ref-87">87</a>,<a href="#ref-88">88</a></sup>, cell extrusion from epithelia<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a>,<a href="#ref-89">89</a>,<a href="#ref-90">90</a></sup>, and cell senescence<sup><a href="#ref-91">91</a>,<a href="#ref-92">92</a></sup>. However, their prominence and roles during mammalian development remain to be determined. Autophagy is morphologically distinct from apoptosis<sup><a href="#ref-93">93</a></sup> and so could be assessed and its frequency could be compared to apoptosis. An overlap between apoptosis and entosis exists in so far as apoptotic cells are eventually engulfed by other cells. Whether mammalian cells are extruded from epithelia (for example, in the intestine) before or after they have initiated cell death appears contentious<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a>,<a href="#ref-94">94</a>,<a href="#ref-95">95</a></sup>, and the ultimate fate of senescent cells seems to be cell death, possibly mostly through apoptosis<sup><a href="#ref-92">92</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d26313e1939>Overall, the mitochondrial apoptotic pathway appears to be the major form of developmental cell death. However, it should be noted that low prevalence of a given process does not necessarily imply little importance. Accordingly, it is possible that in specific cases these alternative forms of cell death play an essential role during mammalian development, which remains to be functionally tested. This will probably require the generation and analysis of mice deficient in multiple cell death pathways and possibly even mice lacking several cell death pathways plus other processes (for example, cell senescence) to uncover redundancy.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26313e1945>Open questions</h2><p class="" id=d26313e1948>The following open questions exist in the field of developmental apoptosis.</p><div class=list><a name=d26313e1951 class=n-a></a><ul style="list-style-type: none"><li><p id=d26313e1958><span class=label>(1) </span> The field might benefit from the results of a comprehensive, sensitive, and quantitative analysis of apoptosis throughout the sequence of mammalian development that either does not rely on tissue sections or uses sophisticated methods of three-dimensional reconstruction and numerical assessment.</p></li><li><p id=d26313e1967><span class=label>(2) </span> Once a sensitive technical approach to (1) has been developed, the question of whether developmental apoptosis is dynamically regulated during development in response to disruptions (for example, of proliferation or cell survival) could be answered.</p></li><li><p id=d26313e1976><span class=label>(3) </span> If similarly sensitive and quantitative detection methods were developed for other forms of cell death, their relative importance compared with apoptosis could be assessed.</p></li><li><p id=d26313e1985><span class=label>(4) </span> The role of a number of BCL-2 family members in development remains to be determined, while single knockout of the genes encoding these does not result in overt phenotypic anomalies, which is possibly due to functional redundancies.</p></li><li><p id=d26313e1994><span class=label>(5) </span> In addition, based on the theory of evolution, one would expect that even these BCL-2 family members have essential functions (that provide a selective advantage), which remain to be discovered.</p></li></ul></div></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d26313e2002>Conclusions</h2><p class="" id=d26313e2005>The available data identify developmental processes that are clearly dependent on functional apoptosis, whereas other processes previously thought to depend on programmed cell death do not appear to be as reliant on apoptosis.</p><p class="" id=d26313e2008>Processes apparently independent of apoptosis based on normal embryonic and fetal development in the combined absence of BAX, BAK, and BOK include, for example, the formation of the pro-amniotic cavity, epiblast, intestine, and lens development as well as enchondral ossification. (For more details, see Table S2 in Ke <i>et al</i>., 2018<sup><a href="#ref-35">35</a></sup>.) However, the caveat that residual apoptosis not detectable by current methods (discussed in the preceding sections) needs to be kept in mind.</p><p class="" id=d26313e2018>It is certainly curious that a complex and regulated process, such as apoptosis, occurs in an evolutionary conserved manner during development, including in places where it appears to be largely or completely dispensable. One possibility is that apoptosis occurs as a by-product of other processes; for example, in the process of tissue remodeling, cells might lose their contact to the extracellular matrix and, without this survival signal, might die by apoptosis (anoikis).</p><p class="" id=d26313e2021>Another possible explanation is that a twofold control of developing tissue size and cell population homeostasis by maintaining a balance between proliferation and cell death affords not just one but two cellular mechanisms of fine-tuning cell number. This would provide a more robust regulation, less prone to errors and variation in response to negative impact.</p><p class="" id=d26313e2025>Indeed, mammalian and other embryos appear to have an astounding capacity to compensate for the loss of cells, certainly by upregulating cell proliferation<sup><a href="#ref-96">96</a>,<a href="#ref-97">97</a></sup> but perhaps also by actively downregulating developmental apoptosis. It can be assumed, on the basis of its evolutionary conservation, that apoptotic cell death, even in regions where it is not absolutely required, provides a competitive advantage.</p></div><div
