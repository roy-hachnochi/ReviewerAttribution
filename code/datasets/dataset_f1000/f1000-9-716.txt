class="abstract-text is-expanded"> <b>Background: </b><i>Rosa damascena</i> L. is the most notable species of the Rosaceae family in the world, and has been used in food, cosmetics, and the pharmaceutical industry. Bioactive compounds in this flower are known to have several activities, such as antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory. In this study, the antioxidant and collagenase inhibitory activities of <i>R. damascena</i> L. petal and receptacle extracts were evaluated.&nbsp;<br/> <b>Methods: </b>Ethanolic extraction of <i>R. damascena</i> L. petals (EERP) and <i>R. damascena</i> L. receptacles (EERR) were obtained, and bioactive compounds (flavonoids, phenolics, alkaloids, steroids, tannins, terpenoids, and triterpenoids) were classified by phytochemical screening. Antioxidant activities were analyzed by Ferric Reducing Antioxidant Power (FRAP) assay, while anti-collagenase analysis was examined through the inhibition of collagenase.<br/> <b>Results: </b>Phytochemical test revealed the presence of flavonoids, phenolics, alkaloids, steroids, triterpenoids, triterpenes, and tannin. EERP showed higher FRAP activity (164.23 &plusmn; 1.34 M Fe(II)) than EERR (12.85 &plusmn; 6.19 M Fe(II)). EERP also had higher inhibitory activity of collagenase (IC<sub>50</sub> = 115.48&plusmn;1.78 &micro;M/mL) compared to EERR (IC<sub>50 </sub>= 141.96&plusmn;6.13 &micro;M/mL).<br/> <b>Conclusions: </b><i>R. damascena </i>L. petal and receptacle ethanol extracts contain several components, such as phenolics, flavonoids, alkaloids, tannins, terpenes, triterpenoids, and steroids. These extracts exhibit antioxidant activity and collagenase inhibition. <i>R. damascena </i>L. petal extract showed higher antioxidant activity through FRAP assay and inhibitory activity of collagenase than <i>R. damascena </i>L. receptacle extract. </div> <div class=abstract-for-mobile> <div class="margin-top-30 padding-bottom-30 research-layout is-centered"> <button class="primary Several studies report that ROS correlates with several human diseases, such as cancer<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>, inflammation<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>, and the aging process<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. In the aging process, ROS play a role in protein unfolding, lipid breakdown, DNA damage, fragmentation, and collagen disintegration<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. In the body, ROS promote the activation of collagenase and block collagen synthesis, leading to a decrease in skin elasticity and acceleration of skin wrinkles. This mechanism by ROS is the major factor of degeneration collagen fiber<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d1820e257><i>Rosa damascena</i> L.<i></i> is the most important species among the Rosaceae family of flowers. This species has many pharmacological properties and has been used in food, herbal medicine, and cosmetics since ancient times<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. Previous research showed that <i>R. damascena</i> L.<i></i> has anti-viral, anti-microbial, anti-cancer, anti-depressant, anti-inflammatory, anti-convulsant, and anti-oxidant activities<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>. Several studies report that phenolic compounds and flavonoids contributed to an increase in antioxidant activity<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a>,<a href="#ref-9">9</a><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup>. In this study, we evaluate the antioxidant and anti-collagenase activity from ethanol extract of <i>R. damascena L.</i> petals and receptacles.<i></i></p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d1820e295>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d1820e298 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Sample extraction</h3><p class="" id=d1820e303><i>R. damascena</i> L. flowers were obtained from Bandung, Indonesia (-6.803662, 107.586633). In total, 1400 g and 700 g of petals and receptacles of <i>R. damascena</i> L.<i></i> were dried and mashed, respectively. The dried samples (250 g petals and 90g receptacles) were soaked with 8000 mL and 3000 mL of 70% ethanol, respectively, and macerated. This solvent was replaced every 3 days and the extracted solution from the first to the last (15<sup>th</sup> day) was mixed and concentrated using a rotary evaporator at 50C for 4 h. The extracts were labeled as ethanol extract of rose petals (EERP) and ethanol extract of rose receptacles (EERR).</p></div><div class=section><a name=d1820e317 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Phytochemical analysis</h3><p class="" id=d1820e322>Phytochemical analysis was done to detect the presence of flavonoids, phenolics, tannins, steroids/triterpenoids, terpenoids and alkaloids. 10 mg of extracts were used for each analysis following the procedures of Widowati <i>et al</i>. without modification<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d1820e333 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Ferric ion reducing antioxidant power (FRAP) activity</h3><p class="" id=d1820e338>The antioxidant activity of EERP and EERR was investigated using FRAP assay following Sricharoen <i>et al</i>. with slight modifications<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup>. Briefly, ferric chloride hexahydrate (Merck) and 2,4,6-tripyridyl-s-triazine (TPTZ) (Sigma) were mixed to form the FRAP reagent. 142.5 L FRAP solution was added into 7.5 L of sample extract and the mixtures were incubated at 37C for 5 min. The absorbance of the reaction mixture was read at 539 nm. A set of concentrations 0200 g/mL of FeSO4.7H2O (Sigma) was used for calibration of the standard curve. The results were expressed as M Fe(II)/g extract.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d1820e349 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Anti-collagenase assay</h3><p class="" id=d1820e354>Anti-collagenase activity of EERR and EERP was assessed by evaluating the inhibition of collagenase, following the procedure of Wittenuer <i>et al</i>.<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup> 10 L collagenase from <i>Clostridium histolyticum</i> (0.01 U/mL, Sigma), 60 L of 50 mM Tricine buffer pH 7.5 (containing 10 mM CaCl<sub>2 </sub>(Merck) and 400 mM NaCl (TCL Co., Ltd)), and 30 L of extract (7.81  250 g/mL) were mixed and incubated at 37C for 20 min. After incubation, 20 L of 1 mM N-[3-(2- Furyl)acryloyl]-leu-gly-Pro-Ala (FALGPA, Sigma) in Tricine buffer (Sigma) was added to the mixture. The collagenase inhibitory activities were measured at 335 nm by monitoring for 20 min after starting the reaction. The inhibition value was calculated according to <a href="#e1">Eq. (1)</a>. IC<sub>50</sub> values were determined from dose-effect curves.</p><p class="" id=d1820e377><a name=e1 class=n-a></a><div class="disp-formula panel"><math display=block id=math1 overflow=scroll><mrow><mo>Collagenase inhibition activity</mo><mo> </mo><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mo>%</mo><mo stretchy=false>)</mo><mo> </mo><mo>=</mo><mo> </mo><mfrac><mrow><msub><mo>A</mo><mrow><mo>negative control</mo></mrow></msub><mo></mo><msub><mo>A</mo><mrow><mo>extracts</mo></mrow></msub></mrow><mrow><msub><mo>A</mo><mrow><mo>negative control</mo><mo> </mo></mrow></msub></mrow></mfrac><mtext></mtext><mo></mo><mtext></mtext><mn>100</mn><mo>%</mo></mrow><mspace width=8em></mspace><mo stretchy=false>(</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy=false>)</mo></math></div></p></div><div class=section><a name=d1820e448 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Data analysis</h3><p class="" id=d1820e453>Statistical analysis using SPSS version 23 was carried out by normality test using numeric variable with Levene test and ANOVA and continued with post-hoc honestly significant difference, and Tukey test to the confidence level of 95% ( = 0.05). In previous research, Patil <i>et al</i>.<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup> found that the ethanol extract of <i>R. damascene</i> Mill L. contains total phenolic and flavonoid compounds at a higher level than acetone, aqueous, and chloroform extracts.</p><p class="" id=d1820e660>The antioxidant and anti-collagen activity of flowers depends on the compounds it contains and the solvent used. The solvent extract also has a contribution to increasing antioxidant and anti-collagenase activity.
