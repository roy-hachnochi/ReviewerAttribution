This study aimed to determine the susceptibility pattern to selected antibiotics and to index the first reported β-lactamases gene (extended spectrum β-lactamases (ESBLs) genes and class C β-lactamases genes) frequency in <i>Ps. aeruginosa </i>in Khartoum State, Sudan.<br/> <b>Methods:</b> 121 <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> clinical isolates from various clinical specimens were used in this cross-sectional study conducted in Khartoum State. A total of 80 isolates were confirmed as <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> through conventional identification methods and species-specific primers (the remaining 40 isolates were other bacterial species). The susceptibility pattern of the confirmed isolates to selected antibiotics was done following the Kirby Bauer disk diffusion method. Multiplex PCR was used for detection of seven β-lactamase genes (<i>bla<sub>TEM</sub>, bla<sub>SHV</sub>, bla<sub>CTXM-1</sub>, bla<sub>VEB</sub>, bla<sub>OXA-1</sub>, bla<sub>AmpC</sub> and bla<sub>DHA</sub></i>).<br/> <b>Results:</b> Of the 80 confirmed <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates, 8 (10%) were resistant to Imipenem while all isolates were resistant to Amoxicillin and Amoxyclav (100%). A total of 43 (54%) <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates were positive for ESBLs genes, while 27 (34%) were positive for class C β-lactamases, and 20 (25%) were positive for both classes. Frequency of ESBLs genes was as follows: bla<sub>TEM</sub>, 19 (44.2%); bla<sub>SHV</sub>, 16 (37.2%); bla<sub>CTX-M1</sub>, 10 (23.3%); bla<sub>VEB</sub>, 14 (32.6%); and bla<sub>OXA-1</sub>, 7 (16.3%). Occurrence of class C β-lactamases genes was bla<sub>AmpC</sub> 22 (81.5%) and bla<sub>DHA</sub> 8 (29.6%). In total, 3 (11.1%) isolates were positive for both bla<sub>AmpC </sub>and bla<sub>DHA </sub>genes.<br/> <b>Conclusion:</b> <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates showed a high rate of β-lactamases production, with co-resistance to other antibiotic classes. The lowest resistance rate of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> was to Imipenem followed by Gentamicin and Ciprofloxacin. Resistance to β-lactams occurs by different mechanisms, including bla<sub><i>AmpC</i></sub> overexpression due to genetic mutations, mutant gene acquisition, overproduction of efflux system, or low permeability<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. β-lactamases refer to enzymes that hydrolyze the amide bond of the β-lactam ring leading to drug inactivation and therapy failure<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. β-lactamases are classified molecularly into four groups: class A (extended spectrum β-lactamases (ESBLs)), class B (metallo-β-lactamases), class C (cephalosporinases), and class D (oxacillinases)<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d28126e394>ESBLs are enzymes that extend their hydrolyzing ability to hydrolyze broad spectrum cephalosporins<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup> and they also confer resistance to penicillins and narrow spectrum cephalosporins<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. ESBLs are inhibited by β-lactamase inhibitors, such as clavulanic acid<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. β-lactamases are transformed to ESBLs usually after point mutations in the β-lactamases gene. These mutations alter the substrate specificity because of changes in the amino acid sequences near the enzyme active site<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. ESBLs producing <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> have been reported worldwide in different countries<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a>–<a href="#ref-17">17</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d28126e424>AmpC β-lactamases are class C cephalosporinases that mediate bacterial resistance to cephalosporins and cephamycins. They also exhibit low rates of monobactam, cefepime and carbapenem hydrolysis<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup> and usually resist the inhibition by clavulanic acid<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. Normally, AmpC is a chromosomal β-lactamase gene that is regulated by ampR gene and expressed constantly. Point mutations of ampR gene in <i>Enterobacter cloacae</i> activate AmpC that mediate resistance to β-lactams<sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup>. In <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> over expressed AmpC β-lactamase mediate the resistance to broad spectrum cephalosporins<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>. AmpC β-lactamase in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> has also been reported in different countries around the world<sup><a href="#ref-20">20</a>–<a href="#ref-27">27</a></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d28126e460>The exact frequency of β-lactamase producing <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> in Khartoum State, Sudan is unknown; therefore, the aim of this study was to determine the susceptibility pattern to selected antibiotics and to determine the frequency of β-lactamases producing <i>Ps. areuginosa</i> isolates collected in Khartoum State hospitals.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d28126e472>Methods</h2><div class=section><a name=d28126e475 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Study design</h3><p class="" id=d28126e480>This is a cross-sectional study conducted between February 2017 and October 2017. Ethical approval for the study was obtained from the ethical committee of the College of Medical Laboratory Science, Sudan University of Science and Technology (SUST) (ethical meeting no, SUST/DSR/1EC/EA2/2017; data, 07<sup>th</sup> January 2017). Written informed consent from participants was waived by the same ethical committee as the study only used previously collected human bio-specimens with limited participant data.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d28126e487 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Collection and identification of bacterial strains</h3><p class="" id=d28126e492>A total of 121 clinical isolates, which initially identified as <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> and bacteria other than <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> was excluded. Selected isolates were obtained from Soba Teaching Hospital, Elribat University Hospital, National Laboratory for Public Health, Ear Nose Throat Hospital, and Military Hospital in Khartoum State. When there was a positive confirmation of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i>, the study supervisor went and collected the sample from the hospital. The samples were collected from patients suffering from urinary tract infections, respiratory tract infections, blood infections, and wound and ear infections. Data pertaining to the site of infection was collected from hospital records. The bacteria were preserved in 20% glycerol and peptone water and stored at -20°C</p><p class="" id=d28126e504>Phenotypic identity of the isolates was confirmed through conventional bacterial identification methods, such as Gram stain, oxidase test, and reactions in media containing sugars, such as Kligler Iron Agar, urease test and, citrate test. Pigment production was assessed using Muller Hinton agar, and then phenotypic identity confirmed by genotypic characterization using multiplex PCR, as previously described<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d28126e512 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Antimicrobial susceptibility testing</h3><p class="" id=d28126e517>Muller Hinton medium (HiMedia, India) was prepared and sterilized as instructed by the manufacturer. Antimicrobial susceptibility testing was performed following the modified Kirby-Bauer disc diffusion method<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup> and the results interpreted according to Clinical Laboratory Standards Institute guidelines (CLSI, 2007). The following antimicrobial discs (HiMedia, India) were used for sensitivity testing: Amoxicillin (25 µg), Cefotaxime (30 µg), Amoxicillin-Clavulanic acid (30 µg), Gentamicin (10 µg), Ciprofloxacin (5 µg), Chloramphenicol (30 µg) and Imipenem (10 µg). <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> ATCC 27853 was used as quality control strain to control the performance of the test and ensure that the test is properly performed.</p><p class="" id=d28126e527>Phenotypic identity of the isolates was confirmed through conventional bacterial identification methods such as Gram stain, oxidase test, reactions in media containing sugars such as Kligler Iron Agar, urease test and citrate test.</p></div><div class=section><a name=d28126e531 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Genotypic analysis of bacterial isolates</h3><p class="" id=d28126e536>Genomic DNA was extracted by simple boiling method<sup><a href="#ref-18">18</a></sup>. The extracted DNA was used as a template for amplification of target genes using multiplex PCR using TECHNE TC-312 (UK) thermocycler. Firstly, oprI and oprL primers (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>) (Macrogen, Korea) were used to confirm the identification of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i>. Seven primer pairs (<a href="#T1">Table 1</a>) (Macrogen, Korea) were used for detection of ESBLs genes (bla<sub>TEM</sub>, bla<sub>SHV</sub>, bla<sub>CTXM-</sub>1, bla<sub>VEB</sub>, bla<sub>OXA-1</sub>) and class C genes (bla<sub>AmpC</sub> and bla<sub>DHA</sub>). The oprI and oprL reaction was carried out with the following cycling conditions: denaturation at 95°C for 5 min; 33 cycles of denaturation at 95°C for 30 secs, annealing at 58°C for 30 sec and extension at 72°C for 30 secs, and final extension at 72°C for 5 min<sup><a href="#ref-28">28</a></sup>.</p><a name=T1 class=n-a></a><div class="table-wrap panel clearfix"><div class=caption><h3>Table 1. id=d28126e1717><i>Pseudomonas aeruginosa</i> is one of the main causative agents of serious nosocomial infections with increased reports of β-lactams resistant strains that makes treatment difficult and complicated<sup><sup><a href="#ref-19">19</a></sup></sup>. Production of β-lactamases is one of the most common mechanisms of β-lactam resistance<sup><sup><a href="#ref-36">36</a></sup></sup>.</p><p class="" id=d28126e1732>In this study the frequency of ESBL genes among <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates was 53.8%. This frequency is close to a report from India by Qureshi and Bhatnagar (2016)<sup><sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup></sup>, where the frequency of ESBLs in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates was 46%. However, the results of this study disagree with another report from India by Gupta <i>et al</i>. (2016)<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a></sup> where the frequency of ESBLs in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates was 22.9%. These discrepancies could be due to differences in strains of the clinical isolates, the antibiotics used or sample size.</p><p class="" id=d28126e1757>The most abundant gene among the ESBLs producing <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates detected in this study was <i>bla</i><sub>TEM</sub> gene (19, 44.2%), followed by <i>bla</i><sub>SHV</sub>, <i>bla</i><sub>VEB</sub>, <i>bla<sub>CTXM</sub></i><sub>-1</sub> and <i>bla</i><sub>OXA-1</sub> (16 (37.2%), 14 (32.6%), 10 (23.3%) and 7 (16.3%), respectively). Similar results were reported by Salah <i>et al</i>. (2016)<sup><a href="#ref-39">39</a></sup> in Egypt concerning the presence of <i>bla</i><sub>TEM</sub>, <i>bla</i><sub>SHV</sub> and <i>bla</i><sub>OXA-1</sub> (50%, 33% and 17%, respectively). The frequency of <i>bla</i><sub>TEM</sub> gene is also similar to that reported by Rafiee <i>et al</i>. (2014)<sup><a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup>, which was present in 39.2% of isolates. In a study in Iran by Sales <i>et al</i>. (2017)<sup><a href="#ref-41">41</a></sup>, similar results were reported concerning the presence of <i>bla</i><sub>CTXM-1</sub> (27.3%), while in India Jamali <i>et al</i>. (2017)<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup> reported a higher frequency (57.5%) of this gene.</p><p class="" id=d28126e1847>On the other hand, the frequency of <i>bla</i><sub>VEB</sub> and <i>bla</i><sub>SHV</sub> genes in this study differs from that reported in Iran by Bokaeian <i>et al</i>. (2014)<sup><a href="#ref-42">42</a></sup> where <i>bla</i><sub>VEB</sub> gene frequency was 13.3%, while the frequency of <i>bla</i><sub>SHV</sub> gene was 6.6%. The report by Jamali <i>et al</i>. (2017)<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup> concerning the genes <i>bla</i><sub>TEM</sub> (15%) and <i>bla</i><sub>SHV</sub> (75%) are also different from those reported in this study, and this could be due to the variation in strains of clinical isolates and the sample size used.</p><p class="" id=d28126e1897>In this study, the frequency of class C β-lactamase genes in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates was 27 (34%). This result is close to a report from India by Gupta <i>et al</i>. (2016)<sup><a href="#ref-38">38</a></sup> where 43% of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> were AmpC producers, and disagrees with a report from Thailand by Katvoravutthichai <i>et al</i>. (2016)<sup><a href="#ref-43">43</a></sup> where 11% of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates were AmpC producers. In this study, out of the class C β- lactamase producing <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates, 22 (81.5%) and 8 (29.6%) isolates were positive for <i>bla</i><sub>AmpC</sub> and <i>bla</i><sub>DHA</sub> genes, respectively. Qureshi and Bhatnagar (2016)<sup><a href="#ref-37">37</a></sup> in India reported that no <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates were positive for <i>bla</i><sub>AmpC</sub> gene, while Rafiee <i>et al</i>. (2014)<sup><a href="#ref-40">40</a></sup> in Iran reported that 60.8% of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> were positive for <i>bla</i><sub>AmpC</sub> gene.</p><p class="" id=d28126e1966>All <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> clinical isolates tested in our study were resistant to Amoxicillin and Amoxyclav and this may be due to the misuse of antibiotics in Sudan<sup><a href="#ref-44">44</a></sup>, where plenty of antimicrobial agents are sold over the counter. This rate of resistance is higher than the rate of resistance reported by Ahmad <i>et al</i>. (2016)<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup> in Pakistan where the resistance to Amoxicillin and Amoxyclav was 73.4% and 67.7% respectively, and this could be justified by the time difference between the studies, as well as the difference in the strains and antibiotics used.</p><p class="" id=d28126e1983>The resistance rate of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> to Imipenem was 10% (n=8). This may be due to the infrequent use of Imipenem antibiotics. This percentage agrees with a study reported in Pakistan by Ahmad <i>et al</i>. (2016)<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup> where 11.1% of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates were resistant to Imipenem, while in Sudan Altom and Ahmed (2015)<sup><a href="#ref-46">46</a></sup> reported that 5.7% <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates were resistant to Imipenem. This finding may indicate that carbapenem resistance is on the rise in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates from Sudan.</p><p class="" id=d28126e2010>In this study, the number of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates resistant to Cefotaxime, Chloramphenicol and Ceftazidime was 71 (88.8%), 71 (88.8%) and 46 (57.5%), respectively. The rate of resistance to Cefotaxime in this study is different from that reported in Pakistan by Ahmad <i>et al.</i> (2016)<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup> who found that 20.3% of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates were resistant to Ceftazidime. The resistance rate in this study also disagrees with that reported by Albadawi (2010)<sup><a href="#ref-47">47</a></sup> in Sudan who found that resistance of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> to Ceftazidime and Cefotaxime were 31% and 42%, respectively. These findings also indicate the rapidly increasing rates of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> resistance to antimicrobial agents in Sudan perhaps due to antibiotic misuse.</p><p class="" id=d28126e2037>In this study, 23 (28.8%) and 19 (23.8%) of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> clinical isolates were resistant to Ciprofloxacin and Gentamicin, respectively. Altom and Ahmed (2015)<sup><a href="#ref-46">46</a></sup> in Sudan also reported that 18.6% of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> were resistant to Gentamicin. Different results were reported by Ahmad <i>et al</i>. (2016)<sup><a href="#ref-45">45</a></sup>, where the percentage of resistance to Gentamicin and Ciprofloxacin were 74.3% and 44%, respectively. These percentages are much higher than those reported in this study probably due to their different geographical location, study time difference and the antibiotic-use rates.</p><p class="" id=d28126e2057>In this study, there was significant association between ESBLs production and site of infection (<i>P</i>=0.030). <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolated from blood showed the highest ESBLs production followed by ear swab, urine, sputum and wound swab. This result agrees with a study in India reported by Basak <i>et al</i>. (2012)<sup><a href="#ref-48">48</a></sup> where the highest ESBLs producing <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates were from blood, but disagrees with Azizi <i>et al</i>. (2015)<sup><a href="#ref-49">49</a></sup> in Iran who found that highest ESBLs production was in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolated from wound followed by urine, sputum and blood. There was insignificant association between the presence of class C β-lactamase genes and site of infection (<i>P</i>=0.215) found in the present study. The highest frequency of class C β<b>-</b>lactamases genes in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> were isolated from blood followed by urine, ear swab, wound swab and sputum. These results agree with the study in India reported by Basak <i>et al</i>. (2012)<sup><a href="#ref-48">48</a></sup> in that the highest percentage of class C β-lactamase genes in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> were found in blood.</p><p class="" id=d28126e2108>In the present study, there were four clinical isolates phenotypically sensitive to third generation cephalosporins (Ceftazidime and Cefotaxime) and genotypically positive for ESBLs genes. This result indicates that <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> may carry hidden unexpressed genes that could be detected through molecular techniques. This result agrees with a study in India reported by Bajpai <i>et al</i>. (2017)<sup><a href="#ref-50">50</a></sup> where out of 38 phenotypically ESBL-negative isolates, 20 isolates were positive for ESBLs genes.</p><p class="" id=d28126e2121>In this study, the frequency of pigment producing <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates was 67.5%. In a study in India a higher percentage was reported where 82.5% of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> were pigment producers (Finlayson and Brown, 2011)<sup><a href="#ref-51">51</a></sup>. The present study revealed that there was no relationship between pigment production and pattern of antimicrobial resistance, except in Chloramphenicol (<i>P</i>=0.02) and Cefotaxime (<i>P</i>=0.00).</p><p class="" id=d28126e2140>In this study, the relationship between pigment production and site of infection was significant (<i>P</i>=0.000). <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolated from wound infections were the highest pigment producing isolates followed by isolates from blood, urine and sputum. There was no pigment production in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolated from ear infections in this study.</p><p class="" id=d28126e2152>The variations between the results of this study and other reports could be attributed to the difference in antibiotic usage patterns in each region, economical causes, geographical differences, sample size, differences in time in which the studies were performed, and study population. Despite the significance of the present study, there were limitations that should be avoided in future studies, such as the small sample size, phenotypic detection of β-lactamases, coverage of other β-lactamase classes, and gene sequencing should be done in order to confirm and to identify all the genes that are carried by <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> strains in Sudan.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d28126e2161>Conclusion</h2><p class="" id=d28126e2164>This is study is of great importance as it raises attention to the existing problem of resistance to β-lactams in <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> in Sudan. This study confirms the reports that a number of antibiotics are becoming useless for treating this problematic bacterium, since all the strains of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates in this study were resistant to Amoxicillin and Amoxyclav. The best antibiotic sensitivity results obtained in this study were those of Imipenem, followed by Gentamicin and Ciprofloxacin. Moreover, <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> isolates showed an increased rate of β-lactamase production with co-resistance with other classes of antibiotics. Of interest is the finding that clinical isolates were resistant phenotypically in high frequencies to Amoxicillin, Amoxyclav and a third generation antibiotic, Cephalosporin, and showed negative results genotypically, indicating that resistance to this family of antibiotics also exist by resistance mechanisms other than β-lactamases production. Also, our PCR results revealed that <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> possesses hidden β-lactamases genes that can’t be detected phenotypically. Finally, this study highlighted for the first time the problem of misidentification of <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> and other microorganisms in Khartoum hospitals as only 80 out of the 120 alleged isolates were confirmed to be <i>Ps. aeruginosa</i> through PCR.</p></div><div id=article1-body class=generated-article-body><h2 class=main-title id=d28126e2190>Data availability</h2><div class=section><a name=d28126e2193 class=n-a></a><h3 class=section-title>Underlying data</h3><p class="" id=d28126e2198>Figshare: SPSS, <a target=xrefwindow href="https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.12453287.v2" id=d28126e2200>https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.12453287.v2</a><sup><a href="#ref-51">51</a></sup></p><p class="" id=d28126e2206>This project contains the following underlying data: <div class=list><a name=d28126e2208 class=n-a></a><ul><li><p id=d28126e2212>SPSS. sav (Result sheet for beta-lactamases detection and sensitivity testing)</p></li><li><p id=d28126e2218>Data Dictionary. docx</p></li></ul></div></p><p class="" id=d28126e2223>Data are available under the terms of the <a target=xrefwindow href="https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0/legalcode" id=d28126e2225>Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International license</a> (CC-BY 4.0).</p></div></div><div id=article1-back class=generated-article-footer><div class=back-section><a name=d28126e2234 class=n-a></a><h2 class=main-title id=d28126e2236>Acknowledgements</h2><p class="" id=d28126e2239>Deep thanks to all the Microbiology Department team at Sudan University of Science and Technology for their significant help during the study.
